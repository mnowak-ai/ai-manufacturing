Lesson 1.2 — Safety & AI

A Real Safety Incident Walk-Through: What Happens When the AI Alert Goes Off

AI detects the hazard. Then what? A step-by-step walkthrough of a realistic safety scenario — and every human decision that still has to happen after the alert fires.

Everything you've read in this lesson so far has described AI safety tools in the abstract: what they detect, how they work, where they fail. This article is different.

This is a single scenario — a realistic safety incident at a food manufacturing facility — told moment by moment. You'll see exactly what the AI detects, what information goes to whom, and every human decision that follows.

Because here's the thing that's easy to forget when you're reading about AI safety statistics: the alert is the beginning, not the end. What happens next is entirely human.

The Setting

The Facility
Hartwell Food Manufacturing — Packaging Line 4

A mid-sized food packaging facility in the Midwest. Line 4 processes and packages dry goods — cereal, granola, trail mix — at high speed. The line runs three shifts. This scenario takes place at 11:40pm, 20 minutes into the start of the night shift.

The facility has deployed an AI vision system — Intenseye — across the production floor and warehouse. Cameras cover all major work areas. The system is configured to detect PPE violations, restricted zone entries, ergonomic risks, and forklift-pedestrian conflicts.

People in this scenario:

• Darius — Line 4 operator, 4 years at Hartwell, experienced and efficient
• Supervisor Kim — Night shift floor supervisor for Lines 3–5, responsible for 22 workers
• Marcus — Maintenance technician on-call for nights
• The AI system — monitoring all cameras, configured with Hartwell's safety zones

The Incident Unfolds

11:41 PM — The hazard develops
The Situation
Darius reaches into the conveyor housing

Line 4's packaging machine is jamming intermittently — a granola cluster is catching on a guide rail inside the conveyor housing. The machine isn't shutting down fully; it's running slowly with a minor jam.

Darius has dealt with this before. The fix takes 30 seconds: reach in, clear the cluster, the line resumes. He's done it dozens of times. On day shift, there are always other workers nearby. At 11:41pm on night shift, the area around Line 4 is quiet.

He doesn't initiate lockout/tagout — the full machine shutdown and energy control procedure required before reaching into moving equipment. He reasons it'll just take a second. He reaches in without going through the procedure.

The conveyor doesn't fully stop. His sleeve catches on the partially moving belt.

11:41:08 PM — AI detects the event
The AI System
Camera 14 flags an unsafe condition

The Intenseye system, monitoring Camera 14 which covers Line 4's conveyor area, detects a worker with an arm inside the conveyor housing while the machine registers as partially operational. The system identifies this as a caught-in/between risk — one of its highest-severity alert categories.

🚨 High Severity Alert
Camera 14 — Line 4 Conveyor
Worker detected with limb inside conveyor housing while machine is in operational state. Possible caught-in/between hazard. Machine status: partially running (slow-jam mode).

Recommended immediate action: Supervisor response to Line 4 area. Verify worker safety. Initiate emergency stop if not already done.
📍 Line 4 — Station 3
🕐 11:41:08 PM
⚠️ Severity: HIGH
📹 Clip saved

The alert fires to Supervisor Kim's tablet and to the main safety control room screen simultaneously. A camera clip of the 8 seconds preceding the alert is automatically saved to the incident log.

11:41:12 PM — The alert reaches Kim
Supervisor Kim
Four seconds after detection: a decision

Kim is at the Line 3 area when her tablet vibrates. She sees the alert: high severity, Camera 14, Line 4 conveyor. Caught-in/between risk.

In her first three seconds, she makes several rapid judgments:

Decision Point 1
Kim gets the alert. What does she do first?
Check the camera clip to see if the situation has already resolved before heading over
Head directly to Line 4 immediately — with a caught-in/between alert, seconds matter more than review
Radio Darius first to ask what's happening on Line 4
Mark the alert as reviewed and continue her Line 3 walkthrough — Darius is experienced and probably handled it
✅ Right call. High-severity caught-in/between alerts mean a person may be in contact with moving equipment right now. A four-second review of a clip is a four-second delay when someone might be getting hurt. Supervisors are trained to respond physically first for high-severity alerts and assess remotely only for lower-severity ones.
❌ Not the best call here. For a high-severity alert involving possible contact with moving equipment, immediate physical response is the protocol. Camera reviews, radio checks, and assumptions about worker competence all introduce delay when the risk is active. The AI flagged something happening right now — respond to now.
11:41:28 PM — Kim arrives at Line 4
Supervisor Kim + Darius
16 seconds after the alert: eyes on the situation

Kim arrives at Line 4's Station 3. She finds Darius standing back from the conveyor, his sleeve pulled and slightly torn, his forearm red but not lacerated. The conveyor has stopped — Darius managed to pull free and hit the emergency stop before any serious injury occurred.

He's shaken but okay. The situation could have been significantly worse.

Now Kim faces her second major decision:

Decision Point 2
Darius is okay. The line is stopped. What does Kim do?
Confirm Darius is okay, restart the line, and document the incident in tomorrow's shift report
Keep the line down, call Marcus for maintenance inspection, file an incident report immediately, and conduct a brief conversation with Darius about lockout/tagout procedure — tonight, before the line restarts
Send Darius home and restart the line with a different operator
Restart the line after clearing the jam — Darius didn't get seriously hurt so the incident doesn't require formal documentation
✅ Correct on all counts. The line stays down until Marcus confirms the conveyor is safe. OSHA requires near-miss incidents to be documented — and near-misses are among the most valuable safety data points a facility has. The conversation with Darius happens now, respectfully and focused on the procedure (not blame), because the lesson is freshest and the risk is clearest in this moment.
❌ Not the right protocol. A near-miss involving a caught-in/between event requires: (1) the machine to be inspected before restart, (2) immediate incident documentation — OSHA requires near-miss reporting and it's one of the most valuable safety data points a facility has, and (3) a procedure conversation with Darius now, while the event is fresh. Deferring any of these increases future risk significantly.
11:43 PM — Marcus is called
Maintenance — Marcus
The machine gets inspected before anything restarts

Marcus arrives six minutes later. He performs a proper lockout/tagout of Line 4's conveyor — verifying that all energy sources are de-energized and locked before reaching in to inspect the guide rail and belt condition.

He finds that the guide rail that caused the initial jam has a metal burr — a sharp edge that's been catching material. Left unaddressed, it would have kept causing jams, and Darius (or whoever worked the line) would have kept reaching in to clear them.

Marcus files the maintenance ticket, grinds the burr smooth, and clears the line for restart after inspection.

The jam is fixed. The root cause — the thing that created the hazardous situation in the first place — is eliminated. Not just managed. Eliminated.

11:58 PM — Incident documented
Supervisor Kim + The System
The paper trail that protects everyone

Kim files the incident report while Line 4 restarts. The report includes: time, location, worker involved, description of event, AI alert details and timestamp, camera clip reference number, maintenance action taken, and conversation notes from the discussion with Darius.

The AI system has already automatically logged the alert, camera clip, and machine-state data at the time of the event. Kim's report connects that automated record to the human context: what actually happened, what was done, and what the root cause was.

This report goes to the safety manager in the morning. The camera clip, with the guide rail burr identified as the root cause, will be used in a brief safety training point shared with all Line 4 operators at the next shift briefing.

✅ Outcome
A near-miss became a safety improvement, not a statistic
Darius went home with a bruised forearm and a shaken feeling — but not a serious injury. The root cause was found and fixed. The incident is documented and will inform training. The next operator to work Line 4 won't face the same jam — because the burr that caused it is gone.

The AI alert fired at 11:41:08. By 11:41:28, a supervisor was on scene. The 20-second response window is what made the difference between "near-miss" and something much worse.

What This Scenario Teaches

Walk back through the timeline. At every moment after the AI alert fired, a human was making a decision. The AI provided the trigger — the human chain determined the outcome.

1
The AI is the first warning, not the final word
Intenseye detected the hazard in seconds and got the right information to the right person. But every action that followed — the response speed, the decision to keep the line down, the maintenance call, the documentation — was human judgment. The AI opened the window; humans had to climb through it.
2
Response speed matters as much as detection
The AI detected the event at 11:41:08. Kim arrived at 11:41:28. Twenty seconds. That response speed is only possible because Kim treated the high-severity alert as an immediate physical response, not a paperwork event. The alert is worthless if it sits on a tablet for five minutes.
3
The root cause was a mechanical problem, not a person problem
Darius skipped lockout/tagout — that's a real problem. But he skipped it because a jammed machine kept creating a situation where someone would need to reach in. Marcus found and fixed the burr. Effective safety doesn't just address the behavior; it addresses why the behavior kept happening.
4
Near-miss documentation is safety's most underused tool
This incident was documented fully — not filed away and forgotten. The camera clip, the maintenance ticket, and the training point all came from treating this near-miss as the critical data point it was. Facilities that investigate near-misses seriously have dramatically fewer serious accidents. The AI makes this documentation automatic; humans have to act on it.
5
The 20-second response gap is also a human gap
The AI fired instantly. Kim responded in 20 seconds. During that window, Darius pulled free on his own. The AI cannot physically intervene. That's not a criticism — it's a design reality. Human responders are the intervention layer. Their speed, judgment, and presence are what the AI enables but cannot replace.

AI safety tools extend what humans can perceive. They don't extend what humans can do. The response — every decision, every action, every conversation — still belongs to the people on the floor.

🧠 Check Your Understanding

Both were necessary. The AI without Kim's judgment, or Kim without the AI's detection speed, would have produced a worse outcome.

That combination of AI detection speed and human response judgment is what made the difference.

What You Learned

✓
When an AI safety alert fires, a chain of human decisions immediately follows — response speed, on-scene assessment, whether to keep equipment down, maintenance calls, documentation, and follow-up training.
✓
The AI is the first warning, not the final word. Detection is only valuable if it triggers a fast, appropriate human response.
✓
Near-miss incidents should be fully documented and investigated — the AI makes documentation automatic, but humans have to act on the findings to prevent recurrence.
✓
Effective safety addresses root causes, not just behaviors. In this scenario, the guide rail burr was the root cause — fixing it eliminated the hazardous situation entirely, not just this one instance of it.
✓
AI cannot physically intervene — it can only detect and alert. Human responders are the intervention layer, and their speed and judgment determine the actual outcome of every safety event.
