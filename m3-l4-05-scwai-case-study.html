What FDA 21 CFR Part 11 Requires

Moving from paper to electronic records in a pharmaceutical facility isn't as simple as scanning forms and storing them as PDFs. The FDA's 21 CFR Part 11 regulation sets specific requirements for electronic records and electronic signatures — requirements designed to ensure that digital records are at least as trustworthy and secure as paper ones.

📋 21 CFR Part 11 — Key Requirements for Electronic Records

These are the rules any electronic documentation system in a pharmaceutical facility must meet before it can legally replace paper records.

Audit Trail

All electronic records must have a computer-generated, secure audit trail that captures who created or modified an entry, when, and what changed. Original entries must be preserved — changes cannot overwrite.

Electronic Signatures

Electronic signatures must be: unique to each individual, linked only to one person, and executed during a logged-in session tied to that person's credentials. Printed name + date alone is not sufficient.

Access Controls

System access must be limited to authorized individuals. Each user must have a unique username and password. Authority levels must restrict what each person can create, modify, view, or approve.

System Validation

The software system itself must be validated — tested and documented to prove it consistently performs as intended. Validation documentation must be maintained and updated when software changes.

Record Protection

Electronic records must be protected against loss, alteration, and unauthorized access. Backup systems, encryption, and access logging are required.

Readability Over Time

Records must be retrievable and readable throughout their required retention period — which can be 5–30 years depending on the product type. Software migrations must preserve the original records.

Meeting these requirements is why pharmaceutical manufacturers can't just use standard business software (like Google Docs or Excel) for regulated records. The system itself has to be designed and validated for 21 CFR Part 11 compliance. This is exactly the regulatory framework SCW.AI was built to satisfy.

How the SCW.AI System Works

SCW.AI's approach places AI-powered documentation at the point of production — workers interact with tablets or terminals at their workstations rather than paper forms, and the AI assists the documentation process in real time.

Step-by-step flow: A worker starts a production task and opens a digital form on their workstation terminal. As they work through each step, the AI system auto-populates fields it can fill from connected sensors and equipment — current temperature, batch weight from the scale, equipment ID from RFID scan. The worker confirms, enters anything that requires human input, and applies their electronic signature to each critical step.

If a required field is left blank, the system won't let the worker proceed. If a value is entered outside the acceptable range, the system immediately flags it and requires acknowledgment — preventing anyone from recording an out-of-spec value without a documented decision to proceed. If the AI detects that a step was completed in an unusually short time (suggesting it may have been skipped), it flags the entry for supervisor review.

The AI isn't replacing the worker's judgment — it's making the documentation of that judgment automatic, complete, and real-time. The person still performs the work and confirms each step. The system ensures they document it correctly as they go.

What to Remember

2,000 paper logbooks in a pharmaceutical facility represented a daily burden of documentation management, compliance risk from illegible entries, and the inability to quickly retrieve records during audits. This was normal before digital systems — and still is in many facilities today.

21 CFR Part 11 sets specific requirements for electronic records in pharmaceutical manufacturing: secure audit trails, electronic signatures tied to unique individuals, access controls, system validation, and long-term record protection. Standard business software doesn't meet these requirements — regulated electronic systems must be purpose-built and validated.

SCW.AI's approach places documentation at the point of production — AI auto-fills what connected sensors can provide, prevents incomplete entries, and generates a complete audit trail automatically. The key safeguard against transcription errors is equipment integration: the actual reading, not a worker's transcription of it.
