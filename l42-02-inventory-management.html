What Is Inventory Management and Why Does It Affect Everything?

Inventory is the foundation of everything that happens on a production floor. Too little and machines stop. Too much and money is trapped in a warehouse. This article explains what inventory actually is, the four types every manufacturer tracks, and why AI resource management starts here.

Inventory Is Not Just "Stuff in a Warehouse"

When most people hear "inventory," they picture shelves full of products waiting to be sold — like a store's stockroom. Manufacturing inventory is different. It's not just the finished product sitting in a warehouse. It's every material, component, and partially-finished item at every stage of the production process.

A manufacturing facility might have steel sheets arriving at the loading dock, partially machined brackets waiting for their next step, finished housings staged for quality inspection, and boxed products ready to ship — all at the same moment, all in different places, all requiring tracking. That entire collection — everything flowing through the facility — is inventory.

Inventory is money in physical form. Too little stops production. Too much ties up cash that could be used for equipment, workers, or growth. Getting it right is one of the hardest and most important challenges in manufacturing.

The Four Types of Manufacturing Inventory

Manufacturing inventory exists in four distinct forms, each at a different stage of the production journey. AI resource management tools track all four simultaneously.

Type 1
Raw Materials Inventory
Raw materials are the inputs that production transforms into products. They arrive from suppliers, are stored in the warehouse, and are pulled to the production floor as jobs begin. This is the most actively managed inventory type — planners need to know exactly how much is on hand, how fast it's being consumed, and when the next shipment arrives.
Examples: Steel sheets, aluminum rod stock, plastic resin pellets, copper wire, timber boards, raw fabric. Anything that arrives from a supplier and hasn't been worked on yet.
Type 2
Work-in-Process (WIP) Inventory
Work-in-process — often just called "WIP" — is everything that has been started but not yet finished. A bracket that's been cut and pressed but not yet welded is WIP. A housing that's been machined and is waiting for inspection is WIP. WIP inventory sits between machines or between shifts, waiting for its next step. Too much WIP suggests bottlenecks — places in the process where work piles up faster than it flows through.
Examples: Partially machined parts awaiting the next step, welded assemblies awaiting coating, coated parts awaiting inspection. Anything mid-process.
Type 3
Finished Goods Inventory
Finished goods are completed products that have passed all inspections and are ready to ship to customers. Maintaining some finished goods inventory provides a buffer — if a customer needs something quickly, you can ship from stock rather than making them wait for a new production run. But too much finished goods inventory means the company has spent money making products that customers haven't ordered yet, with no guarantee they'll sell before they become obsolete or damaged.
Examples: Boxed and labeled brackets awaiting pickup, palletized housing assemblies in the shipping area, inspected and packaged components staged for delivery.
Type 4
MRO Inventory (Maintenance, Repair & Operations)
MRO inventory is everything the facility uses to keep itself running that doesn't go into the product directly — spare machine parts, cutting tools, lubricants, safety equipment, cleaning supplies, and packaging materials. MRO often gets overlooked in inventory management discussions, but running out of a critical spare part can shut down a machine just as effectively as running out of raw material. And MRO items can be surprisingly expensive — a single high-precision bearing might cost $400.
Examples: Replacement bearings, cutting inserts for CNC machines, hydraulic fluid, welding wire, safety gloves, machine calibration tools.

The Two Inventory Failure Modes: Stockout and Overstock

Every inventory management decision is a balancing act between two costly extremes. Lean too far in one direction and production stops. Lean too far in the other and money is wasted. AI inventory management tools are fundamentally trying to keep every material in the sweet spot between these two failures.

What a Stockout Looks and Feels Like

A stockout is when a material reaches zero — or falls so low that production must halt. The word sounds technical. The reality is stressful and expensive. Here's what actually happens in a facility when a key material stocks out:

At 8:20 AM, a machine operator walks to the material staging area to begin their next job. The bin for 4mm stainless steel sheet is empty. They look around — nothing nearby. They tell the foreman. The foreman checks with the warehouse. The warehouse team checks the system: according to the ERP, there should be 18 square feet in Bay 7. They check Bay 7. Nothing. Turns out, 18 square feet was used three days ago by a different shift, and the system wasn't updated when the material was pulled. The actual count is zero.

Now the machine sits idle. The operator can't start the job. The foreman is trying to figure out what this worker should do instead (and whether that alternative job's materials are actually available). The shift supervisor is calling the purchasing team. Purchasing is calling the supplier: earliest delivery — Thursday. Today is Tuesday. Every job that depends on that material — not just this one — is now at risk.

This is a stockout. It happens when inventory management fails — when the reorder didn't happen on time, when the system count was wrong, or when usage spiked unexpectedly and nobody saw it coming.

What Overstock Actually Costs

Overstock is a less dramatic problem than a stockout — nothing stops, no alarm sounds — but it's expensive in a quieter way. Here's where the cost of carrying too much inventory actually shows up:

💰
Capital Tied Up
Every dollar spent on materials that sit in the warehouse is a dollar not available for new equipment, workforce training, or growth opportunities. Excess inventory is essentially an interest-free loan the company gave to its warehouse shelves.
🏭
Warehouse Space
Storage space costs money — rent, utilities, climate control, security. Excess inventory consumes that space, sometimes crowding out materials that are actually needed soon and making it harder to find and pull the right stock efficiently.
📉
Obsolescence & Spoilage
Some materials degrade over time — chemicals lose potency, adhesives cure, plastics become brittle. Others become obsolete when product designs change. Material that sat in the warehouse for 18 months may be unusable by the time someone tries to run it.
📋
Insurance & Carrying Costs
Inventory must be insured against loss, fire, or damage. The industry rule of thumb: the total annual cost of carrying inventory is typically 20–30% of the inventory's value — meaning $100,000 of excess inventory costs $20,000–$30,000 per year to hold.

Check Your Understanding

Quick Check
A factory uses 200 units of a specific component per day. The supplier's lead time — the time between placing an order and receiving the delivery — is 5 days. What is the minimum inventory level at which the factory should place a reorder to avoid running out?
✅ Correct. This is the fundamental reorder point calculation. If the factory uses 200 units per day and it takes 5 days to get a delivery, the factory will consume 200 × 5 = 1,000 units during the delivery window. If the inventory level falls below 1,000 units before the order is placed, the factory will run out before the new stock arrives. This reorder point should actually be set slightly higher than 1,000 to include a safety stock buffer — extra units held in reserve in case usage spikes or the supplier delivers late. AI inventory systems calculate and monitor these reorder points automatically.
❌ The key calculation: daily usage (200 units) times lead time in days (5 days) = 1,000 units. That 1,000 units is the reorder point — the inventory level at which the factory must place an order, because that\'s how much stock will be consumed while waiting for the delivery. Ordering when stock hits 200 units means the factory will run out in 1 day, but wait 5 days for delivery — a 4-day stockout. Ordering when stock hits 1,000 means the delivery arrives just as the last unit is used. In practice, a safety stock buffer of 10–20% is added on top to account for unexpected usage spikes or supplier delays.

What You Learned

✓
Manufacturing inventory comes in four types: raw materials (inputs not yet worked on), work-in-process (partially completed items), finished goods (completed and ready to ship), and MRO (supplies for running the facility itself).
✓
A stockout — running out of a material — stops production, creates idle workers and machines, triggers expensive emergency orders, and risks customer deadlines. It is one of the most disruptive events on a production floor.
✓
Overstock — having too much inventory — ties up cash, consumes warehouse space, risks spoilage or obsolescence, and adds 20–30% carrying cost per year on the value of the excess stock.
✓
The reorder point is the inventory level that triggers a purchase order — calculated as daily usage multiplied by supplier lead time in days. AI inventory management tools monitor stock levels against reorder points continuously and alert teams before shortages hit.
✓
Inventory management is the foundation of AI resource management — every AI prediction about material shortages, reallocation suggestions, and usage rate calculations builds on understanding what inventory is and how it flows through the facility.
