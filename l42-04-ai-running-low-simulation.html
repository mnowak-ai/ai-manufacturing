The Sensor Error Lesson

The third alert in this simulation — the overnight bolt consumption spike — was a sensor error. This is one of the most important failure modes in AI-assisted resource management, and it's worth understanding deeply.

AI inventory systems can only report what their sensors and data inputs tell them. If a sensor malfunctions, produces an erroneous reading, or gets physically disturbed (someone knocked the scale, the RFID reader lost connection), the AI will report the bad data as if it's real. It has no way to know the difference between a real consumption event and a sensor glitch.

The signal that something was wrong in this scenario was context that the AI didn't have: the production floor was idle overnight. No production = no consumption. A 340% bolt usage spike during idle hours is physically impossible under normal circumstances. A human reviewing the alert with that context immediately recognizes the anomaly.

AI alerts are starting points for investigation — not automatic commands to action. The most important skill in working with AI resource tools is knowing when an alert makes sense given the full context, and when it doesn't.

In practice, sensor errors are more common than people realize. Scales drift, RFID readers lose sync, barcode scans get duplicated or skipped. A good materials manager reviews alerts with two questions: does this number make sense given what I know about the floor? And what's the simplest explanation for this reading?

When the simplest explanation is "sensor error" — because the physics don't support the reading — the correct action is to investigate the sensor before spending money on an emergency order that isn't needed.

What This Simulation Teaches

✓
Critical alerts (projected stockout within hours) require immediate escalation — not standard reorder processes. An expedited order to a local supplier, a production rescheduling decision, and direct customer communication may all need to happen simultaneously.
✓
Warning alerts (days away from reorder point) allow time for a standard response — but acting promptly while options are still available is much better than waiting until the alert becomes critical.
✓
Sensor anomalies must be investigated before being acted on. Context is everything — a 340% consumption spike during idle hours is a sensor problem, not a real shortage. Placing an emergency order based on bad data wastes money and erodes trust in the AI system.
✓
AI alerts are not orders — they're information that requires human judgment. The right response to each alert depends on urgency, cost, production impact, and real-world context that the sensor data alone can't provide.
✓
The cost of emergency sourcing (2–5x standard pricing, expedite fees) is almost always higher than the cost of maintaining good reorder discipline. Stockouts happen when reorder points are ignored or when unexpected spikes aren't anticipated — both of which human oversight of AI alerts helps prevent.
