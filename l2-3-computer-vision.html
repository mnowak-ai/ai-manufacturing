Lesson 1.2 — Safety & AI

How AI Cameras See Danger: A Plain-Language Explanation of Computer Vision on the Shop Floor

The video shows AI detecting missing PPE and hazard zone violations. This article explains exactly how that works — and where it doesn't.

When you watch a camera feed from a factory floor, you see it instantly: the worker by the stamping press, the forklift moving down the aisle, the hard hat on the supervisor's head. Your brain processes all of that in milliseconds, effortlessly.

A camera without AI sees none of it. It just sees millions of colored dots — pixels — arranged in a rectangle. It has no idea what any of them mean.

Computer vision is the technology that teaches a camera to understand what it's looking at. To turn a grid of pixels into meaning: that's a person, that's a hard hat, that person is not wearing a hard hat, that person just stepped into a restricted zone.

Understanding how this works — and where it fails — is essential for anyone working alongside these systems on the floor.

What a Camera Actually Sees

Key Term
Computer vision: A field of AI that trains computers to interpret and understand visual information — photographs, video frames, live camera feeds. A computer vision system analyzes images pixel by pixel and applies patterns learned during training to identify objects, people, actions, and relationships between them.

Here's the fundamental challenge: a camera captures an image as a grid of tiny colored squares called pixels. A high-definition camera might capture 2 million of them per frame — and it captures 30 frames per second. That's 60 million pixel readings per second, streaming continuously.

None of those pixels come labeled. The camera doesn't know a pixel is part of a hard hat versus part of a wall versus part of a human face. All of that meaning has to be learned — by training an AI model to recognize patterns that reliably indicate specific objects.

The camera only captures RGB color values. Every pixel is just three numbers — red, green, blue intensity. No meaning, no labels, no context.

The AI's job is to learn the pixel patterns that correspond to hard hats, safety glasses, restricted zones, and dangerous postures — across thousands of different lighting conditions, angles, workers, and environments. That learning process is called training.

How AI Learns to See: The Training Process

Computer vision models don't come pre-programmed to recognize hard hats. They learn by example — by being shown thousands of images and being told what's in them. Here's how that works:

1
Collect Training Images
Engineers gather thousands of photos and video frames showing the objects the AI needs to learn: workers with hard hats, workers without hard hats, safety glasses on faces, safety glasses on heads incorrectly, restricted zone boundaries with and without workers inside them.
A well-trained safety model might use 50,000–200,000 labeled images — captured in different factories, different lighting conditions, different worker body types and clothing, different camera angles.
2
Label Every Image by Hand
Human annotators go through each image and mark exactly where each object is — drawing boxes around each hard hat, each person's head, each safety vest. This labeling is what teaches the AI what it's looking at. The labels are the answers the AI is trying to learn to predict.
This step is painstaking and expensive — but it's why high-quality training data is so valuable. The AI is only as accurate as its training images are correctly labeled.
3
Train the Model to Find Patterns
The AI model is shown each image repeatedly, asked to predict what's in it, told whether it was right or wrong, and adjusted accordingly — millions of times. Over time it learns the pixel patterns that reliably indicate "hard hat" versus "no hard hat," separated from all the visual noise in the image.
Modern training can run for days on specialized hardware, processing billions of pixel values to fine-tune the model's accuracy.
4
Test on New Images It's Never Seen
Before deployment, the model is tested on a separate set of images it wasn't trained on. If it performs well on new images, it has genuinely learned the concept — not just memorized the training data. If accuracy is poor, more training data or model adjustments are needed.
A typical safety model might need to reach 90–95% accuracy on test data before it's considered ready for deployment — because false alarms erode trust and false misses allow real hazards through.
5
Deploy to Live Cameras and Keep Improving
The trained model runs on live camera feeds, making predictions in real time. As it encounters new situations in a specific factory — unusual lighting, new equipment, different worker uniforms — it can be refined with additional training data from that environment.
This is why AI safety systems often perform better over time at a given facility — they accumulate more relevant training data from that specific environment.

What Computer Vision Can Detect on the Shop Floor

Modern AI camera systems can be trained to detect a wide range of safety-relevant situations. Each requires its own model or combination of models working together:

⛑️
PPE Presence and Absence
Hard hats, safety glasses, gloves, high-vis vests, face shields — for each hazard zone's requirements.
How: Detects the distinctive shape, color, and position of each PPE item relative to the person's body. A hard hat has a specific silhouette on top of a head that is distinct from uncovered hair.
🚧
Hazard Zone Violations
Workers entering restricted areas around dangerous equipment — stamping presses, robotic arms, high-voltage zones.
How: The system maps virtual boundaries onto the camera feed. When a person's body crosses that boundary, it triggers an alert — regardless of what the person is doing.
🚜
Vehicle and Pedestrian Proximity
Forklifts or other vehicles getting dangerously close to workers on foot — one of manufacturing's most common fatal hazard categories.
How: The AI tracks both the vehicle and nearby pedestrians simultaneously, calculating proximity in real time and alerting when the gap drops below a safe threshold.
🧍
Unsafe Body Posture
Workers bending, reaching, or lifting in ways that significantly increase the risk of musculoskeletal injury over time.
How: Skeleton tracking — the AI identifies key body joints (shoulders, hips, knees) and calculates angles. Dangerous postures produce measurable angle patterns the model can recognize.
🧯
Blocked Emergency Exits and Equipment
Objects, pallets, or equipment blocking fire exits, emergency stops, or fire extinguisher access points.
How: The camera learns what these specific locations look like when clear. When objects appear in the frame at those locations, it flags the obstruction.
😴
Fatigue and Distraction Indicators
Head drooping, eyes closing, prolonged phone use — behaviors that correlate with increased accident risk during demanding shifts.
How: Facial landmark tracking monitors eye openness and head position over time. Patterns that deviate from alert behavior trigger a review flag.

Where Computer Vision Gets It Wrong

No AI system is perfect. Computer vision makes mistakes — and understanding the types of mistakes helps you work with these systems more effectively. There are two types of errors that matter most:

🚨
False Positive — The AI alerts when there's no real problem
The system flags something as a violation when actually everything is fine. A worker in a brightly colored jacket gets flagged as a "high-vis vest not worn." A welding mask catches the light at an angle that makes the AI think no face shield is present.
Why it matters: Too many false positives erode trust. If supervisors get 50 alerts and 45 are wrong, they start ignoring them — which defeats the entire purpose of the system. Managing false positive rates is critical to keeping AI safety tools effective.
👁️
False Negative — The AI misses a real problem
A genuine safety violation occurs and the AI doesn't catch it. A worker without safety glasses in a restricted zone passes through a camera's blind spot. Poor lighting makes the hard hat detection unreliable for that shift.
Why it matters: False negatives are the more dangerous error — they create false security. A system that supervisors trust but that misses real violations is worse than no system at all, because it stops humans from watching closely themselves.
🌦️
Environmental Factors That Reduce Accuracy
Accuracy drops when conditions deviate from what the model was trained on: unusual lighting, steam or smoke obscuring the view, new types of equipment blocking camera angles, workers in non-standard gear or uniforms.
What it means for you: AI safety systems work best in well-designed environments with good lighting and thoughtfully placed cameras. If conditions change — a temporary barrier, different shift lighting — the system's accuracy may change too. A human eye is still needed to catch what the camera misses.

The goal of computer vision in safety isn't to replace human judgment. It's to watch more things, more consistently, than any human team could — and flag what needs a closer look.
