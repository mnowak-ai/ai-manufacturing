What Happens During an Audit?

Understanding factory inspections from the inside — who shows up, what they look for, what they ask for, what they find, and how AI-generated records change the outcome. Essential context for anyone working in a regulated manufacturing environment.

What Is an Audit?

An audit is a formal examination of whether a facility is operating in compliance with applicable rules — laws, regulations, standards, and its own documented procedures. Audits can be conducted by government inspectors, by customers, or by the facility's own quality team. The result is an official record of what was found, including any gaps between what the rules require and what's actually happening.

For people who've never been through one, audits can feel intimidating. But understanding what auditors are actually looking for — and how AI-generated compliance records make the process smoother — removes much of the mystery.

Key Term
Audit: A systematic, independent examination of a facility's operations, records, and processes to determine whether they comply with applicable requirements. Unlike an inspection (which may be a quick walkthrough), audits typically involve a thorough review of documentation, observation of processes in action, and interviews with workers and managers.
🏛️
Type 1

Regulatory Audit

Conducted by government agencies (FDA, OSHA, EPA). May be announced or unannounced. Results in official citations and required corrective actions.

🔍
Type 2

Internal Audit

Conducted by the facility's own quality or compliance team. Proactively finds gaps before external auditors do. Findings are addressed internally.

🤝
Type 3

Customer Audit

Conducted by a major buyer who wants to verify their supplier meets required standards before awarding or renewing a contract.

The Audit Process — Step by Step

Whether it's an FDA investigator or a customer quality representative, most audits follow a similar arc. Here's what each phase looks like from the facility's side.

📢
Before the Audit

Notification (Sometimes) and Preparation

Regulatory audits by OSHA and FDA may happen without notice — auditors can arrive unannounced. Customer audits are usually scheduled in advance. Internal audits are always planned. When a facility has advance notice, the quality team pulls together records, reviews recent compliance data, and conducts internal checks to find any gaps before the auditor does.

👋
Day 1 — Opening

Opening Meeting

The audit begins with a formal meeting. The auditor explains the scope of the audit (which facilities, which processes, which regulations), the schedule, who will be interviewed, and what documents will be reviewed. The facility introduces the team who will accompany and support the auditor throughout the process.

📋
During the Audit

Document Review

Auditors request and review records — SOPs, batch records, training logs, maintenance records, equipment calibration certificates, incident reports, previous audit findings and corrective actions. This is where AI-generated compliance records become enormously valuable: records that are timestamped, complete, and instantly retrievable take hours to review instead of days.

🏭
During the Audit

Facility Walkthrough

Auditors walk the production floor, observing operations in real time. They check whether what workers are actually doing matches what the SOPs say they should be doing. They look at PPE compliance, zone signage, equipment condition, storage practices, cleanliness, and whether procedures are posted and followed. This is where the gap between "what the policy says" and "what actually happens" becomes visible.

🗣️
During the Audit

Worker Interviews

Auditors talk to workers — not just managers. They ask workers to explain how they perform specific tasks, what they do when something goes wrong, and whether they've received required training. Workers who understand their procedures and can explain them clearly demonstrate a facility with real compliance, not just paperwork compliance.

📝
End of Audit

Closing Meeting and Preliminary Findings

The auditor presents preliminary findings — observations and potential violations identified during the review. The facility has an opportunity to provide clarification or additional evidence before the final report is issued. This is not a negotiation of whether rules apply — but facilities can correct misunderstandings and provide documentation the auditor may not have seen.

📊
After the Audit

Formal Report and Corrective Action

The final audit report is issued with official findings. For regulatory audits, this may include citations requiring formal corrective action plans — documenting exactly what will be fixed, how, and by when. The facility's response to audit findings is itself evaluated in future audits: demonstrating that problems were thoroughly addressed builds credibility with regulators.

What Auditors Are Actually Looking For

📋 Are procedures documented?

Do SOPs exist for all regulated processes? Are they current, approved, and accessible to workers who need them?

👷 Are procedures actually followed?

Do workers perform tasks the way SOPs describe — or has informal practice drifted from the written procedure?

🎓 Are workers trained?

Does training documentation show every worker has received required training before performing regulated tasks? Is training current?

⚙️ Is equipment maintained?

Are maintenance and calibration records complete? Is all equipment within its inspection and calibration validity period?

🔍 Are deviations investigated?

When something goes wrong, is it formally documented and investigated? Are root causes identified and corrective actions implemented?

📦 Is product quality verified?

Are testing records complete? Is product released only after meeting all specifications? Are out-of-spec results handled correctly?

Auditors aren't trying to catch facilities being evil. They're checking whether the systems that are supposed to ensure quality and safety are actually working — not just documented.

Types of Audit Findings — From Minor to Critical

Not all audit findings are equal. Understanding how findings are classified helps you understand the urgency of the required response.

Finding Type 1

📋 Observation

A low-risk gap that doesn't violate a specific requirement but represents a best-practice improvement opportunity. Typically requires acknowledgment and may suggest a corrective action, but no formal penalty.

Finding Type 2

⚠️ Minor Nonconformance

A specific requirement was not fully met, but the gap is limited in scope and doesn't directly threaten product quality or worker safety. Requires a formal corrective action plan submitted within a defined timeframe.

Finding Type 3

🚨 Major Nonconformance

A significant gap that poses real risk to product quality, worker safety, or regulatory compliance. May result in immediate corrective action requirements, temporary production holds, or regulatory citations. Must be addressed before the next audit.

Finding Type 4

🔴 Critical Finding

A serious violation that could directly cause harm to workers or consumers — or evidence of deliberate falsification of records. May result in immediate facility shutdown, product recall, import ban, or criminal referral. Requires immediate action before production can resume.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Audits are formal examinations of compliance — they check whether what's happening in the facility matches what the rules require. Three types: regulatory (government agencies), internal (self-assessment), and customer (supplier verification).

The audit process includes an opening meeting, document review, facility walkthrough, worker interviews, closing meeting with preliminary findings, and a formal report requiring corrective action. Regulatory audits (OSHA, FDA) may arrive unannounced.

Auditors look for: documented procedures, evidence they're actually followed, training records, equipment maintenance and calibration, deviation investigation, and product quality verification. Worker interviews test whether procedures are understood, not just written.

AI compliance records transform audit preparation — records that took days to compile manually become available in minutes, with timestamps, completeness, and searchability that strengthen the facility's position during review.
