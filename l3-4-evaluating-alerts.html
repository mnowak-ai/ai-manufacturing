Lesson 1.3 — Human vs. AI Roles

How to Tell a Good AI Alert From a Bad One: A Worker's Guide to Evaluating What the Machine Is Telling You

The video shows workers reviewing AI alerts. This article explains what that review actually looks like — the specific questions to ask before you act.

You get an alert. The AI system is telling you something needs your attention. What do you do next?

The worst answer is "immediately do whatever the alert recommends" — that's overtrust, and you've seen where it leads. The second worst answer is "ignore it because alerts are usually wrong" — that's alert fatigue, and you've seen where that leads too.

The right answer is in between: a fast, structured evaluation that takes 30–60 seconds and tells you how seriously to take this specific alert right now.

This article gives you that framework — five questions, in order, that any worker can apply to any AI alert on any shop floor.

The 5-Question Alert Evaluation Framework

These questions don't have to be written down. After practice, they become instinct — the mental checklist that runs automatically when an alert hits your screen or tablet.

1
What is the alert actually saying — and what data is it based on?
Before you respond, understand what you're responding to. An alert that says "Motor 4 temperature elevated — 178°F, trending upward for 6 hours" is giving you specific, traceable data. An alert that just says "anomaly detected" with no further detail is much harder to evaluate. The more specific the data, the more you can check it against what you're observing.
✓ Trust signal: Specific sensor readings with trend data
✓ Trust signal: Clear data source (which sensor, which timeframe)
⚠ Doubt signal: Vague "anomaly" with no underlying data shown
⚠ Doubt signal: Alert doesn't specify what it detected or why
2
Does this match what I'm observing right now with my own senses?
Walk over. Look. Listen. Smell. Feel the vibration. Your senses are data — different data than the sensor, and often more contextual. If the AI says the motor is running dangerously hot and you can feel the heat from three feet away, those two signals are agreeing. If the AI says something is failing and the machine sounds, smells, and feels completely normal, you have a discrepancy worth investigating. Discrepancies go both ways — your senses can be wrong too, especially for slow-developing problems.
✓ Trust signal: Sensory observations match what the alert predicts
✓ Trust signal: Something seems off even if you can't articulate what
⚠ Doubt signal: Everything looks, sounds, and feels completely normal
⚠ Doubt signal: Machine running identically to every other day this week
3
Has this type of alert been reliable before — or is it historically noisy?
Not all alert types are created equal. On a well-tuned system, some alert types fire rarely and are almost always right. Others fire constantly and are frequently wrong. Over time, you develop a feel for which is which on your specific equipment. That track record should inform how quickly and seriously you respond — not override your response entirely, but calibrate the urgency. A first-ever alert from a sensor that never fires is different from the 12th alert this month from one that's been wrong 11 times.
✓ Trust signal: This alert type has been accurate in past similar situations
✓ Trust signal: This sensor hasn't alerted in weeks or months
⚠ Doubt signal: This alert type fires constantly and is usually wrong
⚠ Doubt signal: 3rd alert this week with no follow-up issue the first 2 times
4
Has anything changed recently that the AI might not know about?
This is the blind spot question — the one that catches the defects that ship. New materials. A different supplier batch. Settings adjusted last week. Equipment that was recently serviced with non-standard parts. Any process change that happened after the AI model was trained is a potential gap in its knowledge. If something changed and this is the first alert since, ask whether the alert might be a response to the change rather than a real failure. Also consider: could the change itself be causing a real problem the AI is correctly detecting?
✓ Trust signal: Nothing has changed — conditions are the same as always
✓ Trust signal: A process change happened AND this alert makes sense in that context
⚠ Doubt signal: New material batch arrived this week — alert might reflect unfamiliar readings
⚠ Doubt signal: Equipment was serviced yesterday — this could be a post-maintenance artifact
5
Who else should be in this decision — and what's the cost of waiting?
Some alerts you can handle alone. Some require your supervisor. Some require a maintenance tech. Some require a full stop-and-escalate. The decision about who to loop in depends on two things: the severity of the potential consequence if the alert is right, and the cost of acting (or not acting) before you know. A low-severity alert on a non-critical machine can wait for a second opinion. A high-severity alert involving possible worker injury or major equipment damage should trigger escalation immediately — verify later, not before.
✓ Act alone: Low-severity, familiar alert type, observable confirmation, non-critical equipment
✓ Loop in supervisor: Medium-severity, unclear data, or involves stopping production
⚠ Escalate immediately: Any safety-critical alert, or when uncertain and consequences are high
⚠ Document either way: Whatever you decide, note what the alert said and what you did

When to Act Alone, When to Loop In, When to Escalate

The framework tells you how much to trust an alert. This tells you what to do with that assessment:

🚨
Escalate Immediately
Any alert involving immediate safety risk, potential equipment damage, or that matches your observations. Act first, verify second. Loop in supervisor before or during, not after.
📞
Loop In Before Acting
Alert is plausible but uncertain — mixed signals from your observations, process changes in play, or a decision that affects production. Get a second set of eyes before committing.
📋
Monitor and Document
Alert is low-severity, historically noisy, and doesn't match observations. Continue monitoring, document that you evaluated it and why you didn't act, and watch for changes.

The worst outcome isn't acting on a false alert or ignoring a real one. It's doing either without thinking — because thinking is what the "humans make the decisions" rule actually means.

🧠 Check Your Understanding
An AI alert fires for a machine you've worked with for three years. The alert type has been accurate every previous time it fired on this machine. Your observations confirm something sounds different than usual. What does the framework suggest?
✅ Correct. All five framework signals point the same direction: specific data, observational confirmation, historically reliable alert type, no unusual process changes. This is a high-confidence alert. The right response is to act according to the severity — which might mean escalating to supervisor immediately, calling maintenance, or initiating a controlled shutdown, depending on what the alert specifies and your facility's protocols.
❌ Not right. When all five evaluation signals point the same direction — specific data, your observations match, the alert type is historically reliable, no unusual changes in play — that's a high-confidence alert. Waiting for the machine to fail defeats the entire purpose of predictive monitoring. The framework gives you exactly the information you need to act with confidence here.

What You Learned

✓
Evaluating an AI alert is a structured skill, not a gut reaction. The 5-question framework — data clarity, observational match, historical reliability, recent changes, and escalation need — takes under a minute and dramatically improves decision quality.
✓
Your own senses are data. When observations match an alert, confidence increases. When they conflict, that discrepancy is worth investigating — not dismissing.
✓
Alert track record matters. An alert type that fires rarely and is almost always right deserves faster action than one that's been noisy and unreliable — but neither should be ignored entirely.
✓
Process changes are blind spots. Any recent change the AI wasn't trained on is a reason to apply extra scrutiny — in both directions: the alert might be a false reading, or it might be correctly detecting a real problem the change introduced.
✓
Whatever you decide — act, loop in, or monitor — document it. The record of what the alert said and what you chose to do is your professional protection and the feedback that improves the system over time.
