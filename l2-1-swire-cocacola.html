Who Is Swire Coca-Cola?

🥤
Swire Coca-Cola
What they do: One of the largest Coca-Cola bottling and distribution partners in the world — manufacturing, packaging, and distributing Coke products across multiple countries.
Scale: Dozens of production facilities, hundreds of thousands of square feet of factory and warehouse space, thousands of employees across shifts.
The safety challenge: High-speed bottling lines, forklifts, heavy pallets, loading docks, conveyor systems — all operating simultaneously across massive facilities. A complex environment where hazards are constant and hard to monitor.
The AI partner: Intenseye — a computer vision safety platform that plugs into existing CCTV camera systems.

A beverage bottling facility might not seem as dangerous as a steel mill or a chemical plant — but don't let that fool you. Fast-moving conveyors, forklift traffic, wet floors from spills, workers lifting heavy loads repeatedly all shift — this is an environment where the hazards are constant, varied, and often hard to see coming.

What Is Intenseye and What Does It Actually Do?

Key Term
Intenseye: An AI-powered computer vision platform designed specifically for workplace safety. It connects to a facility's existing security cameras and uses machine learning to detect unsafe behaviors and conditions in real time — then alerts supervisors before accidents happen.

The key phrase there is "existing cameras." Swire didn't have to rip out their current CCTV system and install new hardware everywhere. Intenseye ran its AI on top of what was already in place — which made it dramatically cheaper and faster to deploy than starting from scratch.

Here's how the system works, step by step:

1
Connect to Existing Cameras
Intenseye plugs into the facility's existing CCTV network. No new cameras required in most areas. The AI begins receiving live video feeds from every connected camera simultaneously.
Why this matters: A typical facility might have 50–500 cameras. No human team can watch all of them at once. Intenseye watches all of them, all the time.
2
Analyze Video with Computer Vision AI
The AI processes each camera feed in real time, looking for specific safety-related behaviors and conditions. It was trained on millions of images of workplace scenarios — safe and unsafe — so it knows what to look for.
Why this matters: The AI doesn't get tired, distracted, or bored after 6 hours of watching a quiet floor. It applies the same attention to hour 8 as hour 1.
3
Detect Unsafe Behaviors and Conditions
When the AI identifies a potential hazard — a worker without a hard hat, a forklift in a pedestrian zone, someone lifting with poor posture — it flags it. Not every flag triggers an immediate alarm; the system scores by severity.
Why this matters: Some hazards need immediate response. Others are tracked as trends. The system distinguishes between "alert now" and "report this pattern."
4
Alert the Right Person, Fast
Alerts go to supervisors' phones, tablets, or control room screens in real time. The alert includes a clip of the detected event, the location, the type of hazard, and a suggested response — so supervisors can act quickly without needing to investigate first.
Why this matters: Speed is critical. An alert that arrives 10 minutes after an unsafe condition is usually useless. Intenseye alerts arrive in seconds.
5
Generate Safety Analytics and Trends
Every detected event is logged. Over time, the system generates reports: which areas have the most incidents, which times of day are riskiest, which behaviors keep recurring. Safety managers use this data to target training and process improvements.
Why this matters: Before AI, safety data came from incident reports filed after accidents. Now it comes from patterns detected before accidents — a completely different kind of intelligence.

What Was the Experience Like for Workers on the Floor?

Here's a question the slide didn't answer: how did Swire's workers actually feel about being monitored by AI cameras?

The answer is nuanced — and important. Different roles experienced the system differently. Click each perspective to learn more:

Perspective: Floor Worker
The Cameras Were Already There
Most Swire facilities already had CCTV cameras for security purposes. Workers were used to being on camera. The change with Intenseye was that the cameras started doing something with what they saw — flagging safety issues instead of just recording footage that nobody reviewed unless something went wrong.

Initial reactions were mixed. Some workers appreciated the added protection — especially in high-traffic forklift areas where they'd had near-misses before. Others felt it was surveillance, not safety.

Over time, as the system proved it was flagging real hazards (not just generating noise), most workers came to accept it. The key was transparency: workers were told what the system monitored, what triggered alerts, and who received them.
What changed day-to-day
Supervisors began addressing hazards faster. Workers who received safety coaching after an AI flag generally appreciated it when it was delivered respectfully — focused on the behavior, not blaming the person.
Perspective: Line Supervisor
From "Walk and Hope" to Real-Time Visibility
Before Intenseye, a supervisor responsible for a large floor might do two or three safety walks per shift. In between, they had no real visibility into what was happening across the whole area. Problems only surfaced when workers reported them — or after an incident.

With the AI system, supervisors started receiving alerts on their phones or tablets. A flag showing a worker in a forklift zone without a vest, with a clip and a location, let them respond immediately rather than finding out at the end of the shift — or not at all.

The volume of alerts took adjustment. In early deployment, the system generated more alerts than supervisors could respond to — which created its own problems (more on that in the next article in this lesson).
What changed day-to-day
Safety conversations became more specific and timely. Instead of "we need to be more careful around forklifts," supervisors could say "here's what I saw at 2:15pm at dock 4 — let's talk about it."
Perspective: Safety Manager / EHS Director
Data Changed Everything About Safety Strategy
Before AI monitoring, safety managers worked from incident reports and near-miss logs — both of which were severely underreported. Workers often didn't file near-miss reports because of paperwork burden or fear of blame. The data picture was incomplete by design.

Intenseye gave safety managers a continuous, objective record of what was actually happening on the floor. For the first time, they could see patterns: "Dock 7 has 3× more PPE violations on night shift." "Lifting injuries cluster around the end-of-shift rush on Fridays." That's intelligence you can actually act on.

The 60% reduction in unsafe conditions wasn't magic. It came from systematically identifying the highest-risk patterns and targeting training, process changes, and equipment modifications directly at them.
What changed day-to-day
Safety programs became data-driven instead of gut-driven. Resources — training time, equipment upgrades, process changes — could be targeted at documented high-risk areas instead of spread evenly and hoped for the best.

Why Is 60% Actually a Remarkable Number?

To understand why 60% matters, you need context. Safety improvements in mature industrial facilities are hard to come by — most have already addressed the obvious hazards. Marginal gains take enormous effort.

Here's how Swire's result compares to what's typical in manufacturing safety programs:

Safety training programs (industry average improvement)10–15%
Standard safety training reduces incidents, but effects fade quickly without reinforcement.
Traditional CCTV with human safety monitors15–25%
Humans can't watch hundreds of feeds simultaneously. Coverage is limited and inconsistent.
Process redesign and engineering controls20–35%
Effective but expensive and slow — physical changes to equipment and facility layout.
Swire Coca-Cola with Intenseye AI60%
Continuous monitoring + real-time alerts + data-driven targeting of highest-risk behaviors.

60% isn't just a better number. It represents a different category of intervention — one where the system never sleeps, never gets complacent, and never forgets to watch the dock while it's checking the conveyor.

What Does 60% Mean in Human Terms?

A 60% reduction in unsafe conditions means:

If in a given month there were 100 observable hazardous situations on Swire's floors — a worker without a hard hat, a forklift too close to a pedestrian, a repeated unsafe lift — after Intenseye, that number dropped to around 40.

Not every unsafe condition leads to an accident. But every accident starts as an unsafe condition. Reducing hazards at the source is the most effective safety strategy there is.

Swire's result also came with a documented reduction in near-misses and a measurable decrease in recordable incidents — the official OSHA measure of injuries serious enough to require medical treatment beyond first aid. That's a real result for real people who go home safe at the end of their shift.
