What Is a Work Order? The Document That Drives Almost Every Maintenance Task

On your first day in manufacturing maintenance, someone will hand you a work order. This article explains exactly what it is, every field it contains, and how AI is transforming the way work orders are created, scheduled, and closed out.

The Most Important Document You'll Work With

A work order is the official document that authorizes, assigns, and tracks a maintenance task. If someone notices a machine problem, there's no official response until a work order exists. If a work order isn't closed out, the repair has no documented existence in the system. The work order is the paper trail that connects an alert to a completed repair — and everything in between.

In older facilities, work orders were paper forms in a binder. In modern facilities with AI-enabled maintenance systems, they're digital records that integrate with sensor data, inventory systems, and scheduling AI in real time. Either way, the fundamental concept is the same.

📖 Key Term
Work Order (WO) — A document that formally authorizes a specific maintenance task, assigns it to a technician, specifies required parts and estimated labor, tracks its progress, and records the outcome. Work orders are managed in a CMMS (Computerized Maintenance Management System) in most modern facilities.

1
Work Order Number (WO-2024-11847)
Every work order gets a unique identifier. This is how the task is tracked across all systems — from the initial alert, through scheduling, parts procurement, and final closeout. If someone asks "what's the status of WO-11847?" the system shows exactly where it is in the workflow.
AI role: Modern CMMS systems can auto-generate WO numbers and link them to the sensor alert that triggered the work, creating an automatic chain of evidence.
2
Priority Level
Priority tells the planner and technician how urgently this task needs attention. Facilities typically use 3–4 priority levels: Emergency (machine down NOW), Urgent (failure likely within 1–3 days), Routine (can be scheduled in the next 1–2 weeks), and Preventive (planned maintenance due). Priority drives the scheduling decision.
AI role: Predictive maintenance AI can suggest priority levels based on trend rate — "vibration rising 0.06 mm/s per day means failure in approximately 12–18 days" — helping planners allocate urgency accurately.
3
Problem Description
The description connects the symptom to the action. A well-written description includes what was observed (vibration trend), what the AI suggested (bearing wear — 79%), and what action is required (inspect before ordering parts). A poorly written description just says "motor issue" — which forces the technician to investigate from scratch.
AI role: AI-integrated CMMS systems can auto-populate the problem description with sensor data context — trend duration, confidence score, and recommended first action — saving time and improving information quality.
4
Assigned Technician + Skills
Not every technician can handle every task. The assigned technician's skill level matters — replacing a motor bearing in an electrical enclosure requires electrical qualification (lockout/tagout, working safely with electrical equipment). Assigning the wrong skill set to a task is a safety risk.
AI role: Scheduling AI automatically matches work order requirements to technician skill sets — ensuring the right person is assigned without a planner having to manually check credentials for each task.
5
Actual Findings (Closeout)
This field is filled in by the technician after completing the repair. It's the most important field for AI improvement — and the most commonly neglected. "Actual findings" captures what was really wrong, what was done, and any other discoveries. This is the feedback loop that teaches AI what its predictions actually matched.
AI role: Mobile apps let technicians enter findings directly from the machine floor — with photo capture — so closeout happens in real time rather than hours later from memory (which degrades data quality).

Three Types of Work Orders

Not all maintenance work orders are created equal. The three types reflect fundamentally different approaches to maintenance — and AI has transformed the balance between them in modern facilities.

🔥
Reactive (Corrective)
Machine already failed. Respond and repair. No warning, no planning time. Highest cost because of emergency labor rates, production loss, and potential secondary damage. The default mode before AI. Also called "run-to-failure."
📅
Preventive (Scheduled)
Replace or service components on a calendar schedule — every 3 months, every 10,000 hours, etc. Regardless of actual condition. Better than reactive but often replaces parts that still had useful life, or misses problems between scheduled intervals.
🤖
Predictive (AI-Driven)
Perform maintenance when sensor data predicts a developing problem — not on a calendar, not after failure. This is what AI monitoring enables. Work orders are created based on real condition data, not time intervals. The goal: fix it just before it fails.

Facilities that move from reactive to predictive maintenance typically see 25–45% reductions in unplanned downtime. The work order is the same document — but AI changes how and when it's created.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

A work order is the official document that authorizes, assigns, and tracks a maintenance task. Nothing officially exists in the maintenance system without a work order — not the problem, not the repair, not the outcome.

The Actual Findings field at closeout is the most important field for AI improvement — and the most neglected. Vague closeouts ("replaced bearing — machine fine") strip the system of the feedback data that makes AI predictions more accurate over time.

There are three types of work orders: reactive (respond to failure), preventive (scheduled intervals), and predictive (AI-driven, based on condition data). Facilities that shift from reactive to predictive typically see 25–45% reductions in unplanned downtime.

AI integrates with work orders at multiple points: auto-generating WOs from sensor alerts, suggesting priority levels based on trend rates, matching WOs to technician skills via scheduling AI, and updating training data from closeout findings. The work order is the central document that ties all of this together.
