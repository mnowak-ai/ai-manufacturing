AI Helps You Write — But You Decide What to Say

Understanding the human's role in AI-assisted communication. The lesson says "AI is a support, not a replacement." This article explains exactly what that means — including the professional and legal implications that every manufacturing worker needs to understand before using AI to draft work messages.

The Core Principle — Say It Out Loud Until It Sticks

Here is the single most important thing to understand about using AI for workplace communication:

AI drafts the words.
You decide what's true.
Everything you send is your professional communication — regardless of who or what helped you write it.

This isn't just a philosophy point. It has practical consequences. If an AI draft contains a wrong detail and you send it without catching the error, you sent the wrong information. If a maintenance request you sent with AI help leads to the wrong machine being serviced, that's your mistake — not the AI's. If a safety report you drafted with AI assistance contains an inaccuracy that becomes part of the legal record, you're accountable.

This isn't meant to make using AI feel risky. Using AI to help draft work messages is smart and professional. This is meant to make the human review step feel as important as it is — because it is.

Think of AI like autocorrect, but for whole sentences. Autocorrect helps you type faster. You still read what you typed before sending. Same principle — just at a higher level.

What AI Owns vs. What You Own

There's a clear division of responsibility in AI-assisted communication. Understanding it helps you use AI confidently without losing sight of where your judgment is required.

A Note on Legal and Safety Documentation

This matters most for safety reports, incident documentation, and maintenance records — three types of communication that often have legal significance.

Safety incident reports become part of the facility's OSHA recordkeeping. Maintenance records are used for warranty claims, equipment audits, and liability purposes. If an AI draft contains inaccurate information in these documents and you send it without reviewing it, you've created an inaccurate official record — and you're accountable for it.

This doesn't mean you can't use AI to help structure these documents. It means that for high-stakes documentation, the review step is especially careful. Every specific detail — dates, quantities, substances, actions taken, people involved — must be verified against what you actually know before the document is submitted.

⚠️ Extra care required for:
Safety incident reports · OSHA documentation · Maintenance records used for warranty or audit purposes · Quality records in regulated industries (medical devices, aerospace, food processing) · Any message that will be stored as a formal record

Quick Check

Knowledge Check
A supervisor confronts a maintenance technician about a safety report that contained incorrect information — the report said an inspection was completed, but the AI generated that detail and the technician never actually inspected the equipment. The technician says: "That's what the AI wrote — I just sent it." Is this a valid defense?
✅ Correct. "The AI wrote it" is not a professional defense for sending inaccurate communication. The moment you review and send a message, you accept ownership of its contents. The AI cannot be held professionally accountable — you can. This is exactly why the review step is non-negotiable, and why the principle "AI drafts, humans own" matters. The technician sent a false safety record. That is the technician's professional problem, not the AI's.
❌ "The AI wrote it" is not a valid professional defense. When you send a message, you're saying "this is what I'm communicating." AI is a drafting tool — it has no professional accountability. The technician reviewed the document (or should have), sent it, and submitted it as a safety record. That makes it their professional communication and their responsibility. The lesson here: never send anything you haven't read and verified, especially for safety or legal documentation.
