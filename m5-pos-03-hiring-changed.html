How Manufacturing Hiring Has Changed: What Employers Were Looking for 10 Years Ago vs. Today

Every generation of manufacturing workers had to adapt to new technology. Here's how the hiring bar shifted — and why your AI training puts you ahead of where most workers are right now.

This Isn't the First Time Manufacturing Changed

If you talk to someone who has worked in manufacturing for 30 or 40 years, they'll tell you that the job has changed dramatically — more than once. They started with one set of skills and had to keep learning new ones as technology changed around them.

That's not unusual. That's how every major industry works. And understanding that history matters, because it tells you something important: the workers who kept learning are the ones who are still in the room. The ones who refused to adapt often got left behind. The ones who leaned in got promoted.

AI is the latest shift. It's a big one. But it's not the first — and knowing what happened before makes it easier to understand what's happening now.

Every new wave of technology in manufacturing felt scary to some workers and exciting to others. The workers who got curious always came out ahead.

The Workers Who Kept Up — And the Ones Who Didn't

When CNC machines came into manufacturing in the 1980s, some workers refused to learn them. They said their manual machining skills were good enough. Some of them were right — for a while. But the plants that adopted CNC ran more productively, and eventually the workers who hadn't adapted found fewer places to work.

The workers who learned CNC programming didn't replace the manual machinists — they became more valuable than them. They could operate machines that were faster and more precise, and they commanded higher wages because their skills were harder to find.

The same thing happened with ERP software in the 1990s, with robotics in the 2000s, and with digital quality systems in the 2010s. Every time: the workers who learned the new technology moved up. The workers who didn't found the job getting smaller around them.

AI is happening on a faster timeline than any of these previous shifts. The workers who are learning it now — while it's still new — are positioning themselves the same way those early CNC programmers did.

What This Means for You Right Now

You're on the right side of this shift
Most manufacturing workers today were hired before AI tools became widespread in their facilities. They're figuring it out as they go — learning on the job, often without any structured training. You've done something different: you studied it deliberately, with curriculum, before entering the workforce. That's the equivalent of being an early CNC programmer. You're not behind — you're ahead of where most people are. The question is whether you use that advantage or let it sit unused.
