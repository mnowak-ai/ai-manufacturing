How Wrkspce.ai Helped PrecisionTech Schedule a Production Floor

The lesson video mentions PrecisionTech's 28% throughput increase and 35% downtime reduction. This article explains what those numbers actually mean, how the AI system worked, and what changed for every worker on the floor.

Meet PrecisionTech Manufacturing

PrecisionTech is a mid-size metal fabrication company. They make custom precision components — things like brackets, housings, and machined shafts — for the automotive and industrial equipment industries. Their customers demand tight tolerances, strict quality standards, and on-time delivery. Being late or delivering defective parts isn't just embarrassing — it can mean losing a major account.

Before implementing AI scheduling, PrecisionTech ran their production floor the way most mid-size manufacturers did: a combination of ERP software, Excel spreadsheets, and experienced planners who carried a lot of scheduling knowledge in their heads. It worked — until it didn't.

PrecisionTech Manufacturing

Increasing order volume + more complex custom jobs + machine breakdowns and worker variability = scheduling falling apart multiple times per week. Manual re-planning was taking hours. Customer complaints were rising.
What They Implemented
Wrkspce.ai, an AI scheduling and production optimization tool, integrated with their existing SAP ERP system and Siemens MES. Deployed across all three production shifts over a 6-month rollout.

Three Systems Working Together

Before we explain what Wrkspce.ai does, you need to understand what it plugs into. At PrecisionTech — and at most modern manufacturing facilities — there are three layers of software managing the operation. Each one does something different, and they're most powerful when connected.

📊
SAP ERP
Enterprise Resource Planning — the business management system

SAP is one of the world's most widely used business software systems. ERP stands for Enterprise Resource Planning — think of it as the central database that runs the entire business. It stores customer orders, inventory levels, supplier information, financial data, employee records, and production history.

When a customer places an order with PrecisionTech, SAP records it. When materials arrive from a supplier, SAP updates the inventory count. When a work order is created, SAP stores all the details. Every major business event flows through SAP.

What SAP gives Wrkspce.ai: The current work orders and their deadlines, material inventory levels, worker certifications, machine capability records, and historical job completion data — all the inputs the AI needs to build an intelligent schedule.
🏭
Siemens MES
Manufacturing Execution System — the real-time floor monitor

MES stands for Manufacturing Execution System. While SAP manages the business side, the MES manages the production floor in real time. Siemens is one of the leading MES providers for industrial manufacturing.

The MES is connected to sensors on every machine — monitoring temperature, vibration, speed, output rate, and error signals. When a machine starts a job, the MES tracks it. When the job finishes, the MES records the actual completion time. When a machine goes down, the MES knows within seconds and flags the event.

What Siemens MES gives Wrkspce.ai: Live machine status data — which machines are running, which are idle, which just flagged an anomaly. This is the "what's actually happening on the floor right now" feed that allows AI to replan in real time instead of working from yesterday's data.
🧠
Wrkspce.ai
AI Scheduling Agent — the optimizer that sits on top

Wrkspce.ai is described as an AI scheduling agent — not just software that stores information, but software that actively makes recommendations and optimizes decisions. It sits on top of SAP and Siemens MES, reading data from both and using AI algorithms to generate, monitor, and update production schedules.

An "agent" in AI means it doesn't just respond when asked — it continuously monitors conditions and proactively surfaces issues. If the MES signals that a machine is running slow at 9 AM, Wrkspce.ai doesn't wait for a planner to notice. It immediately calculates the downstream impact and generates recovery options.

What Wrkspce.ai does with all that data: Generates optimized production schedules, monitors schedule execution in real time, flags developing problems before they become crises, and offers re-plan options when disruptions hit — all presented as Gantt charts the production team can review and approve.

What a Day With Wrkspce.ai Looks Like

It's easy to talk about AI scheduling in abstract terms. Let's make it concrete. Here's how a typical production day at PrecisionTech actually runs with Wrkspce.ai in place — tracking which actions are AI-driven, which are human decisions, and which are a combination of both.

A Day in the Life
Monday at PrecisionTech — AI + Human at Work
5:30 AM — Before the Shift Starts
Wrkspce.ai generates Monday's optimized schedule overnight
While the facility is quiet, the AI has already pulled Monday's work orders from SAP, checked current inventory levels, confirmed which machines are scheduled for maintenance, and generated an optimized Gantt chart for the full day shift. The production manager receives it by email and on their tablet dashboard.
AI Action
6:45 AM — Production Manager Reviews
Human reviews AI schedule before approving it
Maria, the production manager, reviews the AI-generated Gantt chart on her tablet. She notices the AI has assigned a complex CNC job to Jordan — who completed their certification just three weeks ago. The AI doesn't know Jordan is still building speed. Maria reassigns that job to Alex and approves the rest of the schedule as-is. She releases it to the floor at 6:55 AM.
Human Decision
7:00 AM — Shift Starts
Workers receive digital work assignments on floor terminals and tablets
Workers clock in and check their assignments on floor terminals — digital screens mounted near each workstation. Each worker sees their first job, which machine, what materials to pull from the staging area, and what the target completion time is. No paper. No walking to a central board. No ambiguity.
AI + Human
8:22 AM — Anomaly Detected
Wrkspce.ai flags elevated vibration on CNC Mill #3
The Siemens MES feeds live sensor data to Wrkspce.ai. At 8:22 AM, CNC Mill #3's vibration sensor crosses a threshold — not a full alarm, but above the normal range. Wrkspce.ai immediately calculates: if Mill #3 goes down, three jobs are affected. It generates two recovery options and flags the issue on Maria's dashboard within 90 seconds of the anomaly appearing.
AI Action
8:35 AM — Maintenance Called, Decision Made
Maria reviews AI options, calls maintenance, approves re-plan
Maria walks to Mill #3 herself — it does sound slightly off. She calls maintenance for an assessment. The tech confirms: a bearing is wearing unevenly. Not critical yet, but should be addressed today. Maria picks the AI's Option B: shift Mill #3's afternoon jobs to Mill #4 and do the bearing swap during lunch. The updated schedule is pushed to all worker terminals within minutes.
Human Decision
11:45 AM — Mid-Shift Progress Check
AI compares actual progress vs. planned schedule, flags one job running 18 minutes behind
The MES tracks actual job completion times against the plan. By late morning, the AI has identified that WO-2214 on the laser cutter is running about 18 minutes behind — not because of a machine issue, but because the material setup took longer than estimated. The AI automatically calculates whether this delay affects any downstream deadlines. It doesn't — there's a natural buffer in the afternoon. No action needed. The flag is logged but no alert is escalated.
AI Action
3:30 PM — Shift Handoff
AI generates evening shift briefing; shift supervisor reviews and hands off
Wrkspce.ai automatically generates a shift handoff report: what was completed, what's in progress, what the evening shift is inheriting, and what to watch for. The day shift supervisor reviews it, adds a note about the Mill #3 bearing swap, and the evening shift supervisor starts their shift with full context — instead of spending 20 minutes being verbally briefed.

The Numbers: What Do 28% and 35% Actually Mean?

The lesson video mentions two headline results from PrecisionTech's Wrkspce.ai implementation: a 28% increase in throughput and a 35% reduction in unplanned downtime. These sound impressive — but what do they actually mean for the people working on the floor every day?

28%
Increase in Throughput
The factory is producing 28% more finished output per shift — without adding machines, workers, or hours. The same resources are being used more efficiently.
35%
Reduction in Unplanned Downtime
Machines are unexpectedly stopping 35% less often — because the AI catches early warning signs before they become full breakdowns.

But percentages alone don't tell the human story. Let's translate each number into what it actually changed for the people doing the work.

📈
28% Throughput Increase — What It Means in Practice
Before Wrkspce.ai
Machines regularly sat idle for 20–40 minutes between jobs while planners figured out what came next, materials were located, and workers received their new assignments. Rush orders disrupted the entire week's schedule because re-planning manually took hours. Jobs frequently queued up at bottleneck machines — workers waiting with nothing to do while other machines downstream sat empty.
After Wrkspce.ai
Workers see their next assignment before their current job finishes. Materials are pre-staged at the next workstation. When a disruption hits, a new plan is available in minutes rather than hours. The gaps between jobs shrank. Machine utilization increased. The same 200 workers running the same 40+ machines are producing roughly 28% more output — not by working harder, but by working with less wasted time between tasks.
🔧
35% Downtime Reduction — What It Means in Practice
Before Wrkspce.ai
Machines broke down without warning — mid-job, mid-shift, mid-week. When a key machine went down unexpectedly, everything behind it in the schedule stopped. Workers waited. Jobs missed deadlines. Emergency maintenance crews worked under pressure. The chaos of an unplanned breakdown could affect multiple customers' orders and take days to recover from.
After Wrkspce.ai
The AI reads live sensor data from every machine continuously. When vibration patterns, temperature curves, or current draws start drifting from normal — even slightly — Wrkspce.ai flags the machine for maintenance review before the breakdown happens. Maintenance now does more planned work (scheduled during low-production windows) and less emergency repair work. Workers experience fewer mid-shift surprises. Customers see fewer late deliveries caused by machine failures.
🧑‍💼
What Changed for the Production Planner
Before Wrkspce.ai
Building the weekly schedule took 3–4 hours every Sunday. When something disrupted the plan on Monday morning, re-planning could take another 1–2 hours. The planner spent most of their day reacting — moving jobs around, fielding calls from supervisors about machine conflicts, manually tracking which jobs were behind. Little time for anything else.
After Wrkspce.ai
The AI generates the initial weekly schedule in under a minute. The planner reviews it, applies their judgment (like Maria's reassignment of Jordan's job), and approves — typically in 20–30 minutes instead of hours. When disruptions hit, recovery options appear within minutes rather than requiring manual recalculation. The planner now spends more time on process improvement, customer communication, and training — work that AI genuinely cannot do.

What the AI Still Couldn't Do

PrecisionTech's results are real and significant. But it's important to be clear about what Wrkspce.ai did not do — because understanding the limits is just as important as understanding the benefits.

The AI did not know that Jordan was still learning. Maria had to catch that. The AI did not walk to Mill #3 to listen to the bearing sound and make a judgment call about urgency. Maria did that. The AI did not explain to a customer why their order was running two hours late and negotiate an acceptable delivery window. That was a human relationship conversation the AI couldn't participate in.

Wrkspce.ai didn't replace PrecisionTech's production team. It gave them back the hours they were spending on manual calculation — so they could spend those hours on the judgment work that actually requires a human.

The 28% throughput increase and 35% downtime reduction didn't come from the AI doing everything. They came from the AI handling the high-volume, fast-calculation work (scheduling, monitoring, flagging), freeing humans to focus on the contextual, relational, and judgment-intensive work that the AI couldn't reach.

Quick Check
Wrkspce.ai detected an anomaly on CNC Mill #3's vibration sensor and generated two recovery options. Maria, the production manager, reviewed them and chose Option B. What does this sequence of events illustrate about how AI scheduling tools are supposed to work?
✅ Correct. The AI caught the anomaly in the sensor data within 90 seconds and calculated the downstream implications across all affected jobs — work that would have taken a human planner 30+ minutes to analyze manually, if they noticed the issue at all. Maria brought what the AI couldn't: she physically walked to the machine, she listened to the bearing sound, and she applied 12 years of production experience to calibrate the urgency correctly. Neither the AI alone nor Maria alone would have handled this as well as the combination of both.
❌ The partnership is the point. The AI's value was catching the anomaly early (before a breakdown) and generating options quickly (within 90 seconds). But choosing which option to implement required information the AI couldn't access — like the actual sound the bearing was making, or Maria's knowledge of how that particular machine tends to behave when it's nearing a failure. Auto-selecting an option without human review would mean the AI is acting on incomplete information. That's exactly when expensive mistakes happen.
