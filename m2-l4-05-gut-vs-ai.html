What to Do When AI and Your Gut Disagree: A Decision Guide for Manufacturing Workers

The AI says keep running. Something feels off. This happens — and how you handle it matters. This article gives you a concrete decision path: what to check, how to escalate, and how to document your reasoning when you trust yourself over the machine.

First: Take Your Gut Seriously

When something feels wrong, that feeling is data. Not definitive data — your gut can be wrong for bad reasons, including automation bias in reverse, fatigue, or unfamiliarity with a new machine. But it can also be right, because you're perceiving something real that the sensors aren't capturing: an unusual sound, a vibration pattern in the floor, a subtle change in the smell of the coolant, a visual you noticed that isn't in any alert.

Experienced workers' instincts are trained pattern recognition — built from years of seeing, hearing, and physically feeling what healthy and unhealthy machines feel like. That's a form of sensing that no current AI system can replicate. Your job isn't to override this. It's to investigate it systematically before deciding what to do with it.

The goal isn't to trust your gut over AI or AI over your gut. It's to use both — and investigate whenever they disagree.

Not All Gut Feelings Are Equal

✅ Gut Feelings Worth Acting On
Grounded in specific observation
→ "I can hear a grinding sound this machine didn't make yesterday"
→ "The chip color is wrong — these should be silver, not blue"
→ "This part feels slightly rough where it should be smooth"
→ "I've seen this vibration pattern before — it preceded a bearing failure on Machine 7"
→ "The coolant smells different — there may be contamination"
⚠️ Gut Feelings Worth Questioning
Vague or mood-based
→ "This machine just feels like it's going to fail"
→ "I don't trust this AI, it's always wrong" (general frustration)
→ "I haven't had my coffee yet and something feels off about everything"
→ "I'm nervous because this is a new machine I'm not used to"
→ "It just doesn't feel right" (with no specific observation attached)

The first category deserves immediate investigation — you have a specific physical observation to follow up on. The second category is worth being honest about: Are you reacting to something real, or to your mood or habits? Checking in with a colleague can help calibrate this.

The Decision Path: What to Do Step by Step

Step 1
Can I articulate what specifically feels wrong?
✅ Yes
Name the specific observation (sound, feel, visual, smell, pattern). Write it down. This is your investigation starting point. Proceed to Step 2.
⚠️ No — vague
Check in with a colleague: "Does anything seem off to you?" If yes, investigate together. If no, note the feeling and monitor — don't ignore it, but don't override AI without evidence yet.
Step 2
Can I physically check what's bothering me in 5–10 minutes?
✅ Yes
Pause the machine if needed (or inspect during next natural pause). Conduct the physical check. Document what you find — or confirm "checked, normal." Proceed to Step 3.
⚠️ Needs more time
Assess urgency. If your concern involves safety or high-stakes quality, notify your supervisor now and pause production until you can investigate. If lower stakes, schedule investigation at next opportunity. Never just dismiss it.
Step 3
Did the physical check find anything that confirms your concern?
✅ Yes — found something
Document your finding. Determine whether it's safe and acceptable to continue production, or whether the machine needs to be stopped and a work order created. Override the AI recommendation with documented reasoning.
⚠️ No — nothing found
Document "checked X, normal." Proceed with AI recommendation — your instinct triggered appropriate verification, and the result was "all clear." That's good process. Note the check in the work order.
Step 4
If you're overriding the AI: is this a safety or high-consequence decision?
🔴 Yes — safety involved
Stop production. Notify supervisor immediately. Document your reasoning clearly: what you observed, what you checked, what you found. Do not restart without supervisor sign-off.
✅ No — quality/efficiency
Create work order documenting your override with reasoning. Notify supervisor per your facility's override notification protocol. Continue or stop production based on severity of finding. Always document — the AI learns from overrides.

How to Document an Override

Overriding an AI recommendation is not insubordination — it's exercising professional judgment. But that judgment needs to be documented. A well-documented override shows: what the AI recommended, what you observed that triggered your concern, what you physically checked, what you found, and what decision you made. This protects you, improves the AI over time, and creates a record that management can review.

AI said
Record the AI's exact recommendation: "AI recommended: adjust coolant flow rate +5% and continue. Confidence: 67%."
I observed
Describe your specific physical observation: "I noticed a faint burning smell near the spindle housing that wasn't present yesterday. Spindle temperature reading 74°C (normal is 68°C)."
I checked
Document what you physically inspected: "Physically inspected spindle housing. Found coolant return line partially kinked — coolant flow restricted to estimated 40% of normal."
I found
Document the finding: "Kinked coolant line causing thermal buildup. AI's recommendation (increase coolant flow rate) would have been irrelevant to the actual restriction."
I decided
Document your action and rationale: "Stopped machine. Straightened coolant line. Verified flow restored to spec. Restarted — spindle temp returned to 69°C within 10 minutes. Override was correct."

A technician smells something unusual near a machine, checks it physically, finds nothing wrong, and documents "checked — all normal." The AI recommendation stays unchanged. Was this a waste of time?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

When your gut and AI disagree, investigate — don't default to either automatically. A specific physical observation (sound, smell, visual, pattern) is worth checking even when the AI shows no alert. A vague feeling without a specific observation is worth noting but should prompt a colleague check before overriding.

The four-step decision path: Name the specific concern → Conduct the physical check → Evaluate what you found → If overriding, document with full reasoning and notify appropriately. Each step creates accountability and improves the system.

Document everything — the AI's recommendation, your observation, your check, your finding, and your decision. A well-documented override protects you professionally, improves the AI over time, and demonstrates exactly the kind of critical thinking this module teaches.
