When Should You Trust AI and When Should You Push Back? A Decision Framework for Manufacturing Workers

Knowing that you should evaluate AI recommendations is different from knowing how to evaluate them. This article gives you a practical framework — five questions to ask before acting on any AI suggestion — with concrete manufacturing examples for each.

The Problem With "Just Follow the AI"

In a perfect world, AI systems would always be right, would always account for current floor conditions, would always have perfect data, and would always understand the full context of a situation. In that world, "just follow the AI" would be reasonable advice.

We don't live in that world. AI recommendations are built on historical data that may not reflect today's conditions. They optimize for metrics that may not capture everything that matters. They can't see what happened on the floor this morning that hasn't been logged yet. They can't feel that a situation is "off" in a way that experienced workers sometimes recognize before they can articulate why.

The question is never "should I think about AI recommendations?" The answer is always yes. The question is how to think about them — what to ask, what to verify, and what to trust — and how to do it quickly enough to be useful on a fast-moving production floor.

Four Response Levels — Before the Questions

Before walking through the framework, let's name the four responses available to you when an AI recommendation arrives. These are your options — and your goal is to choose the right one for each situation.

✅
Trust and Act
The recommendation is routine, well within the AI's proven accuracy range, low-consequence if wrong, and consistent with what you're seeing. Act on it.
🔍
Verify Before Acting
Something about the recommendation warrants a quick check — floor walk, supplier call, data review, conversation with a colleague — before you commit to it.
📣
Escalate to Supervisor
The decision is above your authority level, involves safety or significant cost, or requires organizational context you don't have. Bring it to the right person.
🚫
Override It
You have clear, specific reasons — based on floor knowledge, safety assessment, or recent context the AI doesn't have — to not follow the recommendation. Document why.

What You Learned

✓
The four response options for any AI recommendation: Trust and Act, Verify Before Acting, Escalate to Supervisor, and Override. Choosing the right response is the core professional judgment skill in AI-assisted manufacturing.
✓
The five-question framework: Does this involve safety? Has anything changed that the AI might not know? Does this match what I'm seeing? Has the AI been accurate in similar situations before? Does this feel off?
✓
Safety overrides everything. Any AI recommendation that reduces or eliminates a safety control requires escalation or override — not just a faster approval. The question is not whether the AI's math is right, but whether it modeled the full risk.
✓
When you override an AI recommendation, document the reason. Not because the AI needs it — but because your reasoning is professional value that should be captured, shared, and potentially fed back into improving the system.
