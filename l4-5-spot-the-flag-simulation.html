Simulation: Can You Spot What the AI Would Flag?

A realistic shop floor scenario with multiple things happening at once. Your job: decide what the AI monitoring system would catch, what it would miss, and what only a human would notice.

You've learned how AI safety systems work, how quality inspection systems work, and how diagnostic systems work. Now let's put all of it together in a realistic scenario.

The shop floor is always running multiple things simultaneously. Multiple machines. Multiple workers. Multiple conditions — some normal, some not. AI systems are watching, but they see specific things and miss specific things. Your job right now is to think like both the AI and the experienced worker at the same time.

Part 2: The Full AI vs. Human Picture

Here's what the AI systems saw — versus what a skilled human supervisor would add to that picture:

🚨 AI Would Flag This
Press Station 1 — Temperature Elevated
🤖 What the AI sees
Motor temp sensor: 181°F, baseline 152°F. Deviation: +29°F over 6 hours, trend upward. Alert generated. Confidence: 87%.
👤 What the human adds
You know this machine ran heavy-gauge steel all morning — a harder material that drives the motor harder. Check whether the workload explains the reading before calling maintenance. Physical check: housing warm but not hot, no unusual sounds. Monitor, don't halt yet.
🚨 AI Would Flag This
Worker on Conveyor Line — No Safety Glasses
🤖 What the AI sees
Camera 7 detects worker in PPE-required zone without eye protection. Alert to supervisor. Timestamp logged. Camera clip saved for safety record.
👤 What the human adds
You address it directly, respectfully, in the moment — and ask why. Is it a training gap? Comfort issue with the current glasses? Glasses that aren't nearby? The AI caught the violation; you determine the cause and the right response to prevent recurrence.
🚨 AI Would Flag This
Quality Line — Hairline Crack on Part
🤖 What the AI sees
Vision inspection camera: surface anomaly detected at 14mm position, matches hairline crack training pattern. Confidence: 94%. Part diverted for human review. Alert generated.
👤 What the human adds
You examine the diverted part. The crack is real — confirmed under magnification. You then ask: is this the first one today, or are we seeing a pattern? You check the production log for the last 2 hours. If multiple parts are showing cracks, there's a process problem to find and fix, not just parts to sort.
⚠️ AI Would Miss This
Hydraulic Fluid on the Floor — Not Yet Detected
🤖 What the AI sees
No alert. The floor camera is positioned for forklift/pedestrian detection, not low-angle surface monitoring. The fluid is a thin, translucent layer on a dark floor — not detected by the current camera configuration or slip hazard algorithm.
👤 What the human adds
You see it — or smell it, or notice the reflective sheen as you walk through. You call for housekeeping immediately, put out a wet floor sign, and identify the source: a slow leak from Press Station 2's hydraulic line. Two things get done: the floor cleaned and the leak fixed. Neither would have happened without the walkthrough.
⚠️ AI Would Miss This
Worker Acting Unusual — Appears Fatigued or Unwell
🤖 What the AI sees
No alert. The worker is not missing PPE, not in a restricted zone, and not producing visible defects. Basic fatigue detection (eye closure) is not configured at this station. No camera flag generated.
👤 What the human adds
You notice: slower than usual movement, slightly unfocused gaze, dropped a part that didn't register to them. You check in quietly. The worker discloses they're not feeling well — started feeling sick an hour ago. You arrange for relief and send them to rest. A fatigued worker on a press is a safety risk no camera catches the way a supervisor does.
✓ Normal — No Flag Needed
Blocked Pedestrian Aisle — Temporary Pallet
🤖 What the AI sees
Camera 12 detects obstruction in pedestrian walkway zone. Alert generated: blocked aisle, pedestrian path compromised.
👤 What the human adds
You see that the pallet is a materials staging — it's been there for 3 minutes while the forklift is on its way. The "obstruction" is a normal part of the receiving process. You acknowledge the AI's flag, confirm the forklift is en route, and monitor to ensure the aisle is cleared in the next 5 minutes. Not every alert requires the same response — context determines urgency.

The AI saw three things and generated three alerts. A human supervisor saw six things — including two the AI couldn't detect at all, and one that needed context to interpret correctly. That's the partnership.

What You Learned

✓
AI monitoring systems catch specific, trained-for conditions reliably — PPE violations, sensor deviations, known visual defects, zone breaches. These are genuine alerts worth responding to.
✓
AI monitoring systems miss conditions outside their sensor coverage or training — thin fluid on dark floors, worker behavioral changes, novel defect types, and anything that requires contextual interpretation.
✓
The same AI alert can mean different things depending on context. A temperature alert during heavy-gauge steel processing means something different than the same alert during light work — only the human with production context knows which.
✓
Physical walkthroughs remain essential even with comprehensive AI coverage — not because AI is unreliable, but because walkthroughs find things cameras aren't positioned or configured to see.
✓
The AI + human combination sees more than either alone. In this scenario: AI caught 3 real issues; the human supervisor caught 5 — including 2 that the AI had no way to detect. That's the partnership in action.
