The Validation Checklist: How to Evaluate Any AI Recommendation Before Acting on It

The slide says "validation starts with questions." This article turns those three questions into a complete, step-by-step framework — with an interactive checklist you can use on real alerts.

Why a Checklist Matters

Skilled maintenance technicians don't validate AI alerts by instinct alone. They use a mental checklist — a set of questions they ask themselves before acting on any recommendation. Over time, this becomes automatic. But when you're starting out, having an explicit checklist prevents the two most common mistakes: acting too fast (over-trust) or dismissing too quickly (under-trust).

The Lesson 2.2 slides identify three core validation questions. This article expands each one into a full set of checks — organized into a framework you can apply to any AI recommendation you receive on the factory floor.

The Three Core Questions

Every AI alert validation starts with these three questions. They're simple — but the depth of your answers is what separates a careful technician from one who causes expensive mistakes.

Question 1
Does this match the data?
Look at what the sensors are actually showing. Does the AI's suggested cause logically connect to the specific readings that triggered the alert? Or is the AI pattern-matching to a similar-looking scenario that has a different underlying cause?
Question 2
Does this fit the situation?
What's happening in the real world that the sensors can't see? Recent maintenance work, new materials, new operators, environmental conditions — any of these can explain a sensor reading without there being a real equipment problem. Context is everything.
Question 3
Does this make sense?
Step back. Given everything you know — the data, the context, the machine's history, your own experience — does the AI's recommendation actually make logical sense? If something feels off about it, investigate that feeling. You know things the AI doesn't.

Triggered by:Pressure: 148 PSI (normal: 160–200 PSI)
Trend:Declining 5 PSI/day over 6 days
Suggested cause:Hydraulic pump wear — 71% confidence
Other sensors:Temperature: 74°C (normal) · Vibration: 1.4 mm/s (normal)
Action suggested:Inspect hydraulic pump, schedule replacement if wear confirmed
🔵 Question 1: Does This Match the Data?
Is the pressure reading genuinely below the normal range?
148 PSI is 12 PSI below the 160 PSI minimum. Yes — this is a real deviation, not a sensor rounding error.
Is the trend consistent and real (not a one-time spike)?
6 days of 5 PSI/day decline is a clear, sustained trend. Not a spike. This warrants investigation.
Do the other sensors support the AI's suggested cause?
Temperature and vibration are both normal. Pump wear typically increases vibration — normal vibration makes pump wear slightly less likely and points toward fluid-side issues (filter, leak, valve).
🟠 Question 2: Does This Fit the Situation?
Was any maintenance performed on this system in the past 2 weeks?
⚠ Check the maintenance log. If a valve was partially closed during recent work and not fully reopened, that alone would explain this pressure drop without any pump wear.
Has the hydraulic filter been changed on schedule?
Check the filter change log. A clogged hydraulic filter causes exactly this gradual pressure decline — and is far more common than pump wear. Filter check should happen before pump inspection.
Has hydraulic fluid level been checked recently?
Low hydraulic fluid causes pressure drops. Simple to check visually in under 2 minutes. Should be the first physical check — before any disassembly.
🟣 Question 3: Does This Make Sense?
Does the AI's diagnosis (pump wear) logically explain ALL the sensor data?
⚠ Normal vibration is inconsistent with significant pump wear. Worn pump impellers almost always increase vibration signature. This discrepancy weakens the pump wear hypothesis — explore simpler causes first.
Is the AI's confidence level (71%) appropriate to the action suggested?
71% is moderate confidence — the AI is saying it might be pump wear. This is not a "replace the pump" instruction. It's a "investigate the pressure system" prompt. Pump replacement should only follow confirmed diagnosis.
Have you identified simpler causes that should be ruled out first?
Check fluid level (2 min), inspect hydraulic filter (10 min), verify all valves fully open (5 min). These are faster and cheaper than pump inspection — eliminate them first.

The pattern this checklist reveals: the AI's alert is real and worth investigating — but its suggested cause (pump wear) is inconsistent with the normal vibration reading. The right first actions are simple physical checks (fluid level, filter, valves) before any mechanical disassembly. The checklist doesn't dismiss the AI — it focuses the investigation.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Every AI alert validation starts with three questions: Does this match the data? Does this fit the situation? Does this make sense? Together they prevent both over-trust and under-trust.

Cross-referencing sensors is critical. If the AI suggests pump wear but vibration is normal, that contradiction weakens the hypothesis. All sensors must tell a consistent story before you act on a diagnosis.

Always check simpler causes first. Fluid levels, filters, valve positions, and recent maintenance can explain many anomalies in minutes — before any disassembly. Eliminate cheap explanations before pursuing expensive ones.

If the AI's suggested cause doesn't explain all the abnormal readings, keep investigating. A correct alert with a wrong suggested cause still means something is wrong — don't close it just because one hypothesis didn't pan out.
