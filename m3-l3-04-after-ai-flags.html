What Happens After AI Flags a Defect?

The slides say "AI flags issues, humans confirm and decide" — but the decision process itself is a black box. This article opens that box: rework, scrap, pass with deviation, escalate to engineering — what each means, when each applies, and who makes the call.

The AI Flag Is the Beginning, Not the End

When an AI QC system flags a unit, it hasn't made a final decision. It's made a recommendation — a high-confidence signal that something may be wrong. What happens next is entirely a human process: reviewing the evidence, applying judgment, weighing options, and making a documented decision that someone takes responsibility for.

This matters because the consequences of that decision are real — scrapping a good part wastes money, passing a bad part may harm someone, and the wrong choice can affect hundreds of downstream units if the defect has a systemic cause.

The AI has no idea what the defect costs to fix, whether this customer would care, whether a recall would follow, or what the supervisor will say. The human does. That's why humans decide.

The Four Disposition Options Explained

Every QC inspection system uses some version of four core disposition options. Understanding what each means — and when each applies — is essential knowledge for anyone working in a manufacturing quality role.

Option 1

🔧 Rework

The defect can be fixed. The unit is set aside, corrected (sanded, recoated, adjusted, re-soldered, relabeled), and re-inspected before returning to the line. Rework adds cost and time — and must be carefully tracked so reworked units don't get confused with original-pass units. Rework is only appropriate when the defect is fixable without compromising the product's integrity.

Option 2

🗑️ Scrap

The defect cannot be fixed, or fixing it would cost more than making a new unit. The part is destroyed or recycled. Scrap is always a cost — material, labor, and time are lost. High scrap rates signal a systemic production problem that needs investigation. Tracking scrap by defect type and time period is how QC teams find patterns worth fixing.

Option 3

✅ Pass with Deviation (Use As Is)

The defect is real but a qualified engineer determines it doesn't affect function or safety in this application — documented as a formal deviation and approved for release. This disposition requires written justification and sign-off by an authorized engineer. It is NOT the same as ignoring the defect — it is a documented, justified, accountable decision that the defect falls within acceptable limits for this specific context.

Option 4

🔴 Escalate to Engineering

The defect is ambiguous, unusual, potentially systemic, or beyond the QC inspector's authority to disposition. Engineering review is triggered. The unit is placed on hold pending investigation. This disposition is appropriate when: the defect type hasn't been seen before, multiple units show the same defect pattern, the defect might indicate a process problem affecting other batches, or the safety implications are unclear.

Who Has Authority to Make Each Decision?

QC dispositions aren't made by just anyone — authority levels are defined by the severity of the decision. In most manufacturing facilities, a clear chain of escalation exists:

👷

Line Inspector / QC Technician

Authorized to make routine pass/fail and straightforward rework decisions. Can escalate to supervisor for borderline cases. Cannot approve "pass with deviation" on their own. First human in the post-AI decision chain.

📋

QC Supervisor / Team Lead

Can approve rework procedures, authorize scrap, and handle moderately ambiguous cases. Has broader context about production patterns and customer requirements. Escalates structurally significant or safety-relevant defects upward.

🔬

Quality Engineer

Can approve "use as is" (pass with deviation) after engineering analysis. Has technical authority to determine whether a defect affects product function or safety. Owns the formal deviation documentation and justification. In regulated industries, this sign-off may require additional review.

🏢

Quality Manager / Director

Has authority over systemic quality decisions — whether to stop the line, initiate a full batch recall, notify a customer of a quality escape, or trigger a supplier corrective action. Accountable for the facility's overall quality performance to leadership and regulators.

AI gives every level of this chain better information — faster. But the accountability at each level remains human. The AI doesn't sign its name to the disposition record. The engineer does.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

The four disposition options after an AI flag are: Rework (fix it), Scrap (destroy it), Pass with Deviation (document and justify releasing it), and Escalate to Engineering (investigate further). Each has defined conditions for when it's appropriate.

Authority levels define who can make each disposition decision. Technicians handle routine cases; supervisors handle ambiguous ones; engineers authorize pass-with-deviation; managers handle systemic or safety-critical decisions. The AI flag starts the chain — humans complete it at the appropriate level.

Patterns in AI flags are as important as individual flags. Multiple consecutive flags on the same batch suggest a systemic issue requiring engineering investigation — not just individual unit decisions. AI data analysis that reveals these patterns is one of the most valuable aspects of continuous AI QC monitoring.
