Your First 90 Days Working Alongside AI in a Manufacturing Facility

Starting a job in a facility that uses AI tools is exciting and disorienting in equal measure. This article tells you what to actually expect in the first three months — the learning curve, how to build trust with your supervisor, how to raise concerns about AI recommendations professionally, and how to document your observations so they contribute to the facility's improvement.

What Nobody Tells You Before You Start

Manufacturing facilities using AI tools are not the seamless, all-knowing operations they can appear from the outside. The AI scheduling system has calibration gaps. The predictive maintenance alerts sometimes fire on sensor glitches. The inventory dashboards are sometimes days out of sync. Workers who've been there for two years know all of this — and they've developed good instincts about when to trust the system and when to verify.

You won't have those instincts on Day 1. That's normal. The goal of your first 90 days isn't to become the AI systems expert. It's to learn the specific rhythms of your facility's AI tools — where they're reliable, where they need verification, and how the experienced people around you work with them.

Your most important asset in the first 90 days isn't what you learned in training. It's curiosity about why this specific facility does things the way it does — and the discipline to observe before you form opinions.

A 90-Day Roadmap

1
Days 1–14: Orientation
Learn the tools before you judge them
Spend the first two weeks understanding the specific AI tools your facility uses: what dashboards exist, what they display, who acts on what alerts, and what the normal response process looks like. Ask your supervisor or a senior colleague to walk you through a typical alert and what they do with it. Don't form opinions about whether the system is good or bad yet. Just observe how experienced people use it.
Goals: Understand the names and purposes of every AI tool you'll interact with. Know where to look when an alert fires. Know who to tell if something seems wrong.
2
Days 15–45: Active Observation
Start forming your baseline — and your questions
By week three, you'll start developing a feel for what's normal in your area. The machine that runs a little warm in the afternoon. The AI scheduling alert that fires every Monday and gets cleared immediately because it reflects a known constraint the system doesn't account for. The inventory metric that's always slightly off because of an informal material-pull process. Start writing these observations down. This is your facility knowledge forming.
Goals: Build a personal log of observations — what you've noticed, what questions it raises. Identify two or three things about the AI tools in your area that experienced workers know but aren't formally documented.
3
Days 46–90: Contribution
Start surfacing your observations constructively
After 45 days, you have enough context to distinguish "this looks strange because I'm new" from "this looks strange and I've now seen it consistently." Start surfacing the second category to your supervisor — framed as questions, not complaints. "I've noticed X three times now — is that a known issue or something worth looking into?" Document your contributions. This is where your floor knowledge starts becoming organizational value.
Goals: Raise at least one observation constructively through proper channels. Document the response. Have a 90-day check-in with your supervisor where you can discuss what you've learned and what you're still figuring out.

How to Raise a Concern About an AI Recommendation

One of the most professionally important skills in an AI-assisted manufacturing role is knowing how to raise a concern about an AI recommendation without sounding reactive, obstructionist, or like you're just resistant to the technology. Here's how to do it well.

📋
Describe what you observed specifically
"The AI scheduled Jordan on the aerospace welding job. Jordan certified three weeks ago and I'm not sure they're ready for this level of complexity" is specific, factual, and actionable. "I don't think the AI is right" is none of those things. Specific observations get addressed. Vague concerns get dismissed.
❓
Frame it as a question before a statement
"Is it normal for the system to assign a three-week-certified welder to this job type?" is more effective than "the system is wrong about this assignment." The question form invites your supervisor to share context you might not have — and if they agree it's a problem, they own the solution.
📝
Document the concern and the response
Write down what you observed, when you raised it, who you raised it with, and what response you received. If you're told "that's fine, the system accounts for it," note that. If the concern was valid and a change was made, note that too. Your documentation is your professional record — and it contributes to organizational learning.
✅
Know your escalation path
If you raise a concern to your supervisor and feel it wasn't adequately addressed, and you genuinely believe there's a quality or safety risk, know the next step: team lead, production manager, quality team, or safety officer depending on the nature of the concern. Raising a concern once and accepting an inadequate response is not the same as raising it properly.

How to Build Trust in the First 90 Days

Trust in manufacturing is built the same way it's always been: by being reliable, being honest, and being useful to the people around you. AI tools don't change this equation — they add a dimension to it. Here are the behaviors that build trust fastest in AI-assisted manufacturing environments:

⏰
Be the person who follows the process even when the AI seems to have it covered
New workers who check their work order against the AI-generated assignment before starting, rather than assuming the assignment is correct, build a reputation for diligence. Workers who trust the system completely and get surprised when it's wrong build a reputation for inattention. The habits you build in week one tend to stick.
💬
Ask more questions than you give opinions
In the first 90 days, "why does this work this way?" is almost always more valuable than "this should work differently." You'll form opinions. Hold them loosely until you have enough context to know whether they're based on real understanding or just unfamiliarity.
🤖
Don't anthropomorphize the AI
AI tools in manufacturing are not trying to help you or trying to trick you. They're optimization algorithms producing outputs based on their training data and configured parameters. Saying "the AI decided" or "the system wants" creates a mental model that makes you less effective at evaluating outputs critically. The tool made a calculation. You evaluate it.

What You Learned

✓
The first 90 days is for learning how this specific facility's AI tools work in practice — not forming opinions about them. Every facility has calibration gaps, known quirks, and informal knowledge about where the system is reliable and where it needs verification.
✓
Raising concerns effectively requires specificity: what exactly did you observe, when, and why does it seem like it might be a problem? Vague concerns get dismissed. Specific, documented observations get addressed.
✓
Trust is built by being reliable, honest, and useful — and by following the process even when the AI seems to have it covered. The habits formed in the first 90 days tend to define your professional reputation at that facility.
