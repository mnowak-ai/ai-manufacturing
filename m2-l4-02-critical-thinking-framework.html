How to Think Critically About AI Output: A Framework for Manufacturing Workers

This is the module's central skill — the one that determines whether AI makes you more effective or gets you into trouble. Five questions. Applied consistently. Every time an AI system gives you a recommendation.

Why a Framework Matters

Critical thinking about AI output isn't something that comes naturally — because AI systems are designed to look authoritative. Confidence scores, clean interfaces, specific numbers — all of it signals "I know what I'm doing." But confidence in appearance is not the same as accuracy in fact.

A framework gives you a repeatable process. Instead of asking "does this feel right?" — which is unreliable — you ask specific questions in a specific order. The goal isn't to distrust AI. It's to be a more intelligent user of AI by knowing exactly what to validate before acting on it.

You don't need to understand how AI works to use it well. You need to know what questions to ask about what it tells you.

The Five-Question Framework

1
What data is the AI actually seeing?
AI systems analyze the data they have access to — and only that data. Before trusting a recommendation, you need to know what inputs the AI used. Sensor data? Work order history? This machine specifically, or a general model? You can't evaluate a recommendation without knowing what it's based on.
Ask Yourself
Which sensors is this AI reading? Are they all calibrated and clean?
Is this a model trained on this machine, this machine type, or all machines?
How recent is the training data? Is this machine operating in conditions similar to what the AI was trained on?
2
What could the AI be missing?
AI sees what sensors report. It cannot see what sensors don't measure: mechanical condition of fixtures, quality of incoming raw material, recent procedural changes, unusual ambient conditions, or information that workers know but haven't entered into a system. The gap between sensor data and physical reality is where AI blind spots live.
Ask Yourself
Is there anything relevant that happened recently that the AI wouldn't know about? (New supplier batch, tooling change, process modification)
What physical aspects of this machine can't be sensed? (Fixture wear, coolant contamination, chip buildup)
Do I know something about this machine that isn't in the data?
3
Does the recommendation match the physical evidence?
The AI's suggested cause should produce a pattern that matches what you're actually observing. Calibration drift produces progressive errors. A loose fixture produces intermittent errors. Bearing wear produces vibration and heat together. If the physical evidence pattern doesn't match what the AI's suggested cause would produce — that's a logical inconsistency worth investigating before acting.
Ask Yourself
What pattern would I expect to see if the AI's suggested cause were correct?
Does what I'm actually observing match that expected pattern?
Can I think of another cause that would better explain the observed pattern?
4
What are the stakes if the AI is wrong?
The depth of investigation required should scale with the consequences of a wrong action. A 65% confident recommendation to adjust a lubricant interval on a low-priority machine is different from a 65% confident recommendation to continue production on an aerospace component. Same confidence. Completely different investigation requirements. Stakes determine how much verification is appropriate.
Ask Yourself
What happens if I follow this recommendation and the AI is wrong?
Who is downstream of this decision — what customers, products, or safety systems are affected?
Is this reversible? Can we course-correct easily if the recommendation is wrong?
5
What would an experienced technician check first?
Your own expertise and the expertise of your senior colleagues is a validation layer that no AI system can replace. Before acting on an AI recommendation, ask: what would the most experienced technician on your team physically look at first? That question often identifies the fastest path to physical validation — and may surface considerations the AI never had access to.
Ask Yourself
Have I or a senior colleague seen a similar situation before? What was the real cause then?
What physical check would take 5 minutes that could confirm or contradict this recommendation?
If I were mentoring a new technician, what would I tell them to look at first?

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

The five-question framework: What data does the AI see? What could it be missing? Does the recommendation match the evidence? What are the stakes? What would an expert check first? Applied consistently, these questions make you a significantly more effective user of AI recommendations.

Validation depth scales with confidence and stakes — not one-size-fits-all. A quick physical check at 91% confidence is appropriate. A full investigation at 58% confidence is appropriate. Stakes can override confidence: high-stakes situations always require more verification regardless of the AI's confidence level.

Pattern mismatch is a red flag at any confidence level. If the physical evidence doesn't match what the AI's suggested cause would produce, investigate before acting — regardless of the confidence score.
