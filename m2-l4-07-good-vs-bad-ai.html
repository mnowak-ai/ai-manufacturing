What Good AI Looks Like vs. Bad AI: How to Evaluate the Tools Your Employer Gives You

When you start a manufacturing job, the AI tools are already there. You don't choose them. But you can evaluate them — and knowing what separates a trustworthy AI system from a poorly designed one makes you a more effective, safer worker.

You're an Evaluator, Not Just a User

Most workers think of AI tools the way they think of a coffee machine: it's there, you use it, you don't think about whether it's the right machine. But AI tools influence decisions that affect safety, quality, and your career. Understanding what good AI looks like in manufacturing — and being able to recognize a poorly designed system — is part of being a skilled manufacturing professional in 2025.

Knowing what makes an AI system trustworthy is as important as knowing how to use it. You inherit the tools your employer provides — but you don't have to be uncritical of them.

Good AI vs. Bad AI: A Side-by-Side Comparison

Dimension
✅ Good AI System
❌ Red Flag
Confidence scores
✓ Shows confidence scores with all recommendations. Treats low confidence as a signal to investigate, not to act.
✗ Presents recommendations without confidence information, or always shows high confidence.
Transparency
✓ Can explain what data the recommendation is based on. Tells you which sensors and what history informed the output.
✗ Black box — outputs recommendations with no ability to see what inputs drove them.
Override capability
✓ Allows workers to document and submit overrides. Treats overrides as learning data, not system failures.
✗ Has no formal override mechanism, or discourages overrides through workflow design.
Accuracy tracking
✓ Facility tracks AI accuracy over time. You can find out: "How accurate has this system been on this machine type?" Results are shared with workers.
✗ No one knows the AI's actual accuracy rate. Accuracy is not tracked or not disclosed to workers using the system.
Training data relevance
✓ System was trained on data from your facility, your machine types, your process conditions. Or vendor can explain the training data source and how it relates to your operation.
✗ System is a generic model with no customization for your facility. Vendor can't explain what it was trained on.
Error handling
✓ Clear process for what happens when the AI is wrong — who to report it to, how it's investigated, how it feeds retraining.
✗ No formal error reporting process. AI errors are handled informally or ignored. No feedback loop to improve the system.
Worker training
✓ Workers received documented training before using the system — what each alert type means, how confidence scores work, how to escalate.
✗ No formal training. Workers figure out the system on the job. Alert codes are unexplained.

Questions to Ask About the AI System You're Using

"What was this AI trained on — and is that training data relevant to our facility?"
A model trained on a different industry, machine type, or production environment may produce unreliable recommendations for your facility's specific conditions. Asking this question identifies whether the AI was customized for your context or applied generically.
⚠️ If no one can answer this, the training data source is unknown — a significant quality concern.
"How is this AI's accuracy tracked, and what is the current accuracy rate?"
A well-managed AI system tracks how often its recommendations are confirmed vs. overridden, and what percentage of alerts turn out to be real problems. If the facility doesn't track this, there's no way to know whether the AI is performing well or poorly.
⚠️ "We trust it, it seems to work" is not the same as "we track accuracy and it's 82% confirmed."
"What happens when the AI is wrong? Is there a formal error reporting process?"
Good AI systems have a mechanism for workers to report AI errors, which feeds back into model improvement. If there's no formal process, wrong AI recommendations disappear without improving the system.
⚠️ "Just tell your supervisor" without a documented follow-through process means AI errors aren't systematically captured.
"Can I see what data the AI used to make this recommendation?"
Trustworthy AI systems can show you which sensors, what time window, and what historical comparisons informed a specific recommendation. Black-box systems that give recommendations without any ability to see the inputs make validation impossible.
⚠️ A recommendation you can't trace to its inputs is a recommendation you can't evaluate critically.

Red Flags That Warrant Concern and Escalation

🚩
The AI always shows high confidence, even for unusual situations
A well-calibrated AI should show lower confidence when the situation is ambiguous or outside its training distribution. An AI that always shows 85–95% confidence is either poorly calibrated or hiding uncertainty — both of which make it harder for workers to know when to be more cautious.
🚩
Workers who override AI recommendations face pushback or discipline
Overrides are how AI systems get better and how human judgment protects against AI errors. A workplace culture that treats overrides as failures — rather than as professional judgment exercised appropriately — is an automation bias factory.
🚩
No one in the facility knows the AI's accuracy rate
If supervisors, coordinators, and technicians all say "I don't know how accurate it is," the system is being used without the accountability metrics that would allow the organization to know whether it's helping or hurting. This is a management failure, not a technology failure.
🚩
The AI system was deployed without formal worker training
As documented in the Poor AI Integration article, workers who don't understand what their AI tools mean and can't distinguish real anomalies from false alarms will develop informal coping strategies that may be more dangerous than no AI at all.
🧠 Knowledge Check
A new technician asks their supervisor "What was the AI system trained on?" and is told "I don't know — the vendor set it up." What does this tell the technician about how to use the system?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Good AI systems show confidence scores, explain their inputs, have formal override mechanisms, track accuracy, were trained on relevant data, have formal error reporting processes, and include worker training. These are not luxury features — they're what separates trustworthy from untrustworthy systems.

Four questions every manufacturing worker should ask about their AI tools: What was it trained on? What is its accuracy rate? What happens when it's wrong? Can I see what data it used? The answers reveal how much accountability exists around the system.

Red flags worth escalating: AI that always shows high confidence, culture where overrides are discouraged, no one knows the accuracy rate, deployment without formal training. These are management accountability issues, not just technology quirks.
