Lesson 1.1 — AI Overview in Manufacturing

What Is the Internet of Things (IoT) and How Does It Connect to AI in Manufacturing?

AI needs data to work. IoT is the system that collects it. Understanding how these two technologies connect will help you make sense of everything happening on the modern shop floor.

You've probably heard the phrase "Internet of Things" — maybe in a news article, maybe in a tech ad, maybe in this course. It sounds abstract. The concept is actually very concrete.

The Internet of Things just means: physical objects connected to the internet, reporting information automatically.

Your smartwatch tracks your heart rate and sends it to your phone. Your car monitors tire pressure and alerts you on the dashboard. Your home thermostat learns your schedule and adjusts the temperature before you get home. All of these are IoT devices.

In manufacturing, the same idea applies — except instead of your heart rate, it's a motor's temperature. Instead of tire pressure, it's a hydraulic line's PSI. And instead of adjusting the heat at home, it's AI deciding whether to schedule maintenance or keep the line running.

That's exactly what IoT does in a factory. Except the stakes are much higher than whether your front porch light is on.

IoT in Manufacturing: The IIoT

Key Term
IIoT (Industrial Internet of Things): The application of IoT specifically to industrial settings — factories, power plants, oil refineries, logistics operations. Sometimes just called "connected manufacturing." The machines, sensors, and devices on the shop floor are wired or wirelessly connected, continuously reporting their status to systems that track, analyze, and act on that data.

IIoT differs from consumer IoT in one critical way: the consequences of a failure are much bigger. If your smart thermostat goes offline, your house gets a bit warm. If an IIoT sensor on a $4 million turbine goes offline, a factory could lose hundreds of thousands of dollars of production — or have a safety incident.

How Does IoT Data Flow Through a Factory?

Data doesn't teleport from a sensor on a machine straight to an AI system. It travels through layers — each with a specific job. Understanding these layers helps explain why a factory's "connected" system is actually a complex infrastructure, not just a bunch of wires.

Layer 1: Devices
The Physical Things with Sensors
Machines, motors, conveyors, valves, tanks, cameras, robots — anything on the shop floor with a sensor attached. These devices collect raw data: temperature readings, vibration measurements, pressure levels, image captures, speed counts.
Example: A conveyor belt motor has a temperature sensor and a vibration sensor. Every 500 milliseconds, both take a reading.
↓
Layer 2: Connectivity
The Wires, Wi-Fi, and Protocols That Carry the Data
Raw sensor readings need to travel somewhere. This layer is the network — industrial Wi-Fi, wired ethernet, Bluetooth, or specialized protocols like MQTT that are designed for high-speed, reliable data transmission in harsh industrial environments.
Example: The conveyor's sensor readings are transmitted every half-second over the factory's industrial Wi-Fi network to a central hub.
↓
Layer 3: Processing
Where Data Gets Organized and Analyzed
Raw data is noisy and massive. This layer — often called "edge computing" or cloud processing — cleans the data, organizes it, and runs AI analysis on it. Pattern detection, anomaly alerts, and predictive models live here. This is where AI actually runs.
Example: The AI system analyzes 90 days of the conveyor's temperature readings and detects that the pattern this morning looks like what happened 3 days before the last bearing failure.
↓
Layer 4: Action
What Happens as a Result
The processed data becomes something actionable — an alert on a supervisor's screen, an automatic maintenance work order, a line slowdown, or a report. This is the layer workers interact with directly. The dashboard, the alert, the text message.
Example: A maintenance work order is automatically generated and sent to the technician's tablet: "Conveyor 4B — bearing inspection recommended within 72 hours."

Key Term
OEE (Overall Equipment Effectiveness): A single score that measures how well a machine or production line is actually performing compared to its full potential. It factors in availability (is it running?), performance (how fast?), and quality (how many good parts?). World-class manufacturing targets 85% OEE or higher. IoT makes it possible to calculate OEE in real time, not just at the end of a shift.

Why IoT Is the Foundation AI Requires

Here's the critical connection that's easy to miss: AI in manufacturing is only as good as the IoT system feeding it.

AI needs data to think. IoT is what collects that data, continuously, from everywhere in the factory. Without IoT, AI has nothing to work with.

Consider what each AI capability requires from IoT infrastructure:

🔮
Predictive Maintenance
Requires sensors on every critical machine, transmitting data frequently enough for AI to detect pattern changes days before failure. No IoT = no continuous data = no prediction. You're back to reactive repairs.
🔍
Quality Control AI
Requires cameras and imaging sensors capturing every part at every stage. The images travel over the network to an AI vision system that checks them in milliseconds. No IIoT network = images can't get there fast enough to stop bad parts in real time.
💻
Digital Twins
A digital twin is only accurate if it's receiving real-time data from the real machine. That data travels via the IoT network. Without that connection, the twin is just a static diagram — not a live model you can trust for predictions.
📊
Real-Time Dashboards
Supervisors watching live plant status only works if every machine is reporting its status in real time. That real-time reporting is entirely IoT. Pull the network, and the dashboard goes dark.
🧠 Check Your Understanding
A factory's AI system can predict equipment failures 10 days in advance. What does this AI system fundamentally depend on to make those predictions?
✅ Correct. Predictive AI can only predict because it's analyzing patterns in continuous sensor data — and continuous sensor data only exists because IoT infrastructure is collecting and transmitting it constantly. Sensors → network → AI analysis → alert. Remove any link in that chain and prediction breaks down.
❌ Not quite. Human reviews, warranty docs, and daily reports all existed before IoT — and they couldn't predict failures reliably because they were periodic, not continuous. What makes AI prediction possible is the constant stream of real-time sensor data from IoT devices. That's the foundation.

What You Learned

✓
IoT means physical objects connected to the internet, reporting data automatically — from smartwatches to factory motors.
✓
IIoT (Industrial IoT) is IoT applied to manufacturing — machines, sensors, cameras, and devices all connected and reporting continuously.
✓
Data flows through four layers: devices that collect it, networks that carry it, processing systems that analyze it, and action outputs (alerts, dashboards) that workers interact with.
✓
Every major AI capability in manufacturing — predictive maintenance, quality control, digital twins, real-time dashboards — depends on IIoT infrastructure to collect the data AI needs.
✓
IoT is the infrastructure that AI runs on. Understanding both — and how they connect — is what separates workers who use AI tools from workers who understand them.
