How to Write a Clear Inspection Report

Writing a clear inspection report is one of the most directly useful skills in any manufacturing QC role — and it's teachable. This guide covers what goes in a good report, how to describe a defect with precision, the language standards that matter, and how to review an AI draft for accuracy.

Why Report Quality Matters

An inspection report is used by more people than the person who wrote it. The QA manager reads it to make release decisions. The process engineer reads it to investigate root causes. The regulatory auditor reads it to assess compliance. If the report is vague, ambiguous, or incomplete, every one of those readers is working with less information than they need.

A clear inspection report turns a quality event into a usable record. A poor one buries the event in ambiguity — or worse, creates a record that misleads the people depending on it.

Your report is the only version of what happened that most people will ever see. Write it for someone who wasn't there and knows nothing about what you found.

Weak vs. Strong — Before and After

Here's the same inspection event written two ways. Read both carefully, then look at the annotations explaining what makes the difference.

❌Weak Report — What Not to Write
Inspected line 4 today second shift. Found some scratches on several units. Looks like a surface issue. Removed the bad ones. Everything else seemed okay. Will monitor.
❌ "Today" — no date. "Second shift" — no time. This record can't be verified or cross-referenced.
❌ "Some scratches on several units" — how many? How large? On what surface? "Some" and "several" are meaningless in a quality record.
❌ "Looks like a surface issue" — opinion without evidence. What did you actually observe?
❌ "Removed the bad ones" — which units? How many? Were they tagged, logged, held for review?
❌ "Everything else seemed okay" — "seemed" is not a quality determination. What did you check? What was the result?
❌ "Will monitor" — what specifically? At what frequency? Who is responsible?
✅Strong Report — What to Write
Date/Time: 2024-03-14, 15:42 Station: Line 4, Post-Paint Inspection (Station 7) Inspector: A. Chen, QC Technician III Product: Metformin 500mg Tablet, Batch BTH-2024-1143 Units Inspected: 847 (sequential units 44,201–45,047) Findings: Defect type: Linear surface scratches on tablet face Count: 23 units (2.72% of sample) Location: Top face, center, running parallel to imprint Dimension: Approximately 1.5–2.0 mm in length, less than 0.1 mm depth Pattern: Defects clustered on units 44,580–44,720 (consistent with tooling interval) Disposition: 23 flagged units removed and placed in quarantine bin Q-4-B, tagged with NCR reference NCR-2024-0848. Remaining 824 units passed inspection. Next step: NCR-2024-0848 opened. Compression tooling on Station 5 flagged for maintenance review before next production run. QA Supervisor notified at 15:55.
✅ Exact date, time, station, and inspector — this record can be placed precisely in the production timeline.
✅ Specific count, location, dimensions, and pattern — someone who wasn't there knows exactly what was found and where.
✅ Clear disposition with traceability — specific quarantine location and NCR reference number.
✅ Action-oriented close — who was notified, what happens next, who is responsible.

The Five Elements of Every Inspection Report

1

When and Where

Date, exact time, production line or station, shift. These four fields anchor the record in the production timeline — without them, nothing can be cross-referenced against equipment logs, batch records, or other inspections.

2024-03-14 · 15:42 · Line 4, Station 7 (Post-Paint Inspection) · Second Shift
2

What Was Inspected

Product name, batch or lot number, unit range inspected. Enough information that someone six months later can link your report to the specific product, batch, and units you examined.

Metformin 500mg Tablet · Batch BTH-2024-1143 · Units 44,201–45,047 (847 units)
3

What Was Found

Defect type, count, location, dimensions, and pattern — written in objective, measurable language. This is the core of the report. Each element (type, count, location, size) answers a different question that investigators, engineers, and auditors will ask.

Linear scratch · 23 units · Top face, center, parallel to imprint · 1.5–2.0mm length · Clustered at units 44,580–44,720
4

What Was Done

Disposition of affected units — removed, reworked, held, released with deviation. Include the specific quarantine location, NCR number opened, or release authorization. "Removed the bad ones" is never sufficient; "23 units placed in quarantine bin Q-4-B, tagged NCR-2024-0848" is.

23 units quarantined in bin Q-4-B · NCR-2024-0848 opened · 824 remaining units released to next station
5

What Happens Next

Who was notified, what follow-up action is triggered, and who is responsible for it. Reports that end without a clear next step leave investigations without a starting point and accountabilities without owners.

QA Supervisor notified 15:55 · Compression tooling Station 5 flagged for review before next run · NCR-2024-0848 owner: Process Engineering

How to Describe a Defect Precisely

The hardest part of inspection report writing is defect description. Here's a framework for each dimension of a clear defect description:

📍 Location

Use anatomical or spatial references — not "on the side" but "on the leading edge of the top face, approximately 3mm from the center imprint." Use the product's features as landmarks.

Top face · Upper-right corner · Adjacent to dosage imprint · Inner surface at weld seam

📐 Dimensions

Measure and record actual dimensions when possible — length, width, depth, diameter, area. "Small" means nothing; "2.3mm × 0.8mm, depth <0.1mm" can be compared against specifications and tracked over time.

1.5–2.0mm length · <0.1mm depth · Approximately 4mm diameter · 0.3mm wall thickness at thinnest point

🎨 Appearance

Describe what you see — color, texture, shape, orientation. Use concrete terms: "linear scratch running parallel to the imprint," not "mark on the tablet." For visual defects, describe what makes it different from a conforming unit.

Silver metallic streak · Linear, 2mm, parallel to seam · Irregular discoloration, cream to light brown · Surface depression, no sharp edges

📊 Pattern

Note whether defects are random or clustered. Clustering — specific unit ranges, specific times, specific shifts — is the single biggest clue for root cause investigation. "Random across batch" and "clustered on units 44,580–44,720" lead to completely different investigations.

Clustered units 44,580–44,720 · Random across full sample · All defects on same surface orientation · Concentrated in first 200 units of shift

Language Standards: What to Use and What to Avoid

❌ Avoid — Subjective or Vague
✅ Use Instead — Objective and Specific
"Some," "several," "a few," "many"
"23 units," "4 of 50 sampled," "3.2% of batch"
"Looks like," "seems to be," "appears"
"Measured 42.1°C," "confirmed via visual inspection," "classified as [defect type]"
"Bad," "damaged," "defective" (without description)
"Surface scratch 2mm × 0.1mm on top face," "coating void 3mm diameter"
"Today," "this morning," "earlier"
"2024-03-14 at 14:32," "second shift, 15:00–15:45"
"Removed the bad ones," "set aside"
"23 units quarantined in bin Q-4-B, tagged NCR-2024-0848"
"Will monitor," "should be fine," "probably okay"
"QA Supervisor notified. NCR opened. Next action: tooling review before next run."
"Normal," "acceptable" (without reference)
"Within specification per SOP-QC-114 (limit: ±0.5mm)" or "exceeds specification by 0.3mm"
"I think," "in my opinion," "maybe"
State what you observed, measured, or confirmed. If uncertain, write "flagged for QA review — classification pending."

How to Review an AI-Drafted Inspection Report

AI systems can generate draft inspection reports from inspection data. Here's how to review one using the principles above.

🤖 AI DRAFT — REVIEW FOR ACCURACY
INSPECTION REPORT — AI DRAFT (Pending Human Review)

Date: 2024-03-14 · Shift: Second
Station: Line 4, Post-Paint Inspection
Inspector: VISION-AI-03
Product: Metformin 500mg Tablet · Batch: BTH-2024-1143
Units inspected: 847

AI FINDINGS:
23 units flagged with surface defects (confidence: 94–97%)
Defect classification: Linear surface scratches, top face
Estimated dimensions: 1–2mm length
Distribution: Units approximately 44,500–44,750

Recommended disposition: Quarantine pending human review
AI-generated NCR reference: NCR-2024-0848 [AUTO-DRAFT]
Next action: Supervisor notification recommended
✅ Date, station, batch, and unit count confirmed correct against production log.
⚠️ "Estimated dimensions: 1–2mm length" — AI didn't capture depth measurement. Human reviewer should add: "<0.1mm depth" from physical inspection notes before finalizing.
⚠️ "Units approximately 44,500–44,750" — cross-check against inspection data. Actual range per raw data: 44,580–44,720. Tighten this range in the final report — precision matters for root cause analysis.
⚠️ "Supervisor notification recommended" — verify this actually happened, with time logged. Replace with: "QA Supervisor A. Rodriguez notified at 15:55."
✅ NCR reference number correctly generated and cross-referenced. Verify NCR-2024-0848 is actually open in the NCR system before the report is finalized.
⚠️ Missing from AI draft: specific quarantine bin location. Add: "units placed in bin Q-4-B" before approving.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Every inspection report needs five elements: When and Where (date, time, station), What Was Inspected (product, batch, unit range), What Was Found (defect type, count, location, dimensions, pattern), What Was Done (disposition with specific location and NCR reference), and What Happens Next (who was notified, what follows).

Replace vague language with specific, measurable terms: "some" → "23 units," "looks like" → "confirmed as," "set aside" → "quarantined in bin Q-4-B per NCR-2024-0848." Opinion language ("I think," "seems to be") has no place in a quality record — state what you observed and measured.

When reviewing an AI-drafted report, check specifically for: missing measurements the AI didn't capture, approximate ranges that need to be tightened from raw data, recommended actions that need to be confirmed as actually completed, and disposition information with specific locations that AI systems often omit.
