A Full Diagnostic Scenario: From Problem to Resolution

Every skill from this module — sensors, anomaly detection, validation, confidence scores, human judgment, work order documentation — comes together in one extended scenario. Work through each phase and make the calls a real technician would make.

You are a maintenance technician at Apex Stamping — a metal stamping plant producing structural brackets for the automotive industry. It's Tuesday morning, 7:12 AM. Your shift just started. You log into the monitoring dashboard and see an alert you didn't have before leaving last night.

🤖 AI Recommendation
Gradual pressure decline consistent with hydraulic filter restriction. Recommend: replace hydraulic filter (P/N HF-440) during next scheduled production break. Machine may continue operating. Monitor pressure — stop if pressure drops below 145 PSI.
Confidence: 79% · Based on: 72-hr pressure trend, pump vibration pattern, fluid temperature
🎯 Decision Point 1
Before you do anything else — what is your first response to this alert?
2
Phase 2
Apply the Framework and Investigate

You apply the five-question framework from Lesson 2.4. The data makes sense — a 72-hour gradual pressure decline with normal pump vibration and normal temperature is consistent with filter restriction. The AI's logic checks out. 79% confidence is solid. You check the validation checklist from Lesson 2.2: Does the data fit the situation? A filter at 12,840 cycles since last PM is overdue for inspection — the normal interval at this plant is 10,000 cycles.

You go to the machine. You listen — no unusual sounds from the pump. You check the hydraulic fluid visually through the sight glass — slightly darker than fresh fluid. You locate the hydraulic filter housing and put your hand on it — warm but not hot.

🎯 Decision Point 2
Your physical inspection is consistent with the AI's recommendation. What do you do now?
3
Phase 3
The Repair — and an Unexpected Finding

The 10 AM production break arrives. You apply lockout/tagout per SOP-HYD-02. You drain the hydraulic circuit to the filter housing. You remove the filter — and it's 60% blocked with dark sludge. Consistent with the AI's prediction. So far, AI was right.

But here's where your eyes matter: you notice the filter bypass valve housing has a hairline crack — a small fracture in the aluminum casting that would not be detectable by any sensor. If this valve cracks through during operation, it could create an uncontrolled hydraulic pressure bypass that causes an unexpected ram movement. This is a safety issue that the AI could not have flagged, because no sensor measures housing integrity.

🎯 Decision Point 3
You found the cracked bypass valve housing. The original work order is for filter replacement only. What do you do?
4
Phase 4
Resolution and Documentation

Your supervisor arrives within 8 minutes. Maintenance engineering reviews the cracked housing — it's a critical finding; the crack had propagated 60% through the casting wall and would likely have failed within 2–3 days under operating pressure. A full hydraulic bypass failure on Press #3 during operation could have caused an unexpected ram stroke with workers in the die area. Engineering authorizes replacement of both the filter and the bypass valve housing. Parts are pulled from stock. The full repair takes 2 hours and 20 minutes. Machine restarts at 12:42 PM.

Now you close out the work order — and this documentation is some of the most important work you'll do today.

✅ Work Order WO-5519 — Closeout Documentation
AI Recommendation
AI flagged hydraulic pressure decline at 79% confidence — recommended filter replacement. Recommendation validated by physical inspection and confirmed correct.
AI Accuracy
Confirmed: filter was 60% blocked — AI diagnosis was accurate. Original recommendation was appropriate given available data.
Additional Finding (Human)
Physical inspection during repair identified hairline crack in bypass valve housing (P/N BVH-220). Crack estimated 60% through casting wall — safety escalation required. AI had no sensor data that could detect housing integrity. FINDING WOULD NOT HAVE BEEN CAPTURED BY AI MONITORING ALONE.
Action Taken
HF-440 filter replaced. BVH-220 bypass valve housing replaced per engineering authorization. Full lockout/tagout maintained throughout. All connections torqued to spec per SOP-HYD-02.
Post-Repair Readings
System pressure: 174 PSI ✓ (normal 165–185). Fluid temperature: 49°C ✓. Pump vibration: 0.8 mm/s ✓. Machine returned to production 12:42 PM.
Recommendation for Next PM
Recommend engineering inspect ALL bypass valve housings on hydraulic presses at next scheduled PM — same casting batch may have similar defects. Log filter replacement interval: reset at 10,000 cycles.

That last recommendation — to inspect all bypass valve housings on all hydraulic presses — is what turns a single repair into an organizational safety improvement. It's also how human observation prevents problems the AI cannot predict.

What This Scenario Demonstrates

Lesson 2.1
Sensor reading interpretation
You read the pressure trend, pump vibration, and fluid temperature data to understand the machine's condition — not just the alert headline.
Lesson 2.2
Validation before action
You applied the validation checklist and 5-question framework before creating the work order — confirming AI recommendation matched physical evidence.
Lesson 2.2
Confidence score interpretation
79% confidence was treated as "solid starting hypothesis" — not certainty. Physical inspection was still required before proceeding.
Lesson 2.3
Work order creation and documentation
You created a complete work order, escalated appropriately when scope expanded, and documented everything including the AI accuracy and the unexpected finding.
Lesson 2.4
Human-in-the-loop value
The cracked housing — invisible to every sensor — was found by physical inspection during the repair. This is the irreplaceable value of skilled human eyes on equipment.
Lesson 2.4
Safety override and escalation
When the unexpected safety finding appeared, you stopped, escalated, and kept the machine locked out — not because the original work order said to, but because safety takes precedence.
