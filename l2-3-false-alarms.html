Lesson 1.2 — Safety & AI

When AI Gets Safety Wrong: False Alarms, Missed Hazards, and Why Human Oversight Still Matters

AI safety tools are powerful — but they fail in specific, predictable ways. Every worker who relies on them needs to understand exactly how.

There's a comforting idea that tends to follow any powerful new technology: Now that we have this, we're covered.

AI safety systems are no exception. A company installs computer vision monitoring, sees impressive early results, and management starts to think of safety as a solved problem. Workers start to assume the AI will catch anything important. Supervisors rely on the alert feed instead of walking the floor.

This is a mistake — and it can be a deadly one.

AI safety tools fail in three specific, documented ways. Understanding each one isn't pessimism. It's the knowledge that makes you a safer, smarter worker when these systems are part of your environment.

The Three Ways AI Safety Systems Fail

⚠️ Failure Mode 1
False Alarms — The Boy Who Cried Wolf
A false alarm happens when the AI flags something as a safety hazard that isn't actually dangerous. A shadow that looks like a person in a restricted zone. A worker in an approved posture the AI wasn't trained on. A hard hat that the AI can't detect because of lighting conditions — so it flags "no hard hat" when the worker is actually compliant.

One false alarm is annoying. Ten per shift is disruptive. Fifty per day is catastrophic — because humans do something predictable and rational when they receive too many false signals: they stop paying attention to them. This phenomenon is so well-documented in safety science that it has a name.
Real Example
A food manufacturing plant deploys an AI system to detect workers without hairnets. In the first week, the system generates 200+ alerts per day — including flagging workers who are wearing properly fitted hairnets that appear loose to the camera at certain angles. Supervisors start dismissing alerts without reviewing them. Three weeks later, a real hairnet compliance issue in a high-risk zone goes unaddressed because no one is reviewing the feed anymore.
🚨 Failure Mode 2
Missed Hazards — What the AI Wasn't Trained to See
Every AI safety system was trained on a specific set of examples. It can only reliably detect what it was trained to detect. A system trained to spot workers without hard hats won't detect a floor that's gradually developing a dangerous oil slick. A system trained on standard forklift behavior won't flag an unusual piece of equipment being used for a temporary task.

This is called an out-of-distribution problem: the AI encounters a situation it has never seen before, and it has no way to flag it as dangerous because it doesn't recognize it as anything meaningful. Worse, workers may assume that because no alert fired, everything is fine.
Real Example
A factory's AI vision system is trained to detect the standard forklifts used in the facility. A contractor brings in a different type of powered industrial vehicle — a telescopic handler — for a one-week project. The AI doesn't recognize it as a forklift, so it doesn't apply forklift-pedestrian proximity detection rules. A near-miss occurs near a loading dock that the AI logged as uneventful.
💤 Failure Mode 3
Overtrust — When AI Replaces Human Attention Instead of Augmenting It
Overtrust is subtler than a false alarm or a missed hazard. It happens when people — supervisors, workers, safety managers — adjust their behavior based on the presence of AI in a way that reduces their own vigilance. If you know cameras are watching, you might walk the floor less. If you trust the alert system, you might stop developing the personal instinct to notice when something feels off.

This creates a dangerous dependency: the safety of the workplace becomes contingent on the AI functioning correctly. When the system has a technical glitch, goes offline for maintenance, or encounters a novel situation, there's no backup — because the humans who used to provide that backup have been replaced by comfortable assumption.
Real Example
A plant manager reduces supervisor safety walk frequency from twice per shift to once after deploying AI monitoring, reasoning that the cameras provide continuous coverage. Six months later, the AI system's server connection drops during a Saturday morning shift — a gap that isn't noticed for four hours. During that window, two safety incidents occur that would have been caught by a supervisor on the floor.

Alert Fatigue: See It Happen in Real Time

Key Term
Alert fatigue: A well-documented phenomenon where people who receive too many alerts — real or false — begin to ignore them habitually. Originally documented in hospital intensive care units where equipment alarms fire constantly. Now increasingly recognized in AI safety systems. Alert fatigue turns a safety tool into background noise.

What Happens When a Real Hazard Looks "Normal" to the AI?

AI systems learn what "normal" looks like from the data they were trained on. If an unusual hazard is similar enough to normal operations, the AI won't flag it.

Consider these scenarios where a real danger would be invisible to most current AI safety systems:

🌡️
A machine running 20°F hotter than normal — no visual sign
Camera-based AI sees nothing unusual. Only a temperature sensor (and a human who knows the normal range) would catch this early warning sign.
💧
A slow hydraulic fluid leak pooling under equipment
A thin layer of fluid on a dark floor may not trigger a vision-based slip hazard detector. An experienced worker walking the floor would smell or see it immediately.
🔊
A new sound from a machine — a slight grinding or whining
Sound-based AI exists but isn't universally deployed. Camera systems are blind to audio cues. A veteran operator knows immediately that sound shouldn't be there.
😤
A worker behaving unusually — not violating a rule, but clearly impaired
AI can detect some fatigue indicators, but not a worker who's upset, distracted by a personal crisis, or showing early signs of heat exhaustion. A human coworker notices these things.

The things AI misses are often the things only a human can catch — because they require context, intuition, and the kind of knowledge that comes from working the same floor for years.

What Good Human Oversight Actually Looks Like

The lesson isn't "don't trust AI safety tools." It's "use them with clear eyes about what they do and don't do." Here's what maintaining proper human oversight looks like in practice:

🚶
Keep Walking the Floor
Safety walks shouldn't decrease because AI is monitoring. They serve a different purpose — picking up on things the AI can't detect: sounds, smells, human behavior, informal conversations about problems workers haven't formally reported.
📊
Review Alert Quality, Not Just Volume
Track the ratio of real hazards to false alarms. If the false alarm rate is high, the system needs retraining or threshold adjustment — not acceptance. A supervisor who ignores 40 alerts per day to find 2 real ones has a false alarm problem, not an alert problem.
🧠
Maintain Your Own Hazard Recognition Skills
Workers who only rely on AI alerts lose the ability to independently spot hazards. Continuing to actively observe your environment — not just waiting for an alert — keeps those skills sharp for when the AI isn't there.
❓
Know What the AI Can't See
Learn what sensors and cameras cover your area — and what they don't. If the camera covers a loading dock but not a maintenance bay, you know the maintenance bay requires extra human attention. Gaps in coverage aren't failures — they're known quantities to manage.
🔕
Report False Alarms — Don't Just Dismiss Them
Every false alarm you log gives the safety team data to improve the system. Silently ignoring false alerts creates no feedback for improvement. Documenting them helps retrain the model toward better accuracy over time.
🤝
Keep Each Other Safe — Not Just the Algorithm
The video lesson said it directly: "people keep each other safe." AI is a tool, not a culture. A workforce that looks out for one another — speaks up when something seems wrong, helps a coworker who seems off — provides a layer of protection no algorithm can replicate.

What You Learned

✓
AI safety systems fail in three specific ways: false alarms (flagging things that aren't dangerous), missed hazards (not recognizing dangers outside their training), and overtrust (workers reducing their own vigilance because they assume the AI is covering them).
✓
Alert fatigue is a documented, serious problem — when false alarm rates are high, workers and supervisors habituate to dismissing alerts, including real ones.
✓
AI systems can only detect what they were trained to see. Sounds, smells, unusual human behavior, and novel equipment are examples of hazards that fall outside most current AI safety systems' detection capabilities.
✓
Good human oversight means continuing to walk the floor, maintaining personal hazard recognition skills, knowing what the AI can't see, and reporting false alarms rather than silently dismissing them.
✓
AI is a tool, not a culture. The most important safety layer in any facility is still a workforce that looks out for each other.
