Module 3 · Lesson 3.2 — Compliance Checks

Who Makes the Rules?

A beginner's guide to manufacturing regulatory bodies — who they are, what they govern, what happens when factories ignore them, and why AI compliance monitoring is built around the rules they write.

Two Types of Rule-Makers

Manufacturing compliance rules come from two fundamentally different types of organizations, and understanding the difference matters for how you interpret compliance requirements on the job.

Government agencies write legally binding regulations. Violating them means violating the law — with fines, shutdowns, recalls, and criminal liability as potential consequences. Companies don't choose whether to comply; they're required to by law.

Standards organizations write voluntary standards — frameworks for how things should be done. Technically no law requires ISO 9001 certification. But practically, many industries, customers, and regulators treat these standards as de facto requirements. A company that isn't ISO 9001 certified often can't win contracts with major manufacturers. The "voluntary" nature is largely theoretical.

Government agencies write the floor — the legal minimum. Standards organizations write the ceiling — the professional best practice. Serious manufacturers aim to meet both.

The Major Regulatory Agencies

🏛️ GOV

OSHA — Occupational Safety and Health Administration

U.S. Department of Labor · Founded 1971

OSHA is the federal agency that protects workers from workplace hazards. It sets standards for PPE, machine guarding, chemical handling, fall protection, noise exposure, and dozens of other safety areas. Every manufacturing facility in the US is subject to OSHA — regardless of size or industry.

Governs:Worker safetyPPE requirementsHazard communicationMachine safetyRecordkeeping
⚖️ Enforcement power: Unannounced inspections, fines up to $161,323 per willful violation, facility shutdowns, criminal prosecution for deaths caused by willful violations
🏛️ GOV

FDA — Food and Drug Administration

U.S. Department of Health and Human Services · Founded 1906

The FDA regulates the safety and effectiveness of food, drugs, medical devices, cosmetics, and biologics manufactured in or imported into the United States. For manufacturing, this primarily means pharmaceutical plants, food processing facilities, and medical device makers must follow FDA's Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) regulations. FDA inspections are unannounced and can result in Warning Letters, product recalls, and import bans.

Governs:Drug manufacturingFood processingMedical devicesProduct labelingBatch records
⚖️ Enforcement power: Warning Letters (public), product seizures, import bans, mandatory recalls, facility shutdowns, criminal prosecution of executives
🏛️ GOV

EPA — Environmental Protection Agency

U.S. Environmental Protection Agency · Founded 1970

The EPA sets and enforces standards for air quality, water quality, and waste disposal that manufacturing facilities must follow. Factories that emit pollutants into the air or discharge into waterways must have EPA permits with specific limits they cannot exceed. The EPA also regulates how hazardous waste is stored, handled, and disposed of — a critical compliance area for chemical and pharmaceutical manufacturers.

Governs:Air emissionsWastewaterHazardous wasteChemical storageSpill prevention
⚖️ Enforcement power: Fines up to $70,000/day per violation, facility cleanup orders, criminal charges, civil lawsuits from affected communities
🏛️ GOV

NIST — National Institute of Standards and Technology

U.S. Department of Commerce · Founded 1901

NIST develops measurement standards and technical guidelines used across manufacturing and technology. In manufacturing, NIST standards govern calibration — how test and measurement equipment is verified to be accurate. If a factory uses measuring instruments to test product quality, those instruments must ultimately trace back to NIST-certified standards. NIST also develops cybersecurity frameworks increasingly applied to connected manufacturing systems.

Governs:Measurement standardsCalibration traceabilityCybersecurity frameworksTesting methods
⚖️ Enforcement power: NIST itself doesn't enforce — but FDA and DOD contracts require NIST-traceable calibration. Non-compliance affects contract eligibility and product testing validity

The Major Standards Organizations

📋 STD

ISO — International Organization for Standardization

Geneva, Switzerland · Founded 1947 · 167 member countries

ISO is the world's largest developer of voluntary international standards. Their standards cover virtually every aspect of manufacturing and industry — quality management, environmental management, safety management, energy management, and more. ISO standards are developed by committees of industry experts from around the world and represent the consensus on how things should be done professionally.

The three ISO standards most important in manufacturing are: ISO 9001 (quality management systems — how to consistently make products that meet customer requirements), ISO 45001 (occupational health and safety management — how to systematically prevent worker injuries), and ISO 14001 (environmental management — how to minimize environmental impact).

Governs:Quality management (9001)Safety management (45001)Environmental (14001)Energy (50001)Many more
⚖️ Practical power: No legal enforcement, but major manufacturers (automotive, aerospace, pharma) require ISO certification from all suppliers. Without certification, many contracts are inaccessible
📋 STD

GMP — Good Manufacturing Practice

Regulated by FDA (US), EMA (Europe), WHO (Global)

GMP is a system of rules that governs how pharmaceutical, food, and medical device products are manufactured. Unlike most ISO standards, GMP is not truly voluntary — it's codified into FDA regulations (called cGMP, where the "c" stands for "current"). The core principle of GMP is quality by design: every step of production is controlled, documented, and verifiable, so that the quality of the final product is the predictable result of following the process correctly.

GMP touches everything: the cleanliness of facilities, the qualification of equipment, the training of personnel, the testing of raw materials, the documentation of every production step, and the testing of finished product before release.

Governs:Pharma manufacturingFood processingMedical devicesFacilitiesBatch records
⚖️ Practical power: In pharma and food, GMP compliance is required by FDA law. Non-compliance results in Warning Letters, product recalls, and facility shutdowns

Key Insight
Most manufacturing facilities are simultaneously subject to multiple agencies. A pharmaceutical plant must follow FDA (product), OSHA (workers), EPA (environment), and ISO standards (quality systems) — all at once, all the time. This complexity is exactly why AI-powered compliance management tools have real value: the number of overlapping requirements is genuinely difficult to track manually.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Two types of rule-makers: government agencies (legally binding — OSHA, FDA, EPA, NIST) and standards organizations (practically required — ISO, GMP). Government agencies write the legal floor; standards organizations write the professional best practice.

Each agency governs a specific domain: OSHA (worker safety), FDA (food/drugs/medical devices), EPA (environment), NIST (measurement standards), ISO (quality/safety/environmental management systems), GMP (pharmaceutical and food manufacturing processes).

Most manufacturing facilities are subject to multiple agencies simultaneously. A pharmaceutical plant must follow FDA, OSHA, EPA, and ISO standards all at once. This complexity is exactly why AI compliance monitoring tools have real value.

Different industries face different regulatory bodies — pharma (FDA + OSHA + EPA), automotive (OSHA + IATF 16949 + ISO), aerospace (FAA + AS9100), chemical (EPA + OSHA + DOT). Knowing which agencies govern a sector is a fundamental career skill in manufacturing compliance.
