False Alarms and Missed Hazards

Why AI safety systems aren't perfect — and what to do about it. This article connects the false positive / false negative concepts from Module 2 directly to safety monitoring, where the cost of each type of error is real and very different.

You Already Know the Framework — Now Apply It to Safety

In Module 2, you learned about false positives and false negatives — the two ways AI can be wrong. You saw how these errors play out with maintenance diagnostics. Now we're applying the same framework to safety monitoring, where the stakes change significantly.

In maintenance, a false positive might mean checking a machine that's actually fine — wasted time but no one gets hurt. In safety monitoring, the two types of errors have very different consequences, and understanding which is worse in which situation helps you calibrate how to respond to alerts.

TRUE POSITIVE
Real Violation, Alert Fired
AI detected a real PPE violation or hazard, and sent the correct alert. Supervisor responds and prevents potential injury.
✅ Best outcome
FALSE POSITIVE
No Violation, Alert Fired Anyway
Worker was actually compliant but AI misidentified the scene. Supervisor investigates and finds no real issue. Wastes time and causes alert fatigue if frequent.
⚠️ Costly if repeated
FALSE NEGATIVE
Real Violation, No Alert Sent
A real hazard existed but the AI missed it — poor lighting, camera blind spot, training gap. No alert means no response. This is the scenario that leads to injuries.
🚨 Highest safety risk
TRUE NEGATIVE
No Violation, No Alert
Everything on the floor is actually compliant, and the AI correctly generates no alert. The system is working as intended — quiet because there's nothing to flag.
✅ Correct silence

Why False Negatives Are the Greater Safety Risk

False positives are annoying and, if persistent, contribute to alert fatigue. But false negatives — real hazards that the AI misses entirely — are the ones that lead to actual injuries. When the AI fails to detect a violation, the only remaining detection layer is human observation. If no human is actively monitoring that area at that moment, the hazard goes unchecked.

This is why safety-critical facilities often deliberately set thresholds lower — accepting more false positives in exchange for catching more real violations. A supervisor who investigates 10 false alarms but catches the 1 real one that matters has done their job. A system that only alerts on certainties might miss the hazard that injures someone.

In safety monitoring, the cost of a missed hazard is a potential injury. The cost of a false alarm is a supervisor's time. These are not equal — and threshold decisions should reflect that.

What to Do When You Suspect the System Is Missing Things

If you're working in a facility with AI safety monitoring and you notice patterns that concern you — violations happening that the AI isn't flagging, cameras that seem to miss things — here's what responsible workers and supervisors do:

Document the concern. Note the specific location, time, and type of situation the AI appeared to miss. Vague concerns are hard to investigate. Specific examples with timestamps give the system team something to work with.

Report through proper channels. Most facilities have a process for reporting safety system concerns. Use it. This is exactly what that process exists for.

Increase manual monitoring in the gap. While the system issue is being investigated, supervisors should increase walkthrough frequency in areas where detection seems unreliable. The AI's gap is your gap to fill temporarily.

Never assume "the AI would have caught it." If you see a hazard with your own eyes, that observation is valid and actionable regardless of whether the AI flagged it. Human observation remains a safety layer even in AI-monitored facilities.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

In safety monitoring, false positives (false alarms) waste supervisor time and cause alert fatigue. False negatives (missed hazards) are the scenario that leads to actual injuries — making them the more serious error type.

The alert threshold controls the tradeoff. Lower thresholds catch more real violations but generate more false alarms. Higher thresholds produce cleaner alerts but miss more genuine hazards. Safety-critical environments often accept more false positives to reduce false negatives.

Alert fatigue — when supervisors stop engaging with alerts because too many are false — is a system calibration problem. The fix is improving alert precision, not disciplining human responders.

If you observe hazards the AI isn't flagging, document them specifically, report through proper channels, increase manual monitoring in the gap, and never wait for an AI alert before acting on what you personally observe.
