The Situation: Conveyor Motor — Building 3

The Bad AI Recommendation

Based on this data, the AI system generated the following three recommendations. All three are marked wrong on the slide. Read them carefully — then we'll find the clues that explain why.

❌ AI Recommendations — All Incorrect
AI Output: Conveyor B Motor Alert
✗
Option 1: Restart the system — Current anomaly may be transient. Restart motor and monitor.
✗
Option 2: Replace the sensor — Current draw reading may be faulty. Replace current sensor before further action.
✗
Option 3: Check the logs — Review performance log for previous similar events before deciding.
Why are these wrong? The current draw is 41% above normal and the motor is running hot. "Restart and monitor" ignores a real problem. "Replace the sensor" dismisses a genuine reading without physical verification. "Check the logs" is vague — there are specific physical checks that should happen first. None of these recommendations address the root cause. Let's find it.

Find the Clues the AI Missed

The AI only sees sensor data. It doesn't know what's happening in the physical world. Click each clue card to reveal information that the AI didn't have access to — but that a maintenance technician on the floor would discover.

Clue 1
Check the load on the conveyor
Click to reveal what the technician found →
The technician walked to the conveyor and immediately noticed: the line was carrying steel plate stock — the heaviest material in the production schedule. Each plate weighs 340 lbs. The conveyor was loaded to 118% of its rated capacity because a loading error occurred at the start of the shift. The AI has no sensor for "weight on belt."
Clue 2
Check the belt tension
Click to reveal what the technician found →
The belt tension was set for the standard aluminum sheet load that runs on Tuesday. No one reconfigured it for the heavier Wednesday steel plate batch. Running a heavily loaded belt at incorrect tension forces the motor to work significantly harder — producing exactly the current spike and temperature rise the AI flagged.
Clue 3
Check the shift handover notes
Click to reveal what the technician found →
The shift handover log from Tuesday night showed a note: "Wednesday batch is steel plate — conveyor tension needs to be adjusted per SOP-CON-14 before loading." The note was written. But it was never actioned. The morning crew loaded the conveyor without reading the handover notes or adjusting the tension.
Clue 4
Check historical data for similar patterns
Click to reveal what the AI's own logs showed →
A search of the SCADA historian revealed that this exact pattern — current spike + temperature rise + normal vibration — had occurred three times in the past eight months. Each time, the root cause was operating the conveyor above rated load capacity. The AI hadn't been trained to recognize "overload" as distinct from "motor degradation" — both look similar in current/temperature data alone.

Now Fix It: What Should the AI Have Recommended?

You've found the clues. Now select the correct recommendation — what a well-informed maintenance technician would actually do in this situation.

✅ Correct Diagnosis and Action Plan
Root Cause: Conveyor Overload + Incorrect Belt Tension Configuration
1.Stop the conveyor immediately — motor running at 88°C with 18.4A draw risks winding damage if continued
2.Remove excess steel plates to bring load within rated capacity (max 85% for continuous operation)
3.Reconfigure belt tension per SOP-CON-14 for heavy steel plate stock (different from aluminum sheet setting)
4.Allow motor to cool to below 75°C before restart (approx. 15–20 minutes with conveyor stopped)
5.Restart with corrected load and tension — monitor current draw for first 10 minutes to confirm return to normal range
6.Review shift handover process — the note was written but not acted on. Update SOP to require sign-off confirmation

What This Exercise Teaches

The AI wasn't wrong about the data — the current draw and temperature readings were accurate. But it failed to diagnose the correct cause because:

It had no sensor for conveyor load weight. It had no access to the shift handover notes. It had no way to know belt tension wasn't configured for today's material. And it hadn't been trained to distinguish "motor degradation" from "motor overload" — even though both produce similar current/temperature patterns.

The AI's three recommendations (restart, replace sensor, check logs) all failed because they didn't address any of the physical root causes. A maintenance technician with access to the actual factory floor — and 5 minutes of physical investigation — found what the AI couldn't see.

This is why AI is a starting point, not an endpoint. The sensors tell you something is wrong. Your legs, your eyes, and your conversation with the morning crew tell you why.

📌 Exercise Summary

What This Exercise Teaches

AI can be right about the data but wrong about the cause. The motor really was drawing excess current and running hot — but the reason was conveyor overload, not motor failure. Accurate data doesn't guarantee an accurate diagnosis.

Physical context the AI can't see — load weight, belt tension settings, shift handover notes — is often the difference between a correct and incorrect diagnosis. The 5-minute physical walkdown found what no amount of sensor data analysis could reveal.

Training data gaps produce confident-sounding wrong answers. The AI gave three recommendations — all confidently wrong. A failure mode not in the training data will always be misdiagnosed. Workers who understand this stay skeptical of AI recommendations that don't have physical confirmation.

Fixing AI starts with fixing processes. The root cause here wasn't just technical — it was a process failure (shift handover notes not acted on). The correct action plan included both the technical fix and the process fix. AI failures often reveal process failures hiding underneath.
