Scenario 1 of 6 ⬤ Easier 📦 Module 1

The New Machine Nobody Trained For

📘 Key Term — Predictive Maintenance AI: Predictive maintenance AI monitors machines in real time — measuring vibration, temperature, speed, and power use — and alerts workers before a breakdown happens. Instead of waiting for something to break, the AI helps the team fix small problems early. It supports the technician — it does not replace them.

Keystone Fabrication just installed a new AI-powered monitoring system on its CNC (Computer Numerical Control) milling machines. The system watches each machine and sends alerts when it detects patterns that typically lead to breakdowns.

On the first day, the AI sends an alert on Machine 7:

⚠ PREDICTIVE ALERT — Machine 7 CNC Mill Spindle vibration: +34% above baseline
Coolant temp: elevated 12°F above normal
Runtime since last service: 847 hours
AI recommendation: Schedule maintenance within 48 hours

The machine operator, Darnell, says: "This machine runs hot all the time — that's just how it is. I wouldn't worry about it."

The production supervisor wants to keep the machine running because there's a big order due tomorrow. What do you recommend?

Scenario 2 of 6 ⬤ Easier 🔧 Module 2

The AI Diagnostic That Doesn't Add Up

📘 Key Term — Validating AI Output: Validating an AI output means checking whether the AI's recommendation makes sense before acting on it. AI tools in manufacturing can make mistakes — especially when they encounter situations outside their training data. A skilled worker should always cross-check an AI recommendation against their own knowledge, the maintenance manual, and physical inspection before making a repair decision.

The hydraulic press on Line 3 has been making an irregular noise. The AI diagnostic tool analyzes the machine's sensor data and produces a repair recommendation:

🔧 AI DIAGNOSTIC — Hydraulic Press Line 3 Symptom detected: irregular pressure cycling, 2.3 sec intervals
AI diagnosis: Faulty pressure relief valve — replace immediately
Estimated repair time: 2 hours
Confidence level: 71%

Your senior technician, Rosa, reviews the readout. She says: "A pressure relief valve failure would show a pressure spike before the cycle — I'm not seeing that in the raw data. This could be a worn accumulator bladder instead. That's a totally different fix."

What do you do?

Scenario 3 of 6 ⬤ Medium 🦺 Module 3

The Safety Alert Everyone Wants to Ignore

📘 Key Term — Compliance: Compliance means following the rules, regulations, and safety standards that apply to your workplace. In manufacturing, compliance often means following OSHA (Occupational Safety and Health Administration) standards. When an AI tool flags a compliance issue, a human must investigate and correct it — ignoring it is never acceptable, regardless of production pressure.

The plant's AI safety monitoring system uses cameras and sensors to watch for compliance violations on the shop floor. At 10:42 AM, it sends a high-priority alert:

🚨 SAFETY ALERT — Welding Bay C Violation detected: Worker operating angle grinder without face shield (PPE)
Duration of violation: 4 minutes 17 seconds
Risk level: HIGH — potential for eye/face injury from sparks/debris
Recommended action: Halt operation and address PPE compliance immediately

The shift lead, Marcus, says: "That's Trevon — he's our best welder. He only takes his shield off for a few seconds to check his work. He's been doing this for 12 years. It's fine."

There is a large rush order due to ship at 2pm. Stopping Trevon will delay the shipment. What do you do?

Scenario 4 of 6 ⬤ Medium 📋 Module 4

The AI Schedule That Looks Too Aggressive

📘 Key Term — Production Scheduling AI: A production scheduling AI uses data about machines, materials, labor, and deadlines to recommend the most efficient order of operations for a manufacturing line. It optimizes for speed and throughput — but it doesn't always know about real-world constraints like operator fatigue, machine quirks, or parts that are running low. Humans must review AI schedules before they go live.

It's Wednesday afternoon. The AI planning system has generated a new 48-hour production schedule for the weekend shift:

📋 AI PRODUCTION SCHEDULE — Weekend Shift (Fri–Sun) Line 1: Run at 110% rated capacity — 72 continuous hours
Line 2: Normal pace — scheduled maintenance Sunday 6am
Line 3: Run at 100% — zero buffer time between job changeovers
Operator coverage: Weekend crew: 6 operators (normal minimum: 8)
Projected output: +22% above weekly target
Risk flag: Schedule exceeds operator-to-machine ratio guidelines

The plant manager loves the projected output number. She says: "Let's run it — we need this week's numbers."

You notice the AI itself flagged the operator-to-machine ratio risk. Line 1 running at 110% capacity for 72 hours is also beyond its rated spec. What do you recommend?

Scenario 5 of 6 ⬤ Harder 🔧 Module 2.4 + 4.4

The Quality AI That's Missing the Point

📘 Key Term — Quality Control AI: Quality control AI uses cameras and sensors to inspect parts coming off the production line, flagging ones that fall outside acceptable measurements. It can check thousands of parts per hour — far faster than any human. But AI quality systems can miss defects that are hard to see visually, and they can sometimes reject good parts based on surface appearance alone. Human inspectors catch things AI can't.

Keystone's AI quality inspection system has been running on the brake component line for three weeks. This morning, it rejected 340 parts — an unusually high number for one shift. The AI flagged all of them for "surface irregularity."

🔍 QUALITY REPORT — Brake Component Line — Day Shift Parts inspected: 1,847
Parts rejected: 340 (18.4% rejection rate)
Rejection reason: Surface irregularity — code QC-14
Normal rejection rate: 2–4%
AI confidence in rejections: 88%

Your lead quality inspector, Amara, physically examines 20 of the rejected parts. She says: "These parts look fine to me. The surface marks are from the new cutting fluid we switched to last week — it leaves a light film that the AI camera is reading as a defect. Dimensionally these parts are perfect. I think the AI needs to be recalibrated for the new fluid."

What do you do?

Scenario 6 of 6 — Final Challenge ⬤ Hardest ⭐ Module 5 + 6

📘 Key Term — Human-AI Collaboration: Human-AI collaboration in manufacturing means using AI tools to handle data, monitoring, and pattern-detection — while human workers bring hands-on skill, judgment, and accountability that AI cannot replace. The goal is not to replace workers with AI, but to make skilled workers more effective and safer. Knowing when to trust AI, when to override it, and when to escalate are all critical professional skills.
