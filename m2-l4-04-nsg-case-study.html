The Manufacturing Challenge: Float Glass at Scale

NSG Group manufactures float glass — the kind used in car windshields, building windows, and electronic displays. Float glass is made by floating molten glass on a bed of molten tin in a controlled atmosphere, then slowly cooling it into perfectly flat sheets. The float process runs continuously, 24 hours a day, producing glass at speeds up to 1,000 meters per hour.

Quality defects in float glass — inclusions (foreign particles), bubbles, scratches, optical distortions — are tiny and can appear anywhere across a sheet that may be 3 meters wide and moving at high speed. Manual visual inspection of every square meter of glass at production speed is physically impossible. Yet a single defect that reaches an automotive or electronic display customer can trigger a recall, warranty claim, or contract termination.

This is exactly the kind of problem where AI has a structural advantage: inspecting more, faster, more consistently, and in conditions (bright lines, moving surfaces, controlled lighting) that are difficult for human vision but well-suited to computer vision systems.

How NSG's Computer Vision System Works Step by Step

1
🤖 AI
Camera array captures every square centimeter of glass in real time
High-resolution line-scan cameras mounted above the float glass line photograph every segment of the moving glass sheet at production speed. The cameras capture up to 400 million pixels per second. This is physically impossible for human inspection at this scale and speed. The AI receives every image as the glass passes beneath the cameras.
2
🤖 AI
Computer vision model analyzes each image for anomalies
The AI model — trained on hundreds of thousands of labeled images of defective and defect-free glass — compares each captured image against learned patterns. It flags areas where pixel patterns deviate from what defect-free glass looks like. Each flag includes: location (X/Y coordinate on the glass), classification (inclusion, bubble, scratch, distortion), and confidence score.
3
🤝 Both
Flags above threshold are automatically routed to the quality engineer's review station
Flags with confidence scores above 85% are displayed on the quality engineer's screen with the captured image, defect classification, confidence score, and location on the glass sheet. Low-confidence flags (under 60%) are logged but don't require immediate human review — they feed the learning system. Medium-confidence flags (60–85%) go into a secondary review queue.
4
👤 Human
Quality engineer reviews flagged images and makes the disposition decision
The quality engineer — a trained specialist — reviews each flagged image and makes the real decision: Is this a real defect? If yes, does it meet the severity threshold for rejection? Does it affect a critical area (a windshield's driver sight line gets zero tolerance; edge zones have more flexibility)? The engineer confirms or overrides the AI's classification and makes the reject/accept call. They also note whether the AI's classification was accurate — this feeds the learning loop.
5
🤝 Both
Disposition decision triggers automatic downstream action
Once the engineer confirms a reject, the CMMS marks the affected glass segment for removal. The glass handling system automatically diverts that section. If the engineer overrides the AI (marks "accept" what the AI flagged), that override is logged with the engineer's notes. A high override rate on a specific defect type triggers model retraining review.
6
🤖 AI
Engineer decisions feed the continuous learning loop
Every reviewed image — confirmed defect, confirmed false positive, corrected classification — becomes training data. The AI model is retrained weekly on the accumulated engineer decisions. Over time, the model's accuracy improves on NSG's specific glass types, process conditions, and defect patterns. The more carefully engineers document their override reasoning, the better the AI gets.

Why the Human Review Isn't Formality

It would be tempting to ask: if the AI has 99.4% detection accuracy, why review at all? The answer reveals something important about why human oversight in AI systems is structurally necessary, not optional courtesy.

First, context changes. NSG's glass production uses different formulations for different customers — automotive vs. architectural vs. electronic display glass. A defect that's rejectable in a windshield driver zone may be acceptable in the bottom edge of an architectural panel. The AI flags anomalies; the engineer applies the customer-specific rejection criteria that would be prohibitively complex to fully encode in the AI model.

Second, novel defects appear. When a new defect type emerges — one the AI hasn't been trained on — it may flag it at low confidence or not at all. Quality engineers who are physically familiar with the glass can catch new defect signatures that the AI misses. Those catches are what generate the new training data that eventually teaches the AI to detect the novel defect type.

Third, process changes affect AI accuracy. When a new raw material supplier, furnace adjustment, or process modification is introduced, the AI's accuracy temporarily drops because the production conditions have shifted away from its training distribution. Human engineers bridge this gap while the AI retrains on new data.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

NSG's computer vision system inspects 100% of glass output at production speed — something physically impossible for human inspectors. The AI provides scale and consistency; human quality engineers provide contextual judgment, customer-specific criteria, and novel defect detection.

The continuous learning loop is the key to long-term improvement: every engineer review decision — confirmed defects, overrides, corrected classifications — becomes training data for weekly model retraining. Engineers' expertise progressively improves the AI's accuracy on NSG-specific conditions.

Human review is structurally necessary, not formality — because context changes (customer specs), novel defects appear (AI hasn't seen them yet), and process changes temporarily shift conditions outside the AI's training distribution. These gaps require human judgment to bridge.

The quality engineer's career became more valuable, not less — shifting from repetitive visual scanning to high-judgment review, defect classification, and continuous improvement of the AI system itself. This is what human-AI collaboration looks like at scale.
