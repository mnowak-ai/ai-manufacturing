Lesson 1.3 — Human vs. AI Roles

Who Is Responsible When AI Makes a Mistake in a Factory?

Career-essential knowledge. When AI recommends stopping a line and you follow that recommendation — who owns the outcome? The answer matters more than you might think.

Here's a situation that happens in AI-enabled manufacturing facilities every week: the AI recommends an action, a human follows it, and the outcome is wrong. Maybe a line shuts down unnecessarily. Maybe a defective part ships. Maybe a maintenance call turns out to be a false alarm that cost four hours of labor.

And then someone asks: whose fault is this?

This question isn't just academic. It has real consequences for workers' careers, companies' liability, and customers' safety. Every person working alongside AI systems needs a clear answer before a mistake happens — not after.

The Core Principle: AI Recommends, Humans Decide

Key Principle
AI systems are tools, not decision-makers. In manufacturing, AI provides information, predictions, alerts, and recommendations. Every consequential action taken based on that information is a human decision — and human decisions carry human accountability. The AI doesn't own the outcome. The person who acted does.

This isn't a technicality or a legal dodge. It reflects a genuine truth about how AI works: the system produces outputs. A human reads those outputs, interprets them, and chooses what to do. That choice belongs to the human.

The implication is important: "The AI told me to" is not a complete defense. It's a contributing factor — one that changes how responsibility is distributed up the accountability chain — but the worker who acted on the recommendation made a choice. That choice is where accountability starts.

A hammer doesn't bear responsibility when it's used wrong. Neither does an AI system. The person holding the tool — or acting on its output — carries the accountability.

The Accountability Chain: Who Owns What

Accountability in AI-assisted manufacturing isn't a single point — it's a chain. Different people own different parts of what happens when something goes wrong. Here's how it breaks down:

Level 1
AI Vendor / Software Developer
Responsible for: The fundamental accuracy and reliability of the AI system as designed. If the model was built with known defects, trained on biased data, or deployed with false claims about its accuracy, the vendor bears significant liability for resulting harms.
Limit: Vendors typically disclaim liability for harms arising from customer misuse, failure to maintain/retrain the model, or using AI output in ways inconsistent with the product's documented design.
Level 2
Factory Owner / Company
Responsible for: Choosing which AI systems to deploy, ensuring workers are trained on both the system's capabilities and its limitations, maintaining the system properly (including retraining and calibration), and establishing clear policies for when AI recommendations should and shouldn't be followed without additional review.
Limit: If the company established reasonable policies and trained workers properly, individual employee errors in applying those policies shift more accountability toward the individual. If the company ignored known AI limitations, they bear more.
Level 3
Safety Manager / EHS Director
Responsible for: Ensuring AI safety tools are used appropriately within the facility's safety program, monitoring AI system performance and reporting concerning patterns, and making sure AI monitoring doesn't replace human safety oversight.
Limit: If the safety manager followed established vendor and company protocols and a failure occurred anyway due to an AI system defect, liability shifts upward toward company and vendor.
Level 4
Supervisor
Responsible for: Exercising judgment when acting on AI alerts, ensuring their team understands how to interpret AI recommendations, escalating when AI behavior seems wrong, and not authorizing actions based solely on AI output for high-stakes decisions without appropriate review.
Limit: A supervisor following established protocols who acts on an AI recommendation in good faith has reduced personal liability — accountability shifts toward the company that set those protocols and the vendor whose system generated the recommendation.
Level 5
Floor Worker / Operator
Responsible for: Following established protocols for acting on AI recommendations, using personal judgment to flag when an AI recommendation seems inconsistent with observable reality, reporting AI errors and unusual system behavior, and not bypassing required human verification steps because "the AI already checked it."
Limit: A worker who followed training and protocols in good faith has significantly reduced personal accountability when an AI error causes harm. A worker who overrode safety procedures or ignored known red flags takes on more.

Three Specific Scenarios — Who Owns It?

Abstract principles are easier to understand through specific cases. For each scenario, choose who you think bears the most accountability — then see the reasoning.

Scenario 1
AI Recommends Stopping the Line — It's a False Alarm — Worker Follows the Recommendation
The AI predicts an imminent bearing failure and recommends halting Line 3. The operator, following her training, halts the line and calls maintenance. Maintenance inspects and finds nothing wrong — false alarm, $28,000 in lost production. The company's protocol says: "Follow AI halt recommendations and notify maintenance." The operator did exactly that.
Who bears primary accountability for the lost production?
✅ Right. When a worker follows established protocol in good faith, accountability for the outcome shifts toward the institution that established that protocol (the company) and the system that generated the false recommendation (vendor/system). The operator exercised exactly the judgment she was trained to exercise. Holding her accountable would discourage workers from following safety protocols — the opposite of what companies want.
❌ Not quite. When a worker follows established protocol in good faith, accountability shifts significantly toward the company that set that protocol and the AI vendor whose system generated the false alert. Holding the operator personally accountable for following her training creates perverse incentives — workers who fear blame for following protocols start improvising instead, which is more dangerous.
Scenario 2
AI Quality System Passes a Defective Batch — 800 Defective Units Ship to a Customer
The AI quality inspection system passes a batch of electronic components. The defect — a new type of solder crack introduced after a recent reflow oven temperature adjustment — wasn't in the model's training data. 800 units ship and fail at the customer site. The company had changed the oven settings three weeks earlier but didn't notify the AI system maintenance team or initiate retraining. The quality operator ran the parts through the AI system as usual.
Where does accountability primarily fall in this case?
✅ Correct. The AI's blind spot was created by a process change that wasn't communicated to the team responsible for model maintenance. That failure in communication — not the quality operator following her normal process, and not the AI for not detecting what it wasn't trained on — is the proximate cause. Whoever changed the oven settings had a responsibility to flag the change and trigger retraining or supplemental inspection. This is exactly why process change communication protocols matter so much in AI-integrated facilities.
❌ Not the right focus. The quality operator followed her standard process. The AI vendor's model did what it was designed to do. The accountability gap was created when the oven settings were changed without communicating the change to the AI model maintenance team — creating a foreseeable blind spot that nobody flagged. Process change communication is a specific human responsibility in AI-integrated facilities, and its failure here is the primary cause of the defect escaping.
Scenario 3
Worker Overrides AI Safety Alert — Says It's "Always Wrong" — Incident Occurs
A maintenance technician receives an AI alert indicating that a machine's safety interlock is malfunctioning. He's seen this alert fire incorrectly many times before and dismisses it without investigation — he doesn't report the recurring false alerts either. This time, the alert is real. He reaches into the machine during a maintenance procedure. The interlock doesn't engage as expected. He receives a crush injury to his hand.
How does accountability distribute in this case?
✅ Right — this is the most nuanced case, and the answer is genuinely shared. The technician bypassed a safety-critical alert and never reported the false alarm pattern — both are failures that contributed directly to the injury. But the company is also accountable: a high false alarm rate that goes unfixed for long enough that workers develop the habit of ignoring alerts is a systemic failure in how the AI was deployed and managed. Alert fatigue that goes unaddressed is a company-level safety failure, not just a worker behavior problem.
❌ This case is more nuanced than a single point of blame. The technician bypassed a safety alert — a real failure. But the company shares accountability for deploying a system with a false alarm rate high enough to cause alert fatigue, and then not providing a formal channel for reporting or fixing that pattern. Alert fatigue that goes unaddressed until someone ignores a real hazard is a company-level safety failure as much as an individual one.

How to Protect Yourself — and Your Team

Understanding accountability isn't just about knowing who gets blamed. It's about building the habits that put you on the right side of that accountability chain before anything goes wrong.

📋
Follow Documented Protocols
When your company has established a protocol for acting on AI recommendations, following it in good faith is your primary protection. If a protocol seems wrong or unsafe, escalate — don't improvise.
📣
Report AI Errors Formally
When you notice an AI system behaving incorrectly — too many false alarms, missed problems, recommendations that don't match your observations — report it through official channels. Don't just adapt your behavior silently. Undocumented problems are much harder to defend against later.
🔍
Document Your Reasoning for High-Stakes Calls
When you make a significant judgment call based on AI output — stopping a line, passing a batch, escalating a safety concern — briefly document why: what the AI said, what you observed, and what you decided. This contemporaneous record is powerful protection.
📢
Flag Process Changes Immediately
Any change to materials, settings, or equipment creates a potential AI blind spot. Flagging that change to whoever manages AI model maintenance is your responsibility — and it protects you from being blamed for a defect that was the predictable consequence of an unannounced change.
🚫
Never Use AI as Cover for Cutting Corners
"The AI passed it" is not a defense for skipping a required human verification step. If your job includes a manual check, that check exists for reasons — including catching AI errors. Removing it to save time takes accountability off the system and puts it squarely on you.
❓
Ask When You're Unsure
If an AI recommendation seems wrong, inconsistent, or unclear — ask before acting. A supervisor, a more experienced colleague, the safety manager. The few minutes it takes to verify are your protection. Acting on a recommendation you had doubts about without checking is the most accountable position to be in if it goes wrong.
