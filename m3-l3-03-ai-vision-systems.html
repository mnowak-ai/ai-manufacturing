Module 3 · Lesson 3.3 — Quality Control

How AI Vision Systems Inspect Products

A step-by-step walkthrough of how machine vision QC actually works — from camera setup and lighting through image processing, confidence scoring, and the flagging decision. Understanding the mechanics helps you evaluate these systems and explain them to others.

From Camera to Decision in Milliseconds

When a product moves through an AI inspection station, a lot happens very quickly. A camera captures an image. That image is processed by an AI model. A confidence score is produced. A decision is made — pass or flag. All of this happens in milliseconds, at production speed, for every single unit on the line.

To understand how AI QC systems can be both so fast and so reliable — and to understand their limitations — you need to see how each step of this pipeline works.

Key Term
Machine Vision: The use of cameras and computer processing to automatically inspect, measure, or identify objects. In manufacturing QC, machine vision systems replace or augment human visual inspection — operating continuously, consistently, and at speeds humans cannot match. The AI component classifies what the camera sees and determines whether it represents a defect.

The 6-Step Machine Vision Pipeline

Every AI vision QC system operates through the same basic pipeline — from physical image capture to final disposition decision. Here's what happens at each stage:

1

Image Capture — The Camera Setup

One or more industrial cameras photograph the product as it moves past the inspection station. Camera selection matters: resolution (how many pixels per image), frame rate (how many images per second), and wavelength (visible light, infrared, UV, or X-ray depending on what needs to be detected). Multiple cameras at different angles ensure full coverage — a single camera can't see the underside of a part or the inside of a package.

Line scan camerasArea scan camerasInfrared imagingMulti-angle arrays
2

Controlled Lighting — The Secret Weapon

Lighting is often more important than the camera itself. The same surface defect can be invisible under one lighting setup and clearly visible under another. AI inspection systems use precisely engineered LED lighting rigs — angled to create shadows that reveal surface texture, or back-lit to reveal density variations, or strobe-synced to freeze motion. Getting the lighting right is often the most technically demanding part of deploying a machine vision system.

Coaxial (direct) lightingDark field (angled) lightingBacklit illuminationStrobe synchronization
3

Preprocessing — Cleaning the Image

Before the AI model analyzes the image, preprocessing algorithms normalize it — correcting for brightness variation, lens distortion, perspective, and motion blur. A product that's slightly off-center in the camera frame still needs to be compared to reference images correctly. Preprocessing makes the AI's job more consistent by delivering standardized inputs regardless of minor variation in how each unit is positioned.

Geometric correctionBrightness normalizationNoise filteringDistortion correction
4

AI Model Analysis — Finding the Defect

The preprocessed image passes through a trained neural network — typically a convolutional neural network (CNN) — that has learned to recognize defects from thousands or millions of labeled example images. The model scans the image, identifies regions of interest, and evaluates whether each region shows normal variation (acceptable) or an actual defect. Different models are trained for different defect types — a model trained to find paint scratches doesn't automatically detect dimensional deviations.

Convolutional neural networkDefect localizationAnomaly detectionMulti-class classification
5

Confidence Scoring — How Sure Is the AI?

Rather than a binary pass/fail, the AI outputs a confidence score — a probability between 0 and 100% representing how certain the model is that a defect is present. A score of 95% means the model is highly confident. A score of 52% means it's barely more likely than not. The system then applies a threshold: any score above the threshold is flagged; scores below pass. Adjusting this threshold changes the balance between false positives and false negatives — the core tradeoff in QC system design.

Probability score 0–100%Configurable thresholdsMulti-defect scoring
6

Decision & Output — Pass, Flag, or Reject

The scored result triggers a physical action: a green light and the unit continues down the line (pass), a yellow alert triggers a human review station (flag for review), or an automated reject mechanism diverts the unit off the line (hard reject). High-confidence defects may be automatically rejected; borderline scores may go to a human inspector for final decision. The output also writes a record — defect type, location, confidence score, timestamp — to the quality database for later analysis.

Why Lighting Makes or Breaks Everything

Industrial vision engineers joke that machine vision is "90% lighting" — and they're not entirely wrong. The same camera, the same AI model, the same defect can produce completely different results depending on lighting setup. Here's why four different lighting approaches exist for different inspection challenges:

💡

Coaxial (Direct) Lighting

Light hits surface straight-on. Best for flat, reflective surfaces. Reveals color variation and contamination.

🔦

Dark Field (Angled) Lighting

Light hits at a sharp angle — scratches and surface texture catch the light and glow against a dark background.

🌟

Backlit (Silhouette) Lighting

Light from behind the product creates a silhouette — ideal for measuring shape, detecting holes, checking fill levels.

🟣

UV / Fluorescence

Ultraviolet light makes certain materials fluoresce — contamination, adhesives, or coatings become visible that are invisible in normal light.

An AI model trained perfectly on bad lighting data will perform badly in the real world. The quality of the detection starts with the quality of the image — which starts with the lighting.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

The 6-step machine vision pipeline is: image capture → controlled lighting → preprocessing → AI model analysis → confidence scoring → decision and output. Each step must be correctly configured for the system to work reliably.

Lighting is as important as the camera — the same defect can be invisible or clearly visible depending on the lighting setup. Different techniques (coaxial, dark field, backlit, UV) are used for different defect types.

The confidence threshold determines the tradeoff between false positives (flagging good products) and false negatives (missing real defects). There is no perfect threshold — every setting involves this tradeoff. Setting it too low floods human reviewers with false alarms; too high and real defects slip through.
