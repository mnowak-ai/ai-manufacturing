What Is a Non-Conformance Report (NCR)?

An NCR is the document that tracks every quality failure in a manufacturing environment — from the moment a defect is found to the moment the root cause is fixed and verified. You'll encounter NCRs constantly in any QC or documentation role. This article explains what they are, who fills them out, and how AI is changing the process.

What Non-Conformance Means

In manufacturing, non-conformance means a product, material, or process failed to meet a defined specification. It's a broader term than "defect" — it covers anything outside the defined acceptable range, including raw materials that arrive out of spec, process parameters that drifted outside limits, and finished products that fail inspection.

A Non-Conformance Report (NCR) is the document that opens when a non-conformance is discovered and closes when the problem is fully resolved. It's not just a description of what went wrong — it's a structured process for managing the problem to a verified resolution.

Key Term
Non-Conformance Report (NCR): A formal document opened when a product, material, or process fails to meet a specification. The NCR tracks the full lifecycle of the quality problem: identification → investigation → disposition (what to do with the affected product) → corrective action (fix the root cause) → verification (confirm the fix worked) → closure. NCRs cannot be closed until all steps are completed and documented.

A facility with no NCRs is not a high-quality facility — it's a facility that isn't reporting its problems. Regulators know this, and too few NCRs is itself a red flag during audits.

What an NCR Looks Like — Interactive Example

Below is a sample NCR for the temperature excursion event from our pharmaceutical production example. Explore the disposition options to see what each choice means.

NON-CONFORMANCE REPORT — NCR-2024-0847 STATUS: OPEN
1. Identification
Date/Time Opened
2024-03-14 · 10:12 AM
Opened By
A. Chen (QC Tech III)
Batch / Product
BTH-2024-1143 · Metformin 500mg
Equipment
Coating Pan CP-04 / Sensor S-217
Description of Non-Conformance
Coating pan temperature excursion detected at 10:12. Measured: 43.2°C. Specification limit: ≤40.0°C. Excursion duration: 49 minutes. Line halted pending investigation. AI alert generated automatically; production supervisor notified immediately.
2. Immediate Containment
Action Taken
Production halted at 10:12. Affected product held in quarantine pending disposition. Maintenance notified at 10:13. Equipment isolated and tagged out of service.
3. Disposition Decision

Select the disposition option to see what each means for this batch:

Rework: The affected batch undergoes additional processing to bring it into specification. For this temperature excursion — coating tablets at too high a temperature may affect coating integrity. Rework might involve testing a sample of tablets for dissolution profile and film coating thickness. If tests pass, the batch may be releasable. Rework requires a separate batch record documenting the rework process and a QA sign-off confirming the reworked product meets spec.
Scrap: The affected batch is rejected and destroyed. Chosen when rework is not feasible (the quality risk is too high, rework would require more processing than the product can tolerate, or the defect fundamentally affects the product's safety or efficacy). Destruction must be documented, witnessed, and recorded. For pharmaceutical product, specific destruction procedures must comply with environmental regulations.
Use As-Is (with Deviation): The product is released without rework, but the deviation from specification is formally documented and justified. Requires a written justification that the deviation does not affect product safety, quality, or efficacy — signed off by QA management and sometimes the regulatory affairs team. In pharmaceutical manufacturing, Use-As-Is requires especially strong scientific justification and carries regulatory risk if challenged during audit.
Return to Supplier: Applies when the non-conformance originates with incoming materials or components — the defective lot is returned to the supplier with a formal rejection notice. The NCR becomes part of the supplier quality record, affecting the supplier's approval status. Multiple NCRs resulting in supplier returns may trigger a supplier audit or re-qualification process.
4. Root Cause Investigation
Findings
Temperature sensor S-217 had drifted from calibration — reporting 2.1°C below actual temperature. Actual coating pan temperature was 43.2°C when display read 41.1°C. Sensor had reached its 180-day calibration interval on 2024-03-10 (4 days prior). Sensor replacement and recalibration completed 10:38.
Root Cause
Calibration interval for high-temperature sensors not synchronized with production scheduling — sensor due for calibration was not flagged for service before batch production began.
5. Corrective Action
Action Required
Implement pre-batch equipment verification checklist that includes calibration expiry review for all production-critical sensors. Update CMMS to flag calibration due dates 7 days in advance. Review all other sensors in coating department for upcoming calibration intervals. — STATUS: IN PROGRESS
6. Verification
Verification Method
QA to confirm pre-batch checklist implemented and sensors reviewed. Next batch production (BTH-2024-1201) to serve as first verification event. — STATUS: PENDING

An NCR is not closed when the defective product is handled — it's closed when the root cause is fixed and that fix is verified as effective. This distinction is what separates a quality management system that learns from problems versus one that just responds to them.

Who Does What on an NCR

QC Technician
Opens the NCR when a non-conformance is discovered. Documents the initial description, the affected product or lot, and the immediate containment action taken. This is often the first person to interact with an NCR — and getting the initial description right is critical for the investigation.
QA Investigator
Leads the root cause investigation. Uses the 5 Whys method, fishbone diagrams, or statistical analysis to identify what actually caused the non-conformance — not just the symptom, but the underlying process failure. Documents findings in the NCR.
QA Manager
Makes or approves the disposition decision. Decides whether affected product can be reworked, used as-is, or must be scrapped. Reviews root cause analysis for adequacy. Signs off on corrective action plans before they are implemented.
Process Engineer
Designs and implements the corrective action — the actual process change, procedure update, or equipment modification that addresses the root cause. Works with QA to confirm the corrective action is feasible and doesn't introduce new risks.
QA Director
Closes the NCR after confirming that the corrective action was implemented as planned AND that verification evidence shows it's working. A QA director cannot close an NCR until verification is complete — this is the final gate in the process.

How AI Is Changing the NCR Process

🤖 Auto-Detection → Auto-Draft

When an AI inspection system or process sensor detects a non-conformance, it can automatically generate a draft NCR — pre-populated with the affected product, timestamp, sensor readings, and non-conformance description. Workers review and confirm rather than starting from a blank form.

📊 Pattern Recognition

AI quality management systems can identify when multiple NCRs share a root cause — a specific supplier, a specific piece of equipment, a specific shift pattern. Instead of investigating each NCR independently, quality teams can see systemic issues across dozens of events at once.

⏰ Escalation Tracking

AI systems monitor NCR age and escalate automatically when investigation or corrective action steps are overdue. Old, open NCRs are a major audit finding — automated reminders and escalation paths keep the NCR process moving without depending on someone to remember to follow up.

✍️ Draft Corrective Actions

Based on similar past NCRs and their successful corrective actions, AI systems can suggest corrective action approaches — "Similar excursions in the past were resolved by calibration interval adjustment — see NCRs 0441, 0613, and 0729 for reference." Humans still decide and implement; AI surfaces relevant precedent.

🧠 Check Your Understanding
During an FDA audit, an investigator asks to see a list of all open NCRs for the past 12 months. The quality manager shows a list of 3 open NCRs — all opened in the past 30 days. The investigator then notes that the facility had 0 NCRs in the preceding 11 months. What does this likely indicate to the auditor?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

An NCR is the formal document that tracks a quality failure from discovery through verified resolution. It's not closed when the defective product is handled — it's closed when the root cause is fixed and the fix is confirmed to be working.

Disposition is the decision about what to do with the affected product: rework (fix it), scrap (destroy it), use as-is with deviation (document and justify releasing it), or return to supplier (reject incoming materials). Each option requires specific documentation and approvals.

A facility with too few NCRs is a regulatory red flag — it suggests problems aren't being reported. AI systems help by auto-generating draft NCRs from detected non-conformances, identifying patterns across many NCRs, and tracking overdue items to prevent NCRs from aging without resolution.
