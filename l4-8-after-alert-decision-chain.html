Lesson 1.4 — Shop Floor Examples

What Happens After the AI Catches a Problem? The Human Decision Chain in Manufacturing

The alert fired. Now what? A complete walkthrough of everything that happens between an AI flag and a resolved problem — and how that resolution makes the AI smarter over time.

Most of what you've read in this module has focused on the AI part — how it detects, what it monitors, what it catches and misses. But the moment an alert fires, the AI's role is essentially over. Everything that follows is human.

This article closes the loop. From the alert to the investigation to the decision to the documentation to the feedback — here is the full chain, played out in real time through a single realistic scenario.

The Scenario: A Predictive Maintenance Alert at 2:14 PM

We'll follow a single alert from the moment it fires to the moment the AI model is improved by what was learned. Every step has a human making a decision.

2:14 PM — Alert fires
The AI System
Predictive Maintenance Alert Generated
⚠️ Maintenance Alert — Medium Severity
Machine: CNC Mill 6 — Spindle Assembly
Vibration: 2.7 mm/s (baseline: 1.1 mm/s) | Temperature: 164°F (baseline: 148°F) | Current draw: +12% above normal | Trend: gradual increase over 54 hours

Predicted failure window: 5–9 days | Confidence: 84%
Recommended action: Supervisor notification + maintenance inspection within 48 hours

The alert fires to two places simultaneously: the floor supervisor's tablet and the maintenance team lead's work order queue. It includes a timestamp, the specific sensor readings, the deviation magnitude, the trend duration, and the estimated failure window. The AI's job is done at this moment — it has done everything it can do.

2:15 PM — First human response
Floor Supervisor — Yolanda
First Decision: How Urgent Is This?

Yolanda sees the alert on her tablet. She applies the evaluation framework she's developed over years of working with this system: specific sensor data (✓), medium severity (✓), 84% confidence (✓), 5–9 day window (✓ — not immediate). Before deciding on a response, she walks to CNC Mill 6.

She puts her hand on the spindle housing. Warm — noticeably so. She listens. There's a very slight roughness in the spindle sound she wasn't hearing last week. She checks the production schedule on her tablet: Mill 6 is running a critical batch for a customer order that ships Friday — 3 days from now.

Decision Point 1: Urgency Assessment
Does this need to stop now, or can it run until scheduled maintenance?
→
Her call: AI gives 5–9 days, physical check confirms developing but not critical issue, Friday order is 3 days away. She'll let it run but add monitoring. Notifies maintenance lead with "inspect within 24 hours."
2:20 PM — Maintenance notification
Maintenance Team Lead — Riko
Work Order Created, Technician Assigned

Riko receives Yolanda's notification and the original AI alert. He reviews both — the AI data and Yolanda's physical observation notes. He agrees with the urgency assessment: real developing issue, not immediate halt. He creates a formal maintenance work order and assigns it to his most experienced spindle tech, DeShawn, for a next-morning inspection.

He also adds a flag in the monitoring system: "Increase sensor polling frequency for Mill 6 spindle from 5-minute to 1-minute intervals until resolved." If the readings accelerate overnight, they'll know immediately.

Decision Point 2: Who and When
Which technician, what time, what additional monitoring?
→
DeShawn — most spindle experience. 7am tomorrow, before production starts. Increased polling frequency overnight as an early warning backstop.
Next morning, 7:05 AM — Investigation
Maintenance Tech — DeShawn
Diagnosis: What Is Actually Causing the Alert?

DeShawn arrives at Mill 6 before the shift starts. He performs lockout/tagout — verifying all energy sources are de-energized before opening anything. He checks the spindle lubrication system first: oil level normal, viscosity looks fine. He removes the spindle access panel and inspects the bearings with a stethoscope.

He hears it immediately: the classic irregular rolling sound of bearing wear — not catastrophic, but clearly developing. He checks the bearing date code: these bearings are 18 months old. Industry guidance for this machine class recommends replacement at 12–18 months under heavy use. Mill 6 has been running heavy-gauge steel for the last 4 months.

He has his diagnosis: upper spindle bearing wear. The AI predicted a failure in 5–9 days; DeShawn's assessment is that the outer race is showing early fatigue and could fail sooner under heavy load. The Friday customer order runs light-gauge aluminum — lower stress. He thinks they can finish the Friday order and replace bearings over the weekend.

Decision Point 3: Diagnosis and Recommendation
What's actually wrong, and what should happen next?
→
Upper spindle bearing wear. Recommendation to Riko and Yolanda: complete Friday's aluminum order (lower load), bearing replacement Saturday morning during planned downtime. Parts need to be ordered today.
8:30 AM — Decision meeting
Yolanda + Riko + DeShawn
The Three-Way Decision

The three meet briefly at Riko's desk. DeShawn presents his diagnosis. Yolanda confirms the Friday order is aluminum, not the heavy steel that accelerated the wear. Riko checks parts availability — bearings are in stock. All three agree: complete Friday, repair Saturday.

Yolanda's caveat: "If the vibration readings go above 3.5 mm/s at any point before Friday, we halt immediately regardless of the order." She sets a manual alert threshold at 3.5 mm/s in the monitoring system. Riko approves the Saturday overtime for DeShawn's crew.

Decision Point 4: Final Authorization
Does everyone agree on the plan, and are there clear abort conditions?
→
Plan approved. Continue on Friday order. Saturday bearing replacement. Hard stop condition: if vibration exceeds 3.5 mm/s at any time, halt immediately. All three signatories understand the condition.
Saturday, 8:00 AM — Resolution
DeShawn + Crew
Repair Executed and Verified

DeShawn and his crew replace the upper spindle bearing during Saturday's planned downtime window. After reassembly, they run a 30-minute break-in cycle at progressively increasing speeds. Vibration readings: 0.9 mm/s. Temperature: 144°F. Both back to baseline. The machine is clear.

DeShawn runs the first 10 parts of Monday's production as a quality verification — all parts within spec. He gives the machine back to operations.

Step 5: Documentation — Where the Loop Closes

The repair is done. The machine is running. Many people would consider the incident closed. But the most important step for the AI system hasn't happened yet: the documentation that turns this experience into improved future predictions.

📋 What Gets Documented
The Maintenance Record That Improves the Model
Alert details: timestamp, sensor readings at alert time, confidence score, predicted failure window
Physical inspection findings: DeShawn's notes on bearing condition, date code, sound signature
Root cause: upper spindle bearing wear accelerated by 4 months of heavy-gauge steel production
Decision rationale: why they ran through Friday (aluminum order, lower stress) and what abort condition was set
Repair performed: bearing replacement, break-in cycle, post-repair readings
Timeline: alert fired at T+0, diagnosis at T+17hrs, repair at T+3 days — well within the predicted 5–9 day window
Outcome: successful planned repair, no unplanned downtime, no defective parts produced

That documentation goes into three places: the maintenance log (DeShawn's work record), the machine history file (Mill 6's service record), and — critically — the AI system's feedback queue, where it becomes training data for the next version of the predictive model.

Every Resolved Incident Makes the System Smarter

↓

↓

Proper documentation isn't paperwork for its own sake. It's training data. Every incident that gets documented well teaches the AI to be more accurate for every worker who uses it after you.

The Escalation Ladder: Who Decides What at Each Level

Not every alert follows the same escalation path. The level of severity determines who needs to be involved and how fast. Here's the general structure:

Level 1 — Operator or Inspector
Low Severity, Clear Response
When: Alert is low-severity, the response is established by protocol, and the worker can handle it within their scope — adjusting a parameter, diverting a part for review, adding a lubrication check to the next PM cycle.
Who: The individual who received the alert handles it and documents the response.
Level 2 — Supervisor Involved
Medium Severity or Production Impact
When: The response involves stopping production, reallocating work orders, or making a decision that affects more than one person's work. The supervisor coordinates the response and ensures it aligns with production priorities.
Who: Supervisor takes point. Operator, maintenance, or quality tech provides input. Supervisor documents the decision rationale.
Level 3 — Management and Engineering
High Severity, Safety Risk, or Significant Cost
When: A potential major failure, safety incident, large quality escape, or decision that could affect customer commitments. Management authorizes the response; engineering investigates root cause; safety team may conduct a formal review.
Who: Plant manager or operations director takes point. Full investigation team assembled. Formal CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action) report required.
Level 4 — External Escalation
Regulatory, Customer Impact, or Recall Scenario
When: A quality escape has reached a customer, a safety incident has occurred, or a regulatory agency (OSHA, FDA, etc.) needs to be notified. Legal, quality leadership, and executives are involved.
Who: Company leadership and legal. CAPA report becomes formal and externally auditable. Root cause must be demonstrated to external parties.

What You Learned

✓
When an AI alert fires, the AI's role is essentially over. The entire response chain — urgency assessment, investigation, diagnosis, decision, repair, documentation — is human.
✓
The human decision chain involves multiple roles: the supervisor who assesses urgency and context, the maintenance lead who assigns and plans, the technician who physically diagnoses, and all three who align on the plan.
✓
Every resolution needs documented rationale: what was the problem, what was the decision, why was that decision made, what was the outcome. This isn't bureaucracy — it's training data for the AI model.
✓
Escalation level should match severity: low-severity alerts stay at the operator level; medium severity involves supervisors; high severity involves management and engineering; external impact involves leadership and regulators.
✓
The feedback loop is what makes AI systems get better over time. Every incident that gets properly documented improves the model's accuracy — making the next prediction better for every worker who uses it.
