Lesson 1.2 — Safety & AI

What Is PPE and Why Does AI Now Help Make Sure Workers Are Actually Wearing It?

Personal Protective Equipment is one of the last lines of defense between a worker and a serious injury. AI is now helping ensure that defense is always in place.

The number one cause of workplace injuries in manufacturing isn't equipment failure. It isn't lack of training. It's something simpler and more preventable: workers not wearing the protective gear they're supposed to wear.

A hard hat takes two seconds to put on. Safety glasses weigh almost nothing. Cut-resistant gloves are standard issue. And yet, every year, workers are injured by exactly the hazards these items protect against — because in the rush of a busy shift, gear gets skipped.

That's the problem AI is now helping solve. Not by replacing human judgment, but by watching for PPE gaps that humans miss — constantly, across every corner of the facility, every hour of every shift.

What Is PPE?

Key Term
PPE (Personal Protective Equipment): Wearable equipment designed to protect workers from physical, chemical, or environmental hazards. OSHA requires employers to provide appropriate PPE for each job task at no cost to the worker — and to ensure it is actually being used. PPE is considered a last line of defense because it only protects the individual worker, not the hazard itself.

That phrase — "last line of defense" — matters. Safety professionals prefer to eliminate hazards entirely (remove the dangerous machine), then control them (add a physical guard), and only then rely on PPE (give the worker protection). PPE is the final backup, not the first solution.

But in manufacturing, some hazards can't be fully eliminated. There will always be environments where workers need eye protection, where chemicals require gloves, where falling objects require head protection. For those situations, PPE is not optional — and OSHA treats failure to enforce it as a serious violation.

Why Do Workers Skip PPE — and Why That's a Problem

Workers who skip PPE are almost never reckless. They're usually just busy, uncomfortable, or working in a culture that looks the other way. Understanding the real reasons helps explain why enforcement has always been difficult — and why AI changes the equation.

⏱️
"I'm only doing this for two minutes."
Most serious injuries happen in seconds — not during long planned tasks, but during quick, unplanned ones. The "two-minute job" is where injuries spike precisely because PPE gets skipped.
🥵
"It's too hot / uncomfortable / slows me down."
Discomfort is real. But the consequence of a metal fragment in an unprotected eye is permanent vision loss. The discomfort of safety glasses weighs a few ounces. The math is not close.
👀
"Nobody else is wearing theirs and nobody says anything."
Safety culture is contagious in both directions. When non-compliance goes unchallenged, it normalizes. This is exactly what consistent AI monitoring is designed to interrupt — the gradual drift toward treating violations as acceptable.

A facility where PPE violations go unchallenged isn't just breaking OSHA rules — it's building a culture where the next serious injury is only a matter of time.

How AI Detects PPE Compliance in Real Time

Traditional PPE enforcement required a supervisor to be present, observing, and willing to intervene. With dozens of workers across a large facility, full-time human monitoring is impossible. AI cameras change what's achievable.

1
Cameras Capture the Scene
Fixed cameras positioned throughout the facility capture continuous video of every work area. The AI system processes this video in real time — not saving hours of footage to review later, but analyzing it as it happens.
A typical facility might have dozens of cameras already installed for security. AI safety systems often integrate with existing CCTV infrastructure rather than requiring all-new hardware.
2
AI Identifies People and PPE Items
The AI model has been trained on thousands of images of workers with and without each type of PPE. It can detect whether a hard hat is present on someone's head, whether safety glasses are covering their eyes, or whether a high-vis vest is being worn — and it does this for every person in the frame simultaneously.
The model doesn't just look for "something on a head" — it distinguishes between a hard hat and a regular cap, between safety glasses and sunglasses, between a vest and a regular jacket.
3
System Checks Against Zone Requirements
Each area of the facility is mapped to its PPE requirements. A worker without safety glasses in the break room is fine. A worker without safety glasses in the grinding bay triggers an alert. The AI applies the right rules to the right location.
This is more sophisticated than a simple "glasses detected / not detected" check — it requires the AI to know where in the building each camera is, and what the rules are for that zone.
4
Alert Goes to Supervisor — Immediately
When a violation is detected, the system sends a real-time alert — typically to a supervisor's phone, tablet, or a monitor on the floor — with a timestamp, camera ID, and often a screenshot of the violation. Response time drops from "noticed at the next safety walk" to seconds.
At Swire Coca-Cola, which you'll study in the next article, AI camera alerts reached supervisors in under 30 seconds — fast enough to intervene before a hazard became an injury.
5
Violation Is Logged Automatically
Every detected violation creates a timestamped record. Over time, these logs reveal patterns: which areas generate the most violations, which shifts show higher non-compliance, which types of PPE are most frequently skipped. The data drives targeted improvements.
This log also serves as OSHA compliance documentation — evidence that the employer is actively monitoring and responding to PPE violations, which matters enormously if an incident ever occurs.

What AI Can't Do — The Limits of PPE Monitoring

AI PPE detection is genuinely powerful, but it isn't perfect. Understanding its limitations is part of understanding how to use it responsibly.

📐
It can detect presence, not fit.
AI can see that a hard hat is on someone's head. It cannot reliably tell whether the chinstrap is fastened correctly, whether safety glasses are sealed against the face, or whether gloves have a hole in them. Proper PPE use still requires human training and judgment.
💡
Lighting and angle affect accuracy.
Poor lighting, obstructed camera angles, or crowded work areas can reduce detection accuracy. Facilities need well-placed cameras and adequate lighting for AI monitoring to work reliably. Garbage camera placement produces garbage alerts.
⚡
Alerts still require a human response.
When the AI fires an alert, a supervisor still has to see it, decide what to do, find the worker, and address the situation. AI detection compresses the response window — but doesn't eliminate the need for a human to act on what the camera finds.
