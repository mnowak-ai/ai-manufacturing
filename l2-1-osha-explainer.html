What Is OSHA, and What Does It Have to Do With AI?

A plain-language guide to workplace safety law — and why AI is changing how factories keep workers safe.

Every factory in the United States has one thing in common: they are all required by law to keep workers safe.

That law comes from a government agency called OSHA. Most workers have heard the name. Fewer know exactly what it means — or how powerful it actually is.

And almost no one is talking about how AI is now directly connected to OSHA compliance. That's what this article is about.

By the end, you'll understand how workplace safety law works, who is responsible for what, and how AI tools are becoming part of the system that keeps you and your coworkers alive.

What Is OSHA, and Where Did It Come From?

Key Term
OSHA stands for the Occupational Safety and Health Administration. It is a federal government agency created in 1970. Its job is to make and enforce safety rules for American workplaces.

Before OSHA, workplace safety was basically the employer's choice. Some companies cared deeply. Others didn't. Workers had very little legal protection if their boss decided that safety equipment was too expensive or too slow.

In the 1960s, over 14,000 U.S. workers died on the job every year. Congress decided that wasn't acceptable and passed the Occupational Safety and Health Act in 1970. President Nixon signed it into law.

Today, OSHA sets legally required safety standards that every covered employer must follow. If you violate them, OSHA can inspect your facility, issue citations, and fine you — sometimes tens of thousands of dollars per day until the problem is fixed.

OSHA doesn't give suggestions. It gives requirements. Every standard in OSHA's rulebook is backed by law.

What Does OSHA Actually Do Day to Day?

OSHA operates through four main activities. Think of these as the four pillars that hold up workplace safety law:

📋
Setting Standards
OSHA writes specific rules about what employers must do to keep workers safe. There are standards for everything from how high a scaffold can be to what kind of gloves you need when handling chemicals.
Example: OSHA Standard 1910.212 says all machines with moving parts must have guards so workers can't accidentally reach into them.
🔍
Conducting Inspections
OSHA inspectors can visit workplaces — sometimes unannounced — to check whether employers are following the rules. They look at equipment, talk to workers, and review records.
Example: After a worker reports a dangerous machine to OSHA, an inspector visits within days to investigate and document the hazard.
⚠️
Issuing Citations and Fines
If OSHA finds a violation, they issue a citation that explains what was wrong and sets a deadline to fix it. Fines can range from hundreds to hundreds of thousands of dollars depending on severity.
Example: A factory that fails to fix a fall hazard after a citation can be fined up to $156,259 per day for willful violations.
🎓
Providing Training and Education
OSHA also runs outreach programs to help employers and workers understand safety requirements before violations happen. Knowledge is the first line of defense.
Example: OSHA's 10-hour and 30-hour training programs are widely recognized certifications for workers and supervisors in manufacturing.

What Kinds of Hazards Does OSHA Cover?

Manufacturing plants have a lot of ways things can go wrong. OSHA organizes hazards into four main categories. Each one has specific rules attached to it — and AI is starting to help detect each type.

🔧
Physical Hazards
Dangerous machines, moving parts, heavy objects, slippery floors, loud noise, extreme heat or cold. These are the hazards you can see and touch.
🤖 AI role: camera systems that detect workers near unguarded equipment
☣️
Chemical Hazards
Toxic substances, fumes, dust, and liquids that can harm workers through skin contact, breathing, or accidental ingestion.
🤖 AI role: sensors that detect dangerous gas levels before they become visible or smell-able
🦴
Ergonomic Hazards
Repetitive motions, awkward postures, and heavy lifting that slowly injure muscles, tendons, and joints over months or years.
🤖 AI role: wearable sensors that track body position and alert workers when posture increases injury risk
😰
Psychological Hazards
Stress, overwork, and fatigue that impair decision-making and increase the chance of accidents — especially for workers running complex equipment.
🤖 AI role: fatigue detection systems that identify drowsy or distracted workers before accidents occur

Notice that AI is already touching every one of these categories. It's not replacing OSHA rules — it's giving factories new tools to actually follow them.

Who Is Responsible for Safety — Really?

One of the most common misconceptions about workplace safety is that it's only the employer's problem. The truth is more nuanced. OSHA creates a chain of responsibility that includes everyone from the company owner to the worker on the floor.

Level 1
Employer / Company Owner
Legally required to provide a safe workplace, follow all OSHA standards, provide appropriate PPE, train workers, and keep records of injuries and illnesses. This is where most OSHA fines land.
Level 2
Safety Manager / EHS Director
EHS stands for Environment, Health, and Safety. This person designs the safety program, runs inspections, tracks near-misses, and keeps the company in compliance. At AI-enabled factories, this person now manages AI safety tools too.
Level 3
Floor Supervisor / Line Manager
Responsible for making sure workers follow safety procedures on a day-to-day basis. When AI systems send alerts, supervisors are usually the ones deciding what to do next.
Level 4
Worker / You
Workers have rights AND responsibilities under OSHA. You have the right to a safe workplace, to see safety records, and to refuse dangerously unsafe work. You are also responsible for following safety procedures and reporting hazards you notice.
Key Term
Near miss: An incident that almost caused an injury but didn't — because of luck, quick action, or good equipment. OSHA encourages companies to report and investigate near misses because they predict future accidents. AI systems can help track near misses automatically using camera footage and sensor data.

So Where Does AI Actually Fit Into All This?

Here's the key insight: OSHA sets the rules, but factories have to figure out how to follow them. That's where AI comes in.

Traditionally, a safety manager would walk the floor every day looking for problems. They might check equipment manually, review maintenance logs on paper, and rely on workers to report hazards. It worked — but it was slow, and humans miss things.

AI changes what's possible:

📹
Computer Vision on the Floor
AI-connected cameras can watch every inch of a factory floor simultaneously, 24 hours a day. They can be trained to spot workers without proper PPE, people entering restricted zones, or unsafe posture while lifting.
OSHA connection: This helps companies document compliance with PPE requirements and restricted area rules — and catch violations before inspectors do.
📊
Automatic Incident Logging
OSHA requires employers to keep records of all workplace injuries and illnesses. AI systems can automatically log safety-related events — like when a safety sensor trips or a worker enters a danger zone — creating a more complete record than humans could manage alone.
OSHA connection: Better records mean faster investigations when accidents happen — and better evidence that the company was trying to prevent them.
🔮
Predictive Hazard Detection
Just like predictive maintenance spots failing machines before they break, AI safety tools can analyze patterns in near-miss reports, sensor readings, and worker behaviors to predict where accidents are most likely to happen — before they do.
OSHA connection: Proactively fixing predicted hazards is exactly what OSHA's "general duty clause" requires — employers must address known hazards even if there isn't a specific rule covering them.

AI doesn't replace OSHA. It gives employers better tools to actually do what OSHA requires. The rules are the same. The ability to follow them just got a lot stronger.

This matters for workers because it means understanding AI safety tools is now part of understanding your rights. If your factory uses AI to monitor safety — and many do — you should know what it's watching, why, and how it connects to the legal standards that protect you.

What is the main purpose of OSHA?

Scenario 1 of 3
You're working at a stamping press. You notice that the machine guard — the metal cover that keeps your hands away from the press — has been removed. No one told you about it. The machine is running and you're being asked to start your shift.
What should you do?
✅ Correct. Under OSHA, you have the legal right to refuse work you reasonably believe poses a serious danger. Machine guards are specifically required by OSHA Standard 1910.212. Refusing to work until this is addressed is exactly right — and you cannot legally be fired for it.
❌ Not the right call. OSHA explicitly requires machine guards on stamping presses. You have the legal right — and responsibility — to refuse dangerous work. Operating an unguarded press is one of the most common serious injuries in manufacturing.

Which of the following is an example of AI helping a factory follow OSHA rules?
✅ Right. AI tools like computer vision can watch for PPE compliance 24/7 — far more consistently than a human safety walk. This helps companies follow the OSHA standards that require PPE in hazardous areas. AI supports compliance; it doesn't replace the rules.
❌ Incorrect. OSHA is a government agency — AI doesn't write its rules or replace its inspectors. AI is a tool that helps factories follow the existing rules more effectively. It works inside the OSHA system, not instead of it.
