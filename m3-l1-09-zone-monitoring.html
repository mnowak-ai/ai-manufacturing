Zone Monitoring, Proximity Alerts, and Restricted Areas

The slides list "unsafe machine proximity" as a detection type — but what does that actually mean? This article explains how AI tracks movement across a factory floor, why certain zones are restricted, and how digital boundaries enforce safety rules without physically blocking access.

A Factory Floor Is a Map of Risk Levels

Not every part of a factory is equally dangerous. The area near a running conveyor belt is more hazardous than the break room. The space directly beside a chemical storage tank requires different PPE than the loading dock. A manufacturing facility is essentially a map of different risk levels — and managing safety means having different rules for different areas.

This is what zone monitoring is built around. Rather than applying the same alert rules to every square foot of a facility, AI safety systems divide the floor into zones — each with its own specific hazard profile, PPE requirements, and access rules. When the AI detects someone in a zone, it applies the rules for that zone specifically.

Key Term
Zone Monitoring: A safety approach that divides a facility into defined areas with different risk levels and rules. AI monitoring systems apply zone-specific detection logic — checking for the PPE required in that specific zone, flagging unauthorized access to restricted zones, and tracking pedestrian-vehicle proximity in high-traffic corridors.

The Four Zone Types

Most manufacturing facilities organize their zones into a hierarchy of access levels. Here's what each level means:

Level 1

✅ Open Zones

Accessible to all workers with standard facility PPE (e.g., closed-toe shoes). Break rooms, offices, reception areas. AI monitoring is minimal — mainly checking that no one who needs specific PPE is operating without it.

Level 2

⚠️ Controlled Zones

Require specific PPE for entry. Assembly lines, warehouses, loading docks. AI monitors for PPE compliance and may track pedestrian-vehicle proximity in forklift corridors. All authorized workers may enter with correct equipment.

Level 3

🚫 Restricted Zones

Limited to specific authorized personnel with appropriate training and PPE. Chemical handling areas, server/electrical rooms, specialized process areas. AI alerts any time an unauthorized person or someone without appropriate PPE enters.

Level 4

🔴 Exclusion Zones

No humans permitted while machinery is actively operating. The area directly within a robot's operating radius, inside an active press, around certain high-voltage equipment. AI sends an immediate, high-priority alert the moment any person enters — and may trigger an automatic machine stop.

Proximity Alerts: The Forklift Problem

One of the most valuable — and technically complex — applications of zone monitoring is pedestrian-vehicle proximity detection. Forklifts kill approximately 85 workers and seriously injure nearly 35,000 more every year in the US. Most of these incidents happen not because of reckless driving, but because of blind spots, high-traffic corridors, and the basic physics of a heavy vehicle that can't stop instantly.

Proximity alerts use AI to monitor the relationship between pedestrians and vehicles, not just their individual locations. Here's how different proximity alert types work:

📏 Distance-Based Alerts

The AI calculates the distance between a tracked pedestrian and a moving vehicle. When the distance drops below a set threshold (for example, 10 feet), an alert fires — giving the operator a warning before the worker is in the immediate danger zone, not after. The threshold can be adjusted based on the vehicle's speed and the facility's aisle width.

📐 Trajectory Prediction

More advanced systems analyze the direction and speed of both the vehicle and the pedestrian to predict where they'll both be in 2–5 seconds. If the predicted paths intersect — even if they aren't currently close — the system can alert in advance. This is the difference between detecting danger and predicting it before it develops.

🚦 Intersection Monitoring

High-traffic intersections (where pedestrian walkways cross forklift corridors) get specialized monitoring rules. The AI watches for vehicles approaching from one direction while pedestrians approach from another — flagging potential blind-spot collisions before either party can see the other. Some systems also control digital warning lights at these intersections based on live traffic detection.

🔴 Zone Boundary Alerts

Some facilities designate pedestrian-only and vehicle-only corridors with painted floor markings. AI monitoring can detect when a vehicle crosses into a pedestrian corridor or when a worker steps into a vehicle corridor — flagging the boundary violation regardless of whether any other person or vehicle is nearby at that moment.

The most dangerous moment in a forklift corridor is when neither the driver nor the pedestrian knows the other is there. AI proximity monitoring's most important function is catching that moment before it becomes a collision.

How AI Enforces Boundaries Without Blocking Access

A key point: AI zone monitoring doesn't physically prevent anyone from going anywhere. There are no locked doors or automated barriers controlled by the AI safety system (though some facilities add those as a separate system). The AI's enforcement mechanism is information — fast, reliable, automatic information to the right people at the right moment.

🔔

Instant Alerts to Supervisors

Unauthorized zone entry triggers an immediate alert on the supervisor dashboard, tablet, or radio — they can respond before the worker gets far into the restricted area

🔊

Zone Audio Warnings

Some facilities integrate zone monitoring with speaker systems — an automatic voice warning plays in the zone when unauthorized entry is detected, alerting the worker directly

🚦

Warning Lights

Proximity alerts near vehicle corridors can trigger flashing warning lights or digital signage, alerting both the worker and the vehicle operator that a conflict is approaching

⛔

Machine Interlock

In exclusion zones, AI detection can trigger automatic machine stops or slowdowns — preventing the machinery from operating while a person is in the danger zone

📋

Automatic Logging

Every zone entry, proximity event, and alert is timestamped and logged — creating an audit trail that can be reviewed for patterns, incident investigation, and OSHA compliance

📊

Pattern Reporting

Zone violation data aggregated over time shows which zones are being entered most frequently, at what times, and by whom — enabling targeted interventions before a serious incident

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Zone monitoring divides a factory floor into areas with different risk levels and rules. AI applies zone-specific detection logic — checking for the right PPE, flagging unauthorized access, and monitoring pedestrian-vehicle proximity based on the specific hazards in each area.

The four zone levels are: Open (all workers, standard PPE), Controlled (zone-specific PPE required), Restricted (limited authorized personnel), and Exclusion (no humans allowed while equipment is active). Each level has progressively stricter AI monitoring rules.

Proximity alerts monitor the relationship between pedestrians and vehicles — not just their individual locations. Advanced systems predict trajectory intersections before they happen, catching dangerous situations before either party can see each other.

AI enforces zone rules through information and alerts — instant supervisor notifications, audio warnings, machine interlocks — not by physically blocking access. Human response to those alerts is still what determines whether the safety system actually works.
