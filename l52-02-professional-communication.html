Quick Check

Knowledge Check
Which of the following maintenance requests is most likely to result in the problem getting fixed correctly on the first visit?
✅ Correct — Option B. It has all four qualities: Clear (exactly what sound, where, when), Accurate (specific details the maintenance tech can use to diagnose before arriving), Timely (includes onset time and current status), and Respectful (professional language, gives maintenance the information they need to do their job well). A technician responding to Option B arrives knowing what to look for. A technician responding to Options A, C, or D arrives and has to start from scratch.
❌ Option B is the strongest. The others are vague in different ways — A is minimal, C implies no urgency when there may be one, and D admits to having no useful information. Option B gives the maintenance technician everything they need: what the problem is, where, when it started, under what conditions, whether quality is affected, and what action is requested. That's professional communication.

What You Learned in This Article

✓
Clear means specific and unambiguous — not general statements like "it's not working right" but specific descriptions of what, where, and when. Precision is a professional skill.
✓
Accurate means you only say what you know — not what you guess or assume. When you're not sure, you say so. This protects the person acting on your information and protects your professional credibility.
✓
Timely means sending information when it can still be acted on — not hours later when the window to respond has closed. Timely communication also means not waiting to see if something works itself out when others need to know now.
✓
Respectful means professional language appropriate to the workplace and audience — not casual texting language in a formal maintenance request, and not cold or accusatory language when reporting a problem that involves someone else's work.
✓
Every type of manufacturing communication — shift handoffs, maintenance requests, safety flags, supervisor updates — follows the same four-quality framework. The format changes; the standards don't.
