What Is Anomaly Detection? How AI Spots Problems Humans Can't See

The video shows AI "highlighting unusual behavior" — but how does it actually know what's unusual? That's the question this article answers. Everything comes down to one deceptively simple idea: baseline.

The Neighbor Who Only Visits Once a Month

Imagine you live next to a neighbor and see them every day. One morning you notice they're limping slightly. Because you know their normal walk, you immediately notice something is off. You might even ask if they're okay.

Now imagine a different neighbor — one you've only seen once before. You wouldn't know if they always limp or if something happened. You have no baseline for comparison.

This is exactly how anomaly detection works in manufacturing. An anomaly is only detectable if you know what "normal" looks like first. Without a baseline of normal behavior, you have no way to identify what's abnormal. AI can watch thousands of machines 24 hours a day — but it can only raise an alert when it has learned what healthy looks like for each specific machine.

📖 Key Terms
Anomaly — A reading or pattern that significantly differs from what's expected based on historical data. Anomaly detection — The process of automatically identifying when machine behavior deviates from its established baseline. Baseline — The data profile of a machine operating normally, used as the comparison point for everything that comes after.

How AI Builds a Baseline — Step by Step

Before AI can detect anomalies, it has to learn what normal looks like for a specific machine. This isn't a one-time measurement — it's a learning process that happens over weeks of data collection. Click each step to understand what happens.

🔄 How AI Learns "Normal"
Building a Baseline for Machine 12
1
Sensors collect data continuously for 2–4 weeks
The system records temperature, vibration, pressure, and current readings from Machine 12 every few seconds, around the clock. This generates millions of data points. The machine runs through its full production cycle — light loads, heavy loads, startup, shutdown, different shift conditions — so the AI sees the full range of normal operation.
2
AI finds the patterns in that data
The AI analyzes all those readings to find consistent patterns. Temperature typically runs 62–78°C during production. Vibration hovers between 0.9–1.8 mm/s. Pressure stays between 182–218 PSI. But also: temperature is always 5°C higher at the start of each shift and drops to baseline after 20 minutes of warmup. The AI learns ALL of this as "normal."
3
The AI creates a dynamic baseline profile
Rather than a single number, the baseline is a range — the "normal zone" for each reading under each condition. Startup, mid-shift, heavy cut, light cut: each has its own expected range. This is important because a temperature of 80°C during a heavy cut is normal, but the same 80°C during an idle period is abnormal. Context matters.
4
Every new reading is compared against the baseline
From this point on, every incoming sensor reading is measured against the baseline profile. If a reading falls within the normal zone for the current operating condition — no alert. If it deviates significantly — especially if multiple sensors deviate together — the AI flags it as a potential anomaly and generates an alert for the maintenance team.
5
The baseline updates continuously as the machine ages
Machines change over time. As parts wear, as environments shift seasonally, as production loads change, the baseline naturally shifts too. Good AI systems update their baseline gradually to account for legitimate long-term changes — while still flagging concerning short-term deviations. This is what makes AI different from a simple threshold alarm that never adapts.

The Four Types of Anomalies AI Watches For

Not all anomalies look the same. AI systems are trained to recognize different anomaly patterns — because each pattern points to a different kind of problem. Understanding these four types helps you understand what AI alerts are actually telling you.

⚡
Spike Anomaly
A single reading shoots way above or below normal, then returns. Could indicate a real problem or a sensor glitch. AI looks for confirmed spikes (multiple consecutive readings) before alerting.
Example: Temperature jumps from 72°C to 94°C in one reading, then returns. AI waits for confirmation before alerting — one reading could be a bad data point.
📈
Drift Anomaly
Readings slowly and steadily move away from baseline over days or weeks. The hardest to spot visually, but the most valuable to catch early. This is classic wear-and-tear failure developing.
Example: Temperature rises 1–2°C per day for 14 days. On any given day it looks nearly normal. But the trend is unmistakable in the data — and AI catches it.
🔄
Pattern Change
The numbers stay within range, but the pattern of how they move changes. For example, temperature used to drop smoothly after shutdown — now it drops erratically. The range is normal; the behavior isn't.
Example: Vibration at 1.4 mm/s is normal. But vibration oscillating between 0.9 and 2.1 mm/s every 30 seconds is a new pattern — possibly indicating a loose component.
🔗
Multi-Sensor Correlation
No single reading is dramatically out of range — but two or three sensors are mildly deviating in a combination that AI recognizes as a failure signature. This is AI's most powerful capability.
Example: Temperature up 8°C, pressure down 15 PSI, current up 0.8A. Each alone might be ignored. Together, they match the "coolant restriction" profile at 78% confidence.

The multi-sensor correlation is what separates AI from a simple alarm. An alarm goes off when one number crosses a line. AI recognizes when three numbers that are each slightly off, together, tell a specific story.

What Happens After an Anomaly Is Detected

Detecting an anomaly is just the beginning. The AI doesn't fix the problem — it hands off to a human. Here's what that handoff looks like in practice:

The AI generates an alert that includes more than just "something is wrong." A good AI alert tells the maintenance team: which machine, which sensor or sensors triggered the alert, what the current readings are versus baseline, what the trend looks like over the past 24–72 hours, and often a list of probable causes ranked by likelihood.

The maintenance technician receives this alert — on a dashboard, tablet, or their phone. They review the data, use their experience to evaluate whether the alert makes sense, physically inspect the machine if needed, and decide what action to take.

The AI spots the pattern. The human makes the call. This division of responsibility is exactly what good human-AI collaboration looks like on the factory floor.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Anomaly detection works by comparing new sensor readings against a learned baseline of normal operation. Without a reliable baseline, there's nothing to compare against — which is why new AI systems need 2–4 weeks to learn a machine before their alerts become trustworthy.

Good baselines are dynamic, not static. A machine at startup, under heavy load, during summer, and at end-of-shift all have different normal ranges. AI systems learn all of these contexts and account for them — which is why they generate fewer false alarms than simple threshold alarms.

There are four anomaly types: spikes (sudden deviations), drift (slow trend away from baseline), pattern changes (behavior changes without leaving the range), and multi-sensor correlations (multiple sensors mildly deviating together). The last type is AI's most powerful capability — recognizing failure signatures from combined signals no single reading would reveal.

AI detects the anomaly; humans evaluate and act. The AI generates an alert with context (which sensors, what trend, probable causes). The maintenance technician reviews, inspects, and decides. This is what effective human-AI collaboration on the factory floor looks like.
