What Are Sensors and Why Is Every Machine Covered in Them?

Before AI can catch a problem, something has to collect the data. That something is sensors. They're everywhere on a modern factory floor — and understanding what they do is the foundation of everything else in this module.

Your Phone Has Sensors Too

Before we talk about factory machines, let's start somewhere you already know. Your smartphone is packed with sensors. There's one that detects if the phone is face-up or face-down. There's one that measures how bright the room is and adjusts your screen. There's one that counts how many steps you've walked. There's one that feels when you lift the phone to your ear and turns off the touchscreen so your face doesn't accidentally press buttons.

None of those things required you to do anything. The phone just knows — because sensors are constantly measuring the physical world around the device and turning those measurements into data the phone can use.

Factory sensors work the exact same way. A sensor is a device that measures something physical — heat, movement, pressure, position — and converts that measurement into a number a computer can read. Put enough sensors on a machine and you can know, at any moment, exactly what that machine is doing and how it's doing it.

📖 Key Term
Sensor — A device that detects a physical condition (like temperature, vibration, or pressure) and converts it into a digital signal that computers and AI systems can read. Sensors are the "eyes and ears" of an AI monitoring system — without them, there's no data to analyze.

Sensor Type 1
Temperature Sensor
Measures how hot or cold a component is, continuously, in real time.
What it measures

The exact temperature of machine parts — bearings, motors, gearboxes, hydraulic fluid, coolant systems. Reads in degrees Celsius or Fahrenheit, updating every few seconds.

Where it's placed

Mounted directly on or near the component being monitored — often glued, bolted, or clamped onto bearing housings, motor casings, or coolant lines.

What data it produces

A continuous stream of temperature readings: "Motor housing: 72°C... 73°C... 75°C..." Any sustained rise gets flagged by the AI as a developing problem.

Why it matters

Heat is the most common early warning sign of mechanical failure. A bearing that's starting to wear runs hotter than normal — often days or weeks before it breaks down completely.

Real example: A pump's bearing housing normally runs at 65°C. The temperature sensor shows it slowly climbing over two weeks: 66°C, 68°C, 71°C, 74°C. The AI flags this trend and alerts the maintenance team. They inspect and find the bearing is starved of lubrication. They top it off and schedule a bearing replacement — preventing a $40,000 failure.
📳
Sensor Type 2
Vibration Sensor (Accelerometer)
Detects tiny shaking movements in machine components — even ones invisible to the human hand.
What it measures

The frequency and intensity of vibrations inside the machine. Measured in millimeters per second (mm/s) or g-force units. Even vibrations far too small to feel are captured.

Where it's placed

Bolted or magnetically attached to rotating components — motors, fans, pumps, gearboxes, shafts. Wherever something spins, a vibration sensor can watch it.

What data it produces

A vibration "signature" — the specific pattern of frequencies a healthy machine produces. When a part starts to wear, the signature changes in characteristic ways the AI has learned to recognize.

Why it matters

Bearing wear, shaft imbalance, gear tooth damage, and misalignment all create characteristic vibration changes before any visible signs appear. Vibration analysis is one of the most powerful early-warning tools in predictive maintenance.

Real example: A CNC machine's spindle motor normally vibrates at a steady, smooth pattern. The vibration sensor starts picking up a new, higher-frequency signal at a specific rotation speed — a pattern the AI recognizes as early-stage bearing spalling (tiny cracks forming inside the bearing). The maintenance team schedules a replacement 8 days out. The bearing, when removed, shows exactly the spalling the AI predicted.
💨
Sensor Type 3
Pressure Sensor
Monitors the pressure of fluids and gases flowing through the machine's systems.
What it measures

The pressure of hydraulic fluid, compressed air, coolant, oil, or gas inside pipes and cylinders. Measured in PSI (pounds per square inch) or bar units.

Where it's placed

Screwed into hydraulic lines, pneumatic (air) systems, coolant pipes, lubrication lines, and any other system where fluid or gas pressure matters.

What data it produces

A continuous pressure reading. The AI monitors for pressure that's too high (blockage), too low (leak or pump failure), or fluctuating (intermittent valve or seal problem).

Why it matters

Many machines run on hydraulics (fluid pressure) or pneumatics (air pressure). Wrong pressure means wrong force, wrong speed, wrong output — and often damage to seals, valves, and cylinders if left uncorrected.

Real example: In the Module 2.1 video scenario, the machine shows Error Code E-301 with Pressure at 150 PSI (flagged as LOW). The normal operating range is 180–220 PSI. The pressure sensor caught this — and combined with the high temperature, the AI flagged it as a potential cooling system failure. A technician discovers a partially clogged coolant line.
📏
Sensor Type 4
Proximity Sensor
Detects whether something is present, absent, or positioned correctly — without physical contact.
What it measures

Distance or presence. Is a part in the correct position? Did the machine complete its cycle? Is a component moving at the right speed? Answers yes/no questions about machine position and motion.

Where it's placed

Near moving parts, at end-of-travel positions on robotic arms, at product detection points on conveyor belts, and anywhere a position check is needed.

What data it produces

Binary signals (on/off) or distance measurements. The AI tracks cycle counts, timing, and sequence — so if a machine starts taking longer than normal to complete each cycle, it gets noticed.

Why it matters

Slowing cycle times often indicate mechanical resistance, lubrication problems, or wear before any other sensor catches it. Proximity sensors also verify that safety systems (guards, stops) are functioning correctly.

Real example: A stamping press normally completes one cycle every 2.1 seconds. The proximity sensor at the bottom of the stroke starts recording 2.3, then 2.4, then 2.6 seconds per cycle. The AI detects the slowdown trend and flags it. Technicians discover the ram guide is running dry — 10 minutes of lubrication prevents what would have become a seized guide and a 6-hour repair.
⚡
Sensor Type 5
Current / Power Sensor
Monitors how much electrical power a motor is drawing — a surprisingly revealing health indicator.
What it measures

The amount of electrical current (amperes) flowing to a motor. A healthy motor doing its normal job draws a predictable amount of current. Changes in that draw signal problems.

Where it's placed

In the electrical panel that feeds each motor — doesn't require touching the motor itself. This makes power sensors easy to add to existing machines.

What data it produces

Continuous current draw readings. A motor drawing more power than normal is working harder — likely because of increased mechanical resistance (worn bearing, clogged filter, binding component).

Why it matters

Motors tell you more about the health of the whole machine than almost any other indicator. If something is making the motor work harder, the current draw goes up — even before temperatures or vibration readings change significantly.

Real example: A conveyor belt motor normally draws 12 amps at full load. Over two weeks, current readings creep from 12.1 to 12.8 to 13.4 amps. The AI flags the upward trend. Technicians inspect the belt tensioner — it's been gradually over-tightening due to a stuck adjustment bolt. A 20-minute fix prevents a burned-out motor worth $3,200.

From Physical World to AI — How Sensor Data Travels

Once a sensor collects a measurement, that number doesn't just sit there. It travels through a chain of systems before reaching the AI that analyzes it. Understanding this chain helps you understand why good sensor placement and maintenance matters so much.

1
The Sensor Measures
The physical sensor detects the current condition — temperature, vibration, pressure — and converts it into an electrical signal. This happens continuously, often multiple times per second.
2
The Signal Gets Converted to a Number
A small device called an analog-to-digital converter (ADC) turns the electrical signal into a number a computer can read. For example: an electrical signal of 0.47 volts becomes "71.3°C" in the system.
3
The Data Travels to a Controller
The numbers go to a local controller — either the machine's own computer or a separate data collection device called a PLC (Programmable Logic Controller). The controller logs readings and checks for obvious out-of-range values.
4
The Data Reaches the AI Platform
Readings from across the factory are sent — either over a wired network or wirelessly — to the AI analysis platform. This might be running on computers in the factory or on cloud servers the factory connects to over the internet.
5
AI Analyzes and Alerts
The AI compares incoming readings against the machine's baseline, looks for patterns that match known failure signatures, and generates alerts when something warrants attention. A maintenance technician gets a notification on their computer, tablet, or phone.

Sensors are the reason AI can watch 200 machines at once. Without sensors collecting data, AI has nothing to analyze. The sensor is where the machine starts talking.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

A sensor is a device that measures a physical condition and converts it into a number a computer can read. Sensors are the "eyes and ears" of any AI monitoring system — without them, there's no data to analyze.

The five main sensor types in manufacturing are temperature (detects heat buildup), vibration (detects mechanical wear in rotating parts), pressure (monitors fluid and air systems), proximity (verifies position and cycle timing), and power (tracks electrical current draw as an indirect health indicator).

Sensor placement matters. Sensors are positioned close to the components they monitor — especially rotating parts — because vibration and temperature signals are strongest and most diagnostic near their source.

Sensor data travels from the physical world through a controller and network to the AI analysis platform, where patterns are compared against the machine's baseline. This whole pipeline has to work correctly for the AI to give reliable alerts.
