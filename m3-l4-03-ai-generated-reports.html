AI-Generated Reports: How to Read, Verify, and Use Them

The slides say "AI drafts, humans confirm accuracy" — but how exactly does a person confirm accuracy? This article teaches the practical skill: what to look for in an AI-generated report, what AI commonly gets wrong, and when to escalate instead of approve.

Why Human Review Is Not Optional

AI systems that generate manufacturing reports are powerful — they process massive amounts of data, draft summaries automatically, and can flag issues faster than any human team. But they make mistakes. Specific kinds of mistakes, that a trained human reader can catch.

The problem with automation bias — the tendency to over-trust automated outputs — is that it's strongest when the AI is usually right. If the AI is 98% accurate, humans stop checking carefully. The 2% of errors that do occur then slip through undetected, sometimes into legally binding documents that form the basis of regulatory submissions, batch releases, or product dispositions.

Your job when reviewing an AI-generated report is not to re-do the AI's work from scratch. It's to know what to look for — the specific categories of error that AI systems in manufacturing documentation commonly make.

Approving an AI-generated report without reading it is not efficiency — it's signing a document you haven't checked. In manufacturing, that signature carries legal and regulatory weight.

Read a Real AI-Generated Report — With Annotations

Below is an AI-generated shift summary report with annotations highlighting what a human reviewer should notice. Work through each section and see what the flags mean.

SHIFT SUMMARY REPORT — AUTO-GENERATED · LINE 4 · 14:00–22:00 Pending Human Review
Executive Summary
Line 4 completed second shift production of Batch BTH-2024-1143 (Metformin 500mg). Total output: 142,650 units. Overall pass rate: 99.92%. Three inspection categories performed within specification. One temperature excursion event was documented and resolved within 49 minutes. Production resumed at 11:01. Batch is recommended for QA review and release consideration.
✅Check 1 — Summary accuracy: The key numbers here (142,650 units, 99.92%, 49-minute downtime) match the underlying inspection log data. This is the first thing to verify — do the headline numbers in the summary actually match the detailed records?
Defect Analysis
112 units flagged by AI inspection system. Flagged defect types: surface chips (47%), weight deviations (31%), missing imprint (14%), color variation (8%). All flagged units removed from batch. Defect rate consistent with historical average of 0.07–0.09% for this product line.
⚠️Check 2 — Missing step: The report states all flagged units were "removed from batch" — but this skips a required step. Some flags (confidence <85%) require human verification before disposition. Were the 4 "REVIEW REQ'D" items from the inspection log individually verified? The AI summary collapsed this into "all removed" which may be inaccurate or missing the verification documentation.
Temperature Excursion Event
At 10:12, a temperature excursion was detected (43.2°C; limit 40.0°C). The excursion lasted approximately 30 minutes. Root cause: sensor malfunction. Corrective action: sensor replacement. Production resumed at 10:42.
🚨Check 3 — Factual error: The audit trail shows production resumed at 11:01 — not 10:42 as stated here. This is a 19-minute discrepancy in a documented excursion event. In a regulatory submission, this would be an inaccurate record. The AI likely miscalculated the downtime duration. This must be corrected before the report is approved.
Equipment Status
All production equipment operating within validated parameters. Coating pan temperature sensor replaced and recalibrated per SOP-MAINT-078. Calibration certificate recorded in maintenance management system.
✅Check 4 — Cross-reference: Verify that SOP-MAINT-078 is actually the correct procedure for sensor replacement on this equipment model, and confirm the calibration certificate number appears in the maintenance management system. The AI may have referenced the right SOP name but the wrong revision.
Recommendation
Based on the above, Batch BTH-2024-1143 meets release criteria. QA Director approval recommended.
⚠️Check 5 — Context the AI cannot see: The AI cannot know whether the temperature excursion may have affected product quality beyond the documented time window, whether similar excursions have occurred on recent batches (a pattern the QA Director should know about), or whether there are open NCRs from this batch that haven't been closed. The "release recommended" conclusion should only stand after those contextual checks are done by a human.

Four Things AI Gets Wrong in Manufacturing Reports

🔢

Number Errors — Calculation Mistakes in Summaries

AI systems can make arithmetic errors when aggregating data, especially when handling exceptions — units reworked and re-inspected, units held pending review, batch splits. The summary number might not equal the sum of the detailed rows.

AI states: "Total rejected units: 108"
Actual count from inspection log rows: 112 rejected + 4 under review = 116 total flagged
→ Discrepancy of 8 units unaccounted for

How to catch it: Manually add up the detailed rows and compare to the summary totals. Don't assume the AI's arithmetic is correct.

🕳️

Missing Steps — Events That Happened but Weren't Summarized

AI summarizes what it can see in structured data. Actions that were logged informally — a verbal instruction, a procedure step completed without a formal system entry, a supervisor override — may be absent from the AI-generated summary even though they occurred.

Audit trail entry at 09:02: "Compression speed changed 35→40 RPM. Supervisor authorized."
AI shift summary: No mention of compression speed change or supervisor authorization.
→ A process deviation that required supervisor authorization was omitted from the summary

How to catch it: Compare the AI summary to the raw audit trail entries. Look for events logged in the audit trail that don't appear in the summary narrative.

🧩

Context Errors — The AI Doesn't Know What It Doesn't Know

AI generates summaries based on the data in its system. It has no knowledge of: a pending regulatory inquiry about this product, a supplier quality alert for the raw material lot used, a pattern of similar excursions across other recent batches, or a customer complaint that makes this batch especially sensitive. Human reviewers bring context that no AI summary can contain.

AI summary: "Temperature excursion resolved, batch meets release criteria."
Human reviewer knows: This is the third temperature excursion on this line in 30 days. The QA director has asked to be personally notified about any further excursions before release.
→ The AI's "release recommended" ignores a context that completely changes the appropriate response

How to catch it: Before approving, ask yourself: "Is there anything I know about this product, batch, line, or supplier that the AI doesn't have access to that changes this picture?"

⏱️

Timestamp Errors — Time Calculations That Don't Match

AI systems can miscalculate durations, especially when events span shift changes, midnight crossings, daylight saving time transitions, or when there are gaps in the production record. Reported downtime durations, holding times, and process step durations may be wrong.

AI summary: "Excursion downtime: approximately 30 minutes (10:12 to 10:42)"
Audit trail: Line resumed at 11:01, not 10:42. Actual downtime: 49 minutes.
→ 19-minute discrepancy. In a pharmaceutical context, accurate downtime documentation affects whether the product meets holding time specifications.

How to catch it: For any timed event (downtime, holding times, process step durations), verify against the raw timestamps in the audit trail rather than trusting the AI's calculated duration.

When to Approve vs. When to Escalate

🚨 Escalate — Don't Approve Alone

→A factual error in the AI summary (wrong number, wrong timestamp)
→A process deviation that required supervisor authorization but isn't documented in the summary
→An open NCR that the AI summary doesn't acknowledge
→A pattern you recognize that the AI can't see (third excursion this month)
→Anything the AI flagged as requiring human review that you can't verify
→Summary recommends release but your SOP requires QA Director sign-off for any excursion

✅ Approve — After Thorough Review

→All summary numbers cross-check against the detailed records
→All audit trail events appear in the summary narrative
→All timestamps and durations verified against raw data
→No open items requiring escalation or additional verification
→Your contextual knowledge is consistent with the AI's recommendation
→All required sign-off steps are completed per SOP
🧠 Check Your Understanding
An AI-generated batch record summary states: "Batch yield: 98.3% — within acceptable range (specification: ≥97.0%). No yield deviations to report." A reviewer calculates the yield from the raw production data: 139,850 units produced from 142,200 units started = 98.4%. The AI's stated yield is 98.3%. What should the reviewer do?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Automation bias — the tendency to over-trust automated outputs — is the primary risk when reviewing AI-generated reports. Your job is to know what AI commonly gets wrong and check specifically for those things, not to redo all the AI's work from scratch.

Four common AI documentation errors: number errors (arithmetic mistakes in summaries), missing steps (events logged in the audit trail that don't appear in the summary), context errors (the AI can't know what you know from outside the system), and timestamp errors (duration calculations that don't match raw data).

When you find an error — even a small one — escalate before approving. Approving an AI-generated report with a known inaccuracy means signing off on an incorrect legal document. Your signature carries regulatory and legal weight regardless of whether the AI generated the underlying content.
