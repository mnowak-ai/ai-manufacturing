How Could AI Change Your Job in 5 Years? A Structured Reflection Activity

This is the expanded version of the reflection activity from the lesson video. Work through six guided prompts about your specific career path, then see a complete example of what a finished reflection looks like.

The lesson slide says: "How could AI change your job in 5 years? Think about: Tasks, Skills, Opportunities." That's the right question — but three bullet points isn't enough scaffolding to actually do the thinking well.

This article is the full version. Six structured prompts that walk you through the reflection properly. By the end, you'll have a personal picture of where AI is headed in your career path and what you might want to do about it. This isn't a prediction exercise — it's a planning exercise.

First: See a Complete Example

Before doing your own reflection, read through a completed one. This will show you the kind of thinking the activity is designed to produce.

Completed Example
Destiny — Quality Inspector, 2 Years Experience
Target role: Quality Control Analyst. 5-year career goal: Quality Engineer.
Step 1: My current core tasks
Physical inspection of parts using calipers and micrometers. Reviewing AI vision system flags — deciding which are real defects vs. false positives. Completing inspection records and documentation. First-article inspection on new product runs. Reporting quality metrics to supervisor.
Step 2: Which tasks could AI be doing more of in 5 years?
AI vision systems will almost certainly handle more of the initial screening — cameras are getting better and faster at catching surface defects. Routine documentation might be partially automated. AI will probably get better at identifying defect patterns across production runs faster than I can manually.
Step 3: Which tasks will stay human?
Final judgment on whether a flagged part is actually defective — the AI is still wrong enough that a human has to verify. Root cause investigation — figuring out WHY a defect pattern is happening requires floor knowledge AI doesn't have. Customer-facing communication about quality issues. Any quality decision with regulatory or legal implications. Training new inspectors.
Step 4: Which skills will be more valuable?
Understanding how AI quality systems make decisions and where they fail — so I can evaluate their outputs intelligently. Data analysis skills — reading defect trend data and communicating what it means. Root cause analysis — the more AI handles routine screening, the more valuable the skill of finding WHY problems happen becomes.
Step 5: What opportunities could open up?
If AI handles more routine inspection, quality inspectors who develop data analysis and engineering skills will have a clear path to quality analyst and quality engineer roles. There may be new positions specifically around AI quality system management — configuring, validating, and improving the AI systems themselves.
Step 6: What do I want to learn next?
SPC (Statistical Process Control) — I use control charts but don't fully understand the math behind them. I want to fix that this quarter. The ASQ Quality Inspector certification — studying for it will fill in the gaps in my formal quality knowledge. Python basics or Excel data analysis — not to become a data scientist, but to be able to pull and visualize quality data myself without asking someone else every time.

What You Learned in This Activity

The 5-year reflection framework has six steps: current tasks → tasks AI will likely do more → tasks that stay human → skills that will become more valuable → opportunities that could open up → what to learn next.
