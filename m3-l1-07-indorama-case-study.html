What Is PET?
PET (Polyethylene Terephthalate) is a type of plastic. It's the clear plastic in most water and soda bottles (look for the recycling #1 symbol). In fiber form, it becomes polyester clothing. Manufacturing PET involves chemical processes, high heat, and pressurized systems — all of which create significant workplace hazards.

The Real Hazards on an Indorama Factory Floor

Chemical manufacturing plants have a unique combination of hazards that make PPE compliance especially critical. Understanding what workers actually face helps explain why the AI monitoring program was so impactful.

🌡️

Extreme Heat and Hot Surfaces

PET production involves melting plastic at temperatures above 250°C (480°F). Workers near extrusion and molding equipment are at risk of serious burns from steam, hot surfaces, and splashes of molten material.

☠️

Chemical Exposure

The PET production process uses ethylene glycol and terephthalic acid — chemicals that can cause skin and respiratory irritation. Workers in chemical handling zones require appropriate gloves and respirators at all times.

⚙️

Heavy Machinery and Moving Parts

Large extruders, conveyors, and cutting machinery create entanglement and crush hazards. Machine guarding and restricted zone protocols exist around all active equipment.

🏗️

Large-Scale Logistics

Moving bulk materials, large resin pellet containers, and finished product pallets involves forklifts and overhead cranes — creating vehicle-pedestrian collision risk across the facility floor.

In this environment, PPE isn't optional safety theater — it's the line between a shift that ends normally and one that ends with a serious injury. And across a large facility with dozens of zones, monitoring PPE compliance manually was simply not possible at the scale needed.

The Problem Before AI: Manual Monitoring at Scale

Before the AI system was deployed, Indorama's safety team faced a challenge that's common to large manufacturing operations: the facility was simply too big for humans to watch effectively.

Safety officers would conduct periodic walkthroughs — checking zones for PPE compliance, hazard signs, and equipment condition. But between walkthroughs, large sections of the floor were effectively unmonitored. A worker who removed their hard hat to wipe sweat might put it back on before a supervisor arrived. Or they might not. No one would know either way.

The result was a gap between the safety rules that existed on paper and the actual behavior happening on the floor — particularly during busy periods when production pressure was high and safety shortcuts felt harmless.

The problem wasn't that workers were reckless. It was that large, complex facilities have too many people in too many places for any human team to monitor continuously.

How the AI System Was Implemented

Indorama worked with an AI safety monitoring vendor to deploy a computer vision system across their facilities. Here's how the implementation unfolded:

1

Camera Infrastructure Setup

High-resolution cameras were installed throughout production zones, covering areas with the highest historical incident rates and PPE requirements. Camera placement was designed to minimize blind spots around the most hazardous equipment.

2

Defining Detection Rules

Safety managers worked with the AI team to define exactly what violations should be flagged: missing hard hats, missing hi-vis vests, missing chemical gloves in chemical handling areas, missing eye protection near certain equipment. Each zone had its own rule set based on the specific hazards present.

3

Training and Calibration

The AI model was trained on images from Indorama's actual facilities — workers in their specific PPE, in their specific lighting conditions, at the specific camera angles installed. This facility-specific training is important: generic safety AI trained on other factories often performs poorly in a new environment until it's calibrated to local conditions.

4

Alert Routing and Response Protocols

The team built alert routing so that violations in each zone went to the right supervisor or safety officer — someone physically close enough to respond quickly. Response time targets were set: supervisors were expected to review alerts within 60 seconds and initiate a response within 3 minutes.

5

Rollout and Worker Communication

Workers were informed about the new system before it went live — explaining what it monitors, how alerts work, and emphasizing that the goal was safety, not surveillance or discipline. This communication step is often skipped but is critical for worker trust and cooperation.

6

Ongoing Review and Improvement

After deployment, the safety team reviewed alert data weekly — looking at false alarm rates, response times, and violation trends by zone. This data drove ongoing calibration: adjusting thresholds, adding training data, and improving camera angles where needed.

It's also worth understanding how an 83% reduction happens. It's not just that the AI caught violations faster. The presence of consistent monitoring created a behavioral change: when workers knew that the cameras were always watching and that violations would be caught quickly, the rate of violations themselves dropped. The AI's deterrent effect was as significant as its detection capability.

This is called a compliance culture shift — when the knowledge of consistent monitoring changes the day-to-day behavior of people on the floor, not just the response to violations after they happen.

What Workers Actually Experienced

Safety monitoring AI often gets described purely from the management perspective. But the workers on the floor are the ones whose daily routine is most directly affected. Here's an honest picture of what that looked like:

👷 The Worker's Perspective
😟

Initial Concern: "Are We Being Watched?"

Many workers' first reaction to camera-based monitoring is discomfort — a feeling of being surveilled. This is a real and legitimate concern. Indorama's implementation included explicit worker communication explaining that the system was tracking PPE compliance, not individual productivity, bathroom breaks, or conversations. Transparency about what the system monitors — and what it doesn't — was key to acceptance.

📱

The Experience of Receiving an Alert

When a worker was flagged, the process wasn't a public shaming — a supervisor would approach them directly and privately remind them about their PPE. In most cases, workers had simply forgotten (a hard hat left at a workstation, glasses pushed up on a forehead). The alert gave supervisors a way to address the issue immediately and specifically, rather than waiting for a scheduled safety meeting.

✅

What Changed Over Time

Workers reported that knowing the cameras were active created a consistent mental reminder to check their PPE before entering monitored zones. The AI essentially built a habit — not through punishment, but through the same consistent reinforcement that makes any safety habit stick. The repeated small reminders changed default behavior over time.

What This Case Study Teaches Us About AI Safety Monitoring

Indorama's implementation is a useful real-world example because it illustrates several things that the theory alone doesn't capture:

Scale matters. AI monitoring has the most transformative impact in large facilities where human coverage gaps are significant. The bigger the floor, the more a continuous monitoring system changes what's possible.

Implementation quality determines outcomes. The 83% result wasn't automatic — it required thoughtful camera placement, facility-specific AI training, clear alert protocols, and ongoing calibration. A poorly implemented AI system in the same facility might have produced much weaker results or even made things worse through alert fatigue.

Worker communication is not optional. The behavioral change that created the 83% improvement required workers to understand and accept the system. Surveillance that workers feel is punitive or opaque tends to breed resentment, workarounds, and reduced cooperation with safety programs overall.

The AI is a tool — people create the culture. The AI flagged violations. Supervisors responded. Workers changed their behavior. Safety officers analyzed the data and improved the system. The 83% came from all of those humans doing their jobs effectively, aided by better information.
