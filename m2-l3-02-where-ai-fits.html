Where AI Fits (and Where It Doesn't): A Map of AI's Role Across the Production Process

The Lesson 2.3 slide asks: where does AI fit in a manufacturing workflow? This article answers that in detail — stage by stage, showing exactly what AI handles, what humans must own, and where the boundaries are.

The Five-Stage Production Workflow

Every manufacturing workflow — whether it's producing a product or maintaining a machine — moves through five stages: Data Collection, Analysis, Decision-Making, Execution, and Feedback. AI has a different role at each stage. Click through each stage to see exactly what AI does and what humans must own.

📡
Stage 1
Data Collection

This is where raw information about the factory enters the system — sensor readings, machine status, production counts, quality measurements. It's continuous, happening thousands of times per second across hundreds of sensors. AI excels here. Humans set the system up — AI runs it.

🤖 AI Does
Continuously reads thousands of sensor data points per second
Timestamps and stores all readings in the historian database
Flags data that falls outside expected parameters for review
Monitors data quality and detects sensor errors automatically
👤 Humans Own
Selecting which data points to monitor and why
Installing and maintaining the physical sensors
Calibrating sensors on their maintenance schedule
Deciding what "normal range" means for each measurement
Real example: A vibration sensor on a gearbox reports 1.8 mm/s every 100 milliseconds — 10 readings per second. AI captures all of them, timestamps them, compares them to the baseline, and stores them in the historian. The human set the sensor position and baseline threshold during installation.
🧠
Stage 2
Analysis

Raw data becomes meaningful information at this stage. AI's ability to find patterns across massive data sets — comparing current readings to years of historical data, correlating multiple sensors simultaneously — vastly exceeds what any human can do manually. This is where AI provides the most value.

🤖 AI Does
Detects anomalies by comparing current readings to historical baseline
Correlates readings across multiple sensors simultaneously
Identifies trends developing over days or weeks
Matches current patterns to known failure signatures in training data
Generates confidence-scored diagnosis suggestions
👤 Humans Own
Reviewing and validating AI alerts before acting on them
Adding physical context the sensors can't capture
Overriding AI suggestions when physical inspection disagrees
Updating training data when new failure modes are discovered
Real example: AI detects that vibration has been slowly increasing over 3 weeks and correlates it with a simultaneous temperature rise. It matches this combined pattern to 14 historical cases in its training data — 11 of which were bearing wear. It generates an alert: "Bearing wear — 78% confidence." A technician reviews the alert, physically inspects the bearing, and confirms the diagnosis.
⚡
Stage 3
Decision-Making

This is the most human-critical stage. AI can generate recommendations — but the decision to act, and what action to take, belongs to a person. This is where the validation framework from Lesson 2.2 applies. AI recommends. Humans decide.

🤖 AI Does
Generates recommendations ranked by probability
Provides the data evidence supporting each recommendation
Suggests urgency level based on trend rate
Can automatically draft a work order for human approval
👤 Humans Own
Deciding whether to act on the recommendation
Choosing which recommended action to take
Applying physical context that changes the recommended action
Approving or modifying the auto-drafted work order
Full accountability for the outcome of the decision
Real example: AI recommends: "Schedule bearing replacement within 5 days — 78% confidence." The maintenance planner reviews the alert, checks that the required bearing is in stock, verifies the technician's schedule, and approves a work order for Thursday. The planner also checks with the operator who confirms an unusual noise started two days ago — which increases confidence. Human decision confirmed: repair Thursday.
🔧
Stage 4
Execution

Physical work happens here. AI supports execution in important ways — providing documentation, step-by-step guidance, and parts information — but the actual physical work is entirely human. No current AI can reach into a gearbox.

🤖 AI Does
Provides the work order with detailed task instructions
Shows relevant technical documentation and schematics
Confirms parts availability and location in inventory
Monitors machine sensors during repair for safety
👤 Humans Own
Every physical action — removing, replacing, adjusting components
Safety lockout/tagout procedures
Judgment calls when physical findings differ from expectations
Documenting actual findings vs. expected findings
Real example: Technician follows the work order on a mobile device — shows the bearing specification, torque settings, and step-by-step replacement procedure. Upon opening the gearbox, technician discovers not just the failed bearing but also scoring on the gear surface — unexpected damage. Technician documents the additional finding and escalates for engineer review before closing out. This physical discovery was impossible for AI to know in advance.
🔄
Stage 5
Feedback & Learning

After the work is done, the system learns. This is where AI improves over time — by comparing what it predicted to what actually happened, and updating its models accordingly. But this learning depends entirely on humans documenting their findings accurately.

🤖 AI Does
Monitors the repaired machine's return-to-normal sensor behavior
Compares its prediction (bearing wear) to the actual finding (bearing wear + gear scoring)
Updates pattern models based on confirmed vs. incorrect diagnoses
Adds the completed work order data to the training dataset
👤 Humans Own
Entering accurate root cause and findings in the work order closeout
Noting when AI suggestions were wrong — and why
Reviewing AI prediction accuracy trends over time
Deciding when retraining or recalibration is needed
Real example: After closing the work order, the technician documents: "Found bearing wear as predicted (78% confidence — correct) plus unexpected gear scoring — possible result of delayed bearing replacement. Gear surface damage documented with photos." AI system logs: prediction correct on primary cause, missed secondary damage. Over time, the AI learns that delayed bearing replacement often leads to gear surface damage — and begins including this in its recommendations.

The Clear Boundaries

⚠️ Where AI Cannot Go
Physical Action, Final Decisions, and Accountability
No matter how advanced AI systems become, three things remain entirely human: (1) Physical execution — touching, moving, replacing components. (2) Final decisions — the human reviews, approves, or overrides before action is taken. (3) Accountability — when something goes wrong, a person is responsible, not an algorithm. These boundaries aren't limitations of today's AI — they're intentional design principles that keep humans in meaningful control of manufacturing operations.

AI is strongest at the stages that involve data — collection, analysis, and learning. Humans are irreplaceable at the stages that involve judgment, physical presence, and accountability.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

The five workflow stages — Data Collection, Analysis, Decision, Execution, and Feedback — each have a different AI/human split. AI excels at data-intensive stages (collection and analysis). Humans are essential at judgment-intensive stages (decision and execution).

Analysis is AI's greatest advantage: it monitors thousands of sensors simultaneously, 24/7, detecting patterns over days and weeks that no human team could track. This is why predictive maintenance AI catches failures earlier than traditional methods.

Three things are always human: physical execution, final decisions, and accountability. These aren't current limitations of AI — they're intentional design principles that keep people in meaningful control of manufacturing operations.

The Feedback stage is where AI improves over time — but only if humans document their findings accurately. The quality of human documentation at work order closeout directly determines how much smarter the AI gets from each repair cycle.
